Phase transition

Explore competition between unitary evolution and measurements as vary measurement frequency, \(p\)

  • \(p =0\); No measurement, Volume law entanglement
  • \(p =1\); Measure every Qubit, no entanglement (area law)
  • Transition at \(p=p_{c}\) ??

  1. a volume law entangled phase for low measurement rates, which exhibits a sub-dominant logarithmic behavior in the entanglement entropy, \(S_{A}=\) \(\alpha \ln |A|+s|A|,\) with sub-system size \(|A| .\)
  2. With increasing measurement rate the volume law phase is unstable to a disentangled area law phase \(S_{A}=c(p)|A|^{0}\)

RG equation

From the renormalization operation above, our scale has \(s\) changed \(s^{\prime}\), the corresponding black grid probability \(p\) has also changed \(p\left(s^{\prime}\right)\). \[ p\left(s^{\prime}\right)=f(p(s)) \] the function \(f\) depend on the coarse-grained rules: how we ignore information.

the left side 2 by 2 size is \(s_{n}\). Then the right side is \(s_{n+1}\). The \(p\left(s_{n+1}\right)\) and \(p\left(s_{n}\right)\) relationships can be obtained by:： \[ p\left(s_{n+1}\right)=p\left(s_{n}\right)^{4}+4 p\left(s_{n}\right)^{3}\left(1-p\left(s_{n}\right)\right)+2 p\left(s_{n}\right)^{2}\left(1-p\left(s_{n}\right)\right)^{2} \] where

  • The fourth power term \(p\left(s_{n}\right)^{4}\) corresponds to the last rule in the rule (that is, in the original scale, the black grid should appear 4 times in a row, and its probability is obviously \(p\left(s_{n}\right)^{4}\) )
  • the third power term is the rule with three black grids on the left
    • In this case, there are 4 rules in total so the coefficient is \(4,\)
    • the probability is that the black grid appears 3 times in a row
    • and the last time is the white grid, so yes \(p\left(s_{n}\right)^{3}\left(1-p\left(s_{n}\right)\right)\);
  • the second power corresponds to two two blacks connected together vertically. The 2 rules of, the coefficient is 2

RG flow and fix point

The figure shows \(p\left(s_{0}\right)=0.5,0.6,0.7\) the evolution trajectory of the renormalization equation at three different initial points.

  • for \(p\left(s_{0}\right)=0.5\) the situation, the curve gradually decreases. If the original square is infinite and the renormalization operation continues, then the curve will gradually approach \(0 .\)
  • For \(p\left(s_{0}\right)=0.7,\) the curve will gradually approach 1 .
  • For \(p\left(s_{0}\right)=0.6\), Although it decreases slowly, it will gradually approach \(0 .\)

In fact, the asymptotic behavior of these curves is completely determined by the fixed point of the renormalization group. The so-called fixed point of an equation refers to a special \(p^{*}\) satisfaction: \[ p\left(s^{*}\right)=p\left(s^{*}\right)^{4}+4 p\left(s^{*}\right)^{3}\left(1-p\left(s^{*}\right)\right)+2 p\left(s^{*}\right)^{2}\left(1-p\left(s^{*}\right)\right)^{2} \] In the limit \(n \rightarrow \infty\) get the above equation, then solve this equation, we get \(p\left(s^{*}\right) 4\) different solutions: \[ \left\{0,1, \frac{-1-\sqrt{5}}{2}, \frac{-1+\sqrt{5})}{2}\right\} \] the third solution is negative, so it is omitted, so we get three meaningful solutions: \[ \{0,1,0.618\} \] These solutions are called fixed points of the renormalization equation. \[ \left\{0,1, \frac{-1-\sqrt{5}}{2}, \frac{-1+\sqrt{5})}{2}\right\} \]

  • For the cases of 0 and \(1,\) we call it the trivial fixed point (Trivial fixed point), which respectively correspond to the density of the final square after the renormalization operation is 0 (no square) and 1 (all squares) )
  • For the 0.618 it is a non-trivial fixed point (non-trivial fixed point), which happens to be the case of the fractal we are looking for.

Another scaling behavior is related to fractals and is also related to the renormalization method we will introduce.

How do we know this? In fact, we can use the Box Covering method to calculate the fractal dimension of this red clump.

Box Covering method

  1. use boxes of different resolution sizes to cover the geometry, then in the case of a given resolution \(s\), we can determine the size of the box to cover the geometry used The quantity is \(l(s)\) used as the approximate area of the geometry in the s scale;
  2. then I use a smaller (finer resolution) box \(s^{\prime}\) to cover these squares to obtain a new approximate area \(l\left(s^{\prime}\right)\).
  3. Such repeated the process of the transformation of different $ s$ can be obtained a \(l(s)\) relationship between.

We know that for a normal two-dimensional geometry (such as a circle), the curve we get by this method is a power-law \[ l(s) \sim s^{-D} \] where \(D=2 .\)

But for a fractal geometry, although we can also get a power-law relation, it is D generally less than 2 , for this example the fractal dimension is D=1.95, so it is a fractal.

3Hybrid Circuits Meet Percolation

The zeroth Rényi entropy of entanglement between a subsystem and its exterior, \(S_{0},\) counts the number \(N\) of nonzero eigenvalues of the reduced density matrix: \[ S_{0}=\log _{2} N \]

  • we do not usually think of it as a physically significant quantity. Still, in an idealized system it can be a useful conceptual starting point for thinking about the von Neumann and higherorder entanglement entropies.
  • The tool for calculating \(S_{0}\) is the 'minimal cut', a geometrical upper bound on entanglement in a tensor network which is a useful starting point for thinking about the scaling of entanglement in various situations ranging from holography to unitary dynamics.

minimal cut

  1. In the absence of projective measurements

\(S_{0}(A)\) is given exactly by the number of links in the circuit that must be cut in order to separate the physical 'legs' (external bonds at the top boundary) for the spins in \(A\) from those for the spins outside \(A\).

FIG. Any cut through the unitary circuit which separates the legs to the left and right of \(x\) (on the top boundary) gives an upper bound on \(S(x, t)\). The best such bound is given by the minimal cut (note that the cut shown in the figure is not the minimal one). Finding the minimal cut in a random network is akin to finding the lowest energy state of a polymer in a random potential landscape. (arXiv:1608.06950)

  1. In the presence of measurements

There is a simple generalization of this picture

FIG.4 Mapping between circuit dynamics with measurement (Left) and bond percolation on the square lattice (Right). For the purposes of the minimal cut picture, a projective measurement breaks a bond. The minimal cut may pass through broken bonds at zero cost. For the figure on the right, which is topologically equivalent, we represent each unitary as the vertex of a square lattice. Broken bonds are dashed (unoccupied ) and unbroken bonds are solid (occupied). This is bond percolation on the square lattice, with a probability \(1-p\) for a bond to be occupied. The minimal cut lives on the dual square lattice. (arXiv:1808.05953)

Problems of finding a minimal-cost path in a disordered medium are well-studied in the mathematical literature under the name 'first passage percolation'

Mapping

a rotated square lattice with width \(L\) and depth \(t,\) of which each bond has a cost of either zero (with probability \(p\) ) or unity (with probability \(1-p\) ).

  • \(S_{0}\) is defined as the total cost of this minimal-cost pathway, which corresponds to the number of unbroken bonds that must be cut in the circuit in order to separate the subsystem from the rest of the network.

FIG.5 Example comparison between the unitary circuit and classical percolation results for the zeroth Rényi entropy, \(S_{0}\), between two halves of a spin chain that has undergone unitary evolution. In this example, the circuit contains \(L=24\) spins and the evolution time is \(t=48 .\) The value of \(p\) on the horizontal axis represents the probability of measurement for each spin after each time step. The magenta circles with error bars show the result of the full simulation of the unitary circuit, while the black line shows the result of the classical percolation simulation. The inset shows the same data on a logarithmic scale. (arXiv:1808.05953)

Given the percolation picture, one can understand the dynamics of the Hartley entropy \(S_{0}\) as a function of time \(t\) using the following scaling arguments.

1 small

At small \(p,\) broken bonds exist only in small, isolated clusters. The minimal cut is therefore forced to pass through a number of unbroken bonds that is proportional to \(t\) with an \(O (1)\) coefficient. This situation is shown in Fig. \(6 .\)

FIG. 6 . Cartoon of the minimal cut in the entangling phase for \(S_{0}\) (at \(L=\infty\) ). The small white domains are clusters of broken bonds: we only show those clusters which the minimal cut passes through. A minimal cut is shown traversing the spacetime patch.

  • The red sections have nonzero cost per unit length while the green regions have zero cost.
  • In the entangling phase the cost of the minimal cut scales like \(t\) (with subleading KPZ fluctuations), so that \(S_{0}(t) \sim t\).

In this phase randomness in the location of measurements has a subleading effect on the entanglement, giving subleading Kardar-Parisi-Zhang fluctuations, as for purely unitary dynamics with randomness.

2 large

At \(p\) close to unity, on the other hand, broken bonds predominate over unbroken bonds, and there is a connected cluster of unbroken bonds that spans the entire system. Thus at \(p>p_{c}\) the only contribution to the entanglement comes from passing through unbroken bonds located near the starting point of the cut. (For the bipartite entanglement, this starting point is at the top center of the network.) Once this small set of unbroken bonds has been traversed, the minimal cut can proceed arbitrarily far in the time direction using only broken bonds, and consequently the average \(S_{0}\) becomes independent of \(t\) at large \(t .\) This is illustrated in Fig. \(7 .\)

FIG. 7. Cartoon of the minimal cut in the disentangling phase for \(S_{0}\) (cf. Fig. 6 ). The clusters of broken bonds now percolate, forming the infinite white region. The cost of the minimal cut remains finite as \(t \rightarrow \infty\) : only a finite number of unbroken bonds need to be traversed to reach the infinite white cluster. Therefore \(S_{0}(t) \sim t^{0}\).

3 critical

At the top of the network, where the cut is anchored, the minimal path typically passes into an empty chamber of size \(O (1)\). It then passes though a sequence of larger chambers until it reaches one of scale \(\sim t\) and exits the system. Typically, at each step, the path can find a chamber that is larger by an \(O (1)\) factor than the previous one. This progression is illustrated schematically in Fig. \(8 .\)

FIG.8 Cartoon for the scaling argument showing \(S_{0}(t) \sim \log t\) at the percolation critical point (cf. Figs. \(\left.6,7\right)\) The minimal cut passes through the sequence of white domains shown in blue/white. Writing the linear sizes of consecutive domains in this sequence as \(R_{1}, R_{2}, \ldots,\) the ratio \(R_{i+1} / R_{i}\) is typically larger than one (see text), so for an \(i\) of order \(\log t\) the minimal cut reaches a cluster of \(O (t)\) size that borders the boundary and the sequence ends. Domains \(i\) and \(i+1\) typically approach each other to within one lattice spacing, so the cost scales as the number of domains in the sequence.

Critical behavior

the total number of chambers in the sequence is only of order \(\ln t,\) and \[ S_{0}\left(t, p_{c}\right) \simeq A \ln t \] This logarithmic scaling for critical first passage is proved in Ref. \(45 .\) 【Critical behavior of the two-dimensional first passage time (1986)】

  • The coefficient \(A,\) which can be thought of as an entanglement per scale, is universal as a result of the scale-invariance of the process. Below, we estimate \(A=0.27(1)\)

typical size

The typical size \(\xi\) of the empty chambers is finite for \(p \lesssim p_{c},\) but diverges with the correlation length exponent as \(p_{c}\) is approached: \[ \xi \sim 1 /\left|p-p_{c}\right|^{\nu} \]

  • For classical percolation in two dimensions, the critical exponent \(\nu=4 / 3 .\)
  • The part of the minimal cut within \(\xi\) of the top of the sample costs \(A \ln \xi,\) as above.

At farther distances from the starting point the path travels through chambers of size \(\sim \xi .\) Each chamber-to-chamber crossing involves passing through an order-unity number of unbroken bonds, and consequently the minimal cut passes through a total number \(S_{0} \sim t / \xi\) of unbroken bonds, so that:

\[ \begin{aligned} S_{0} &\sim \frac{t}{\xi} \\ &\sim \frac{t}{1 /\left|p-p_{c}\right|^{\nu}}\\ &\sim\left(p_{c}-p\right)^{\nu} \times t \end{aligned} \]

\[ S_{0}(t, p) \sim\left(p_{c}-p\right)^{\nu} \times t, \quad\left(p \lesssim p_{c}\right) \]

That is, the entanglement \(S_{0}\)

  • at \(p<p_{c}\) grows without bound as a function of time, with a growth rate that vanishes as \(p \rightarrow p_{c}\).

  • At \(p \geq p_{c}\) the minimal cut escapes to the infinite cluster of unbroken bonds after a section of length \(O (\xi)\) at the top of the lattice.

On scales smaller than \(\xi\) the critical scaling applies, giving a cost \(A \ln \xi,\) so that \[ S_{0}(t, p) \simeq A \nu \ln \left(\frac{1}{p-p_{c}}\right) \quad\left(p \gtrsim p_{c}\right) \] The above results are all limiting cases of the general scaling form \[ S_{0}(t, p)=A \ln \xi+F(t / \xi) \] where \(F\) is the scaling function

scaling

The scaling expectations can be tested using numerical simulations.

4.CFT

Future Work

1 Conformal invariance of Percolation

Percolation models on two‐dimensional lattices are conjectured to have conformal invariance, in the sense that, if the lattice is rescaled so the spacing tends to zero, then certain limiting probabilities of crossings of regions are invariant under conformal mappings of the plane when the model is at criticality. Conformal invariance has been proved for the triangular site percolation mode

Reference: Smirnov, S. (2001). Critical percolation in the plane: Conformal invariance, Cardy's formula, and scaling limits. C. R. Acad. Sci. Paris, \(333,239-244\)

2 area and volume law phase transition

Notably, Ref. constructed a replica description of a 'holographic' random tensor network state (projected entangled pair state) and used it to argue that there is a phase transition between area law and volume law phases of the tensor network state. While the transition itself is hard to address as a result of the replica limit, the assumption of a single transition described by a conformal field theory leads to logarithmic scaling of the entanglement with subsystem size, and scaling forms analogous to Eq. (12). The replica description also gives a formal explanation for why all the Rényi entropies become simultaneously critical in the tensor network: they correspond to distinct correlation functions in the same conformal field theory [25]

Ref: Romain Vasseur, Andrew C Potter, Yi-Zhuang You, and Andreas WW Ludwig. Entanglement transitions from holographic random tensor networks. arXiv preprint arXiv:1807.07082, 2018 .

def RankOrderPlot(data):
    d=array(data)
    d = d[d>0]
    t=array(sorted(d,key=lambda x:-x))
    r=array(range(1,len(d)+1))   
    y = log(t)
    x = log(r)
    X = sm.add_constant(x, prepend=True)
    res = sm.OLS(y,X).fit()
    C,beta = res.params
    plt.plot(r,t,"o",color="b")
    plt.plot(r,exp(C)*r**beta,"r-")
    plt.xscale('log')
    plt.yscale('log')
    plt.text(max(r)/2,max(t)/100,"beta = " + str(round(beta,2)))
    print beta
