1994年综述 Conformal invariance in two-dimensional percolation

综述

摘要

Percolation models on two‐dimensional lattices are conjectured to have conformal invariance, in the sense that, if the lattice is rescaled so the spacing tends to zero, then certain limiting probabilities of crossings of regions are invariant under conformal mappings of the plane when the model is at criticality. Conformal invariance has been proved for the triangular site percolation model .

Stochastic Loewner Evolution is a stochastic process of random planar curves generated by a differential equation with Brownian motion as input. It is conjectured to be the scaling limit of various critical percolation models and other stochastic processes in the plane, such as uniform spanning trees and planar loop‐erased random walk. A parameter \(\kappa\) determines the distribution of the process, with \(\kappa=6\) believed to correspond to the scaling limit of the boundary of percolation clusters at criticality in two‐dimensional models. This connection would yield proofs of the conjectured critical exponent values for two‐dimensional models, and has been proven to be correct for site percolation on the triangular lattice.

Basic elements of conformal field theory

recall some basic results of conformal field theory in two dimensions.

关联函数，以及关联函数在临界点的行为

Let \(L\) be a regular lattice with lattice spacing \(a\) and the Hamiltonian of a statistical mechanics model formulated \[ H(\{s(x)\},\{J\}) \] where

  • variables \(s(x)\)
  • coupling set \(\{J\}\).

The two-point correlation function is the average \[ \langle s(x) s(0)\rangle=\frac{1}{Z} \sum_{\{s(x)\}} e ^{-H} s(x) s(0) \] The behavior of two-point correlation function Near the critical point \[ \langle s(x) s(0)\rangle=\xi^{-2 X_{s}} F(|x| / \xi) \] where

  • the correlation length \(\xi\), the typical length scale of the fluctuations
  • \(X_{s}\) the scaling dimension of \(s(x)\)

形状

  1. 发散 $ $ diverges at criticality and the region of validity of \(\langle s(x) s(0)\rangle=\xi^{-2 X_{s}} F(|x| / \xi)\) is the scaling region.
  2. 与构型的细节无关 In the scaling region the functional form \(\langle s(x) s(0)\rangle=\xi^{-2 X_{s}} F(|x| / \xi)\) is not affected by the specific details of the lattice \(L\) and we can identify renormalized local densities (scaling fields),

for example \(\phi(x) \sim a^{-X_{s}} s(x),\) whose conjugated couplings transform multiplicatively under the linearized renormalization group transformation.

连续极限：连续极限下的动力学密度可以用二五欧几里得QFT描述

In the continuum limit \(a \rightarrow 0\) the dynamics of the densities is described by an Euclidean two-dimensional quantum field theory \[ \langle\phi(x) \phi(0)\rangle_{S}=\frac{1}{Z} \int D \phi e ^{-S[\phi]} \phi(x) \phi(0) \] with

  • action \(S\) specified by the requirement that for \(|x| \gg a\)

\[ \langle s(x) s(0)\rangle=K\langle\phi(x) \phi(0)\rangle_{S} \] where

  • \(K\) is an a dependent normalization constant.

The mass of the quantum field theory \(m\) is the inverse of \(\xi\). At the fixed point of the renormalization group transformation \(\xi=\infty\) and the densities \(\phi_{i}(x)\) with scaling dimension \(X_{\phi_{i}}\) obey global scale invariance under \(x \rightarrow b x\) \[ \phi_{i}(b x)=b^{-X_{\phi_{i}}} \phi(x) \] The corresponding quantum field theory has action \(S^{*}\) and it is massless, with two-point function \[ \left\langle\phi_{i}(x) \phi_{i}(0)\right\rangle_{S^{*}}=\frac{1}{|x|^{2 X_{\phi_{i}}}} \] which is a power-law.

共形不变形的要求

  1. we assumed rotational and translational invariance;
  2. if we further require the invariance of \(S^{*}\) under local scale transformations \(x \rightarrow b(x) x\)

the full group of symmetry is the conformal group and \(S^{*}\) is conformal invariant.

保角映射

two dimensions Conformal transformations of coordinates maintain local angles between vectors in the plane

For convenience we introduce complex coordinates

  • \(z=x_{1}+i x_{2}\)
  • \(\bar{z}=x_{1}-i x_{2}\)

the Euclidean metric \[ g=\frac{1}{2}[d z \otimes d \bar{z}+d \bar{z} \otimes d z] \] is off-diagonal in the coordinates \((z, \bar{z})\) and therefore the tangent vectors \(\partial \equiv \frac{d}{d z}\) and \(\bar{\partial} \equiv \frac{d}{d \bar{z}}\) have zero norm.

The transformation of coordinates \(w(z, \bar{z})\) preserves the angles if \[ \begin{array}{l} \left\langle\partial_{w}, \partial_{w}\right\rangle=\left\langle\bar{\partial}_{w}, \bar{\partial}_{w}\right\rangle=0 \\ \left\langle\partial_{w}, \bar{\partial}_{w}\right\rangle=\frac{1}{2} \Omega(w, \bar{w}) \end{array} \] where

  • \(\partial_{w} \equiv \frac{d}{d w}, \bar{\partial}_{w} \equiv \frac{d}{d \bar{w}}\)
  • the inner product \(\langle a, b\rangle\) between two vectors \(a, b\) is \(g(a, b)\).

It is simple to prove that \(\left\langle\partial_{w}, \partial_{w}\right\rangle=\left\langle\bar{\partial}_{w}, \bar{\partial}_{w}\right\rangle=0\) and \(\left\langle\partial_{w}, \bar{\partial}_{w}\right\rangle=\frac{1}{2} \Omega(w, \bar{w})\) are satisfied by the isogonal mappings \(w(z, \bar{z})=w(z)\) and \(w(z, \bar{z})=w(\bar{z})\) giving \(\Omega(w, \bar{w})=\left|\frac{d z}{d w}\right|^{2}\).

However only analytic functions preserve the local angle orientation and define a conformal mapping.

The scaling fields for which the requirement of conformal covariance analogous to \(\left\langle\partial_{w}, \bar{\partial}_{w}\right\rangle=\frac{1}{2} \Omega(w, \bar{w})\) naturally translates into \[ \phi_{i}(w, \bar{w})=\left|\frac{d z}{d w}\right|^{X_{\phi_{i}}} \phi_{i}(z, \bar{z}) \] are the primary fields of the conformal field theory.

Conformal transformations are analytic functions

  1. it is useful to regard \(z\) and \(\bar{z}\) as independent variables
  2. defining two different scaling dimensions \(h_{\phi_{i}}, \bar{h}_{\phi_{i}}\)

such that \(X_{\phi_{i}}=h_{\phi_{i}}+\bar{h}_{\phi_{i}} .\) Under \(z \rightarrow w(z), \bar{z} \rightarrow \bar{w}(\bar{z})\) a primary field transforms as \[ \phi_{i}(w, \bar{w})=\left(\frac{d z}{d w}\right)^{h_{\phi_{i}}}\left(\frac{d \bar{z}}{d \bar{w}}\right)^{\bar{h}_{\phi_{i}}} \phi_{i}(z, \bar{z}) \] where

  • The real numbers \(h_{\phi_{i}}\) and \(\bar{h}_{\phi_{i}}\) are equal for a scalar field and are called conformal dimensions.

An infinitesimal conformal transformation \[ w(z)=z+\varepsilon(z) \] is generated by the stress energy tensor \(T_{\mu \nu}(x),\) which is symmetric, traceless and conserved. In complex coordinates \(T_{\mu \nu}\) has only two independent components \[ T_{z z}=T_{11}-2 i T_{12}-T_{22} \equiv T(z) \\ T_{\bar{z} \bar{z}}=T_{11}+2 i T_{12}-T_{22} \equiv \bar{T}(\bar{z}) \] which are purely analytic and antianalytic as a consequence of the conservation law \(\partial^{\mu} T_{\mu \nu}=0\)

To quantize a conformal field theory

  1. we introduce light-cone coordinates \(w_{\pm}=\) \(x \pm t\)
  2. and think the Minkowsky space-time as a cylinder of circumference $ R$.
  3. The asymptotic Hilbert spaces \(H_{i n}\) and \(H_{\text {out }}\) are defined on the cylinder at time \(t=\pm \infty\).

Operators of the quantum theory \(\Phi(x, t)\) are of the factorized form \(\Phi_{+}\left(w_{+}\right) \Phi_{-}\left(w_{-}\right)\) \[ \left\langle\Phi_{1}\left(x_{1}, t_{1}\right) \ldots \Phi_{n}\left(x_{n}, t_{n}\right)\right\rangle \equiv\left\langle T \left\{\Phi_{1}\left(x_{1}, t_{1}\right) \ldots \Phi_{n}\left(x_{n}, t_{n}\right)\right\}\right\rangle \] and inside correlation functions time-ordering \(T\) is understood \[ T \left\{\Phi_{1}\left(x_{1}, t_{1}\right) \ldots \Phi_{n}\left(x_{n}, t_{n}\right)\right\}=\sum_{\sigma \in S_{n}} \prod_{i=1}^{n-1} \theta\left(t_{\sigma(i)}-t_{\sigma(i+1)}\right) \Phi_{\sigma(1)} \ldots \Phi_{\sigma(n)} \] After the analytic continuation \(t=i y\) to Euclidean space let \(w=x+i y\). The quantization prescription (radial quantization) on the complex plane of \(z\) is obtained exploiting conformal covariance through the mapping \(z= e ^{-\frac{2 i \pi}{R} w}\).

对易子

For example, suppose to compute on the \(z\) plane the equal times commutator between two operators \(A(\zeta), B(z) .\) Time ordering on the cylinder translates into radial ordering on the plane and we find \[ [A(\zeta), B(z)]=\lim _{|\zeta| \rightarrow|z|} \theta(|\zeta|-|z|) A(\zeta) B(z)-\theta(|z|-|\zeta|) B(z) A(\zeta) \] If the operator \(A\) is expressed as a contour integral of some operator density \(Q(w)\) at constant time \(|w|=|\zeta|\) \[ A=\oint_{|w|=|\zeta|} \frac{d w}{2 \pi i} Q(w) \] the commutator reads \[ [A, B(z)]=\oint_{C_{z}} \frac{d w}{2 \pi i} Q(w) B(z) \] where

  • \(C_{z}\) is a contour encircling the point \(z\).

central charge of CFT

To evaluate \([A(\zeta), B(z)]\) it is necessary to know the singularities arising from the short distance expansion \(\lim _{w \rightarrow z} Q(w) B(z)\) which are ruled by the Operator Product Expansion (OPE). Let us consider the stress energy tensor \(T(z) .\) By dimensional analysis \[ \lim _{w \rightarrow z} T(w) T(z)=\frac{c / 2}{(w-z)^{4}}+\frac{2 T(z)}{(w-z)^{2}}+\frac{\partial T(z)}{z-w}+\text { regular terms } \] the real parameter \(c\) is the central charge of the conformal field theory.

Virasoro algebra

As operators A and \(B\) of the form \(A=\oint_{|w|=|\zeta|} \frac{d w}{2 \pi i} Q(w)\) we take the Laurent modes defined as \[ L_{n}=\frac{1}{2 \pi i} \oint_{C_{O}} d z z^{n+1} T(z) \] with

  • \(C_{O}\) a contour encircling the origin \(O\).

The combined use of

  1. \([A(\zeta), B(z)]=\lim _{|\zeta| \rightarrow|z|} \theta(|\zeta|-|z|) A(\zeta) B(z)-\theta(|z|-|\zeta|) B(z) A(\zeta)\)
  2. \(\lim _{w \rightarrow z} T(w) T(z)=\frac{c / 2}{(w-z)^{4}}+\frac{2 T(z)}{(w-z)^{2}}+\frac{\partial T(z)}{z-w}+\) regular terms

gives the commutation rule \[ \left[L_{n}, L_{m}\right]=(n-m) L_{n+m}+\frac{c}{12} \delta_{n+m, 0} n\left(n^{2}-1\right) \] which defines the Virasoro algebra.

representation of Virasoro algebra & Hilbert space

Consider now a primary field $ _{h}(z)$ with conformal dimension \(h\). Assuming the OPE \[ \lim _{w \rightarrow z} T(w) \phi_{h}(z)=\frac{h \phi_{h}(z)}{(w-z)^{2}}+\frac{\partial \phi_{h}(z)}{w-z}+\text { regular terms } \] from \([A(\zeta), B(z)]\) the commutation relations with the Laurent modes follow. \[ \left[L_{n}, \phi_{h}(z)\right]=h(n+1) z^{n} \phi_{h}(z)+z^{n+1} \partial \phi(z) \] The study of the representation theory of the Virasoro algebra \(\left[L_{n}, L_{m}\right]\) is equivalent to the characterization of the Hilbert space \(H_{i n}\) and we summarize the basic results .

The Hilbert space \(H_{i n}\) is the vector space of an infinite dimensional representation of the Virasoro algebra. Highest weights \(|h\rangle\) are eigenvector of \(L_{0}\) defined by the action of the primary \(\phi_{h}(0)\) on the conformal invariant vacuum \(|\Omega\rangle .\) Form \(\left[L_{n}, \phi_{h}(z)\right]\) \[ L_{0}|h\rangle=h|h\rangle \] The vacuum \(|\Omega\rangle\) is the only highest weight with conformal weight \(h=0\).

Descendant states of \(|h\rangle\) are obtained applying the modes \(L_{-n}, n>0\) to \(|h\rangle .\) The descendant state \(L_{-n_{1}} \ldots L_{-n_{k}}|h\rangle\) has \(L_{0}\) eigenvalue \(h+N\), \(N=\sum_{i=1}^{k} n_{i} . N\) is the level of the descendant and the number of descendant states of \(|h\rangle\) at level \(N\) is \(P(N)\), the number of partitions of \(N\) into positive, not necessarily distinct, integers. The set of states resulting from the application of the \(L_{-n}\) 's to the highest weight \(|h\rangle\) is the Verma module of \(|h\rangle .\) The Hilbert space \(H_{i n}\) is a direct sum of Verma modules.

矩阵表象，在不同参数下的类型

The \(P(N) \times P(N)\) matrix resulting from the scalar products of the descendant states of \(|h\rangle\) at level \(N\) is the Grahm matrix \(G(h, c, N)\). Virasoro algebra representations are unitary if all the elements of \(G(h, c, N)\) are non-negative and irreducible if they are non-vanishing. The classification of the Virasoro algebra representations is based on the exact formula, found by Kac, for the determinant of \(G(h, c, N)\). We summarize the conclusions.

  • For \(c<0\) there are no unitary representations.

  • For \(c>1\) all the representations are unitary and irreducible for every value of \(h\)

  • For \(c=1\) there are infinite values of \(h\) for which the representations are reducible but all of them are unitary.

  • For \(0<c<1\) unitary representations are obtained for \[ c=1-\frac{6}{t(t+1)}, \quad h_{m, n}=\frac{[(t+1) m-t n]^{2}-1}{4 t(t+1)}, \] where \(t, m\) and \(n\) are integers with \(t>2\) and \(m=1, \ldots, t-1\) \(n=1, \ldots, m\). The representations are reducible and irreducible representations are obtained subtracting all the vectors with vanishing norm (null vectors) from the Verma module. For a given \(t,\) the conformal field theory defined by (1.32) is called the minimal model \(M _{t}^{9} .\) In a minimal model the null norm condition for a vector generates a differential equation for the primary correlation functions, which can be solved exactly. The primary fields with conformal dimensions (1.32) are also the generators of the OPE algebra.

Potts model as example of CFT

The critical Potts model for arbitrary values of \(q\) is described by a conformal field theory \(^{10}\). The parameter \(t\) in \(c=1-\frac{6}{t(t+1)}\) relates to \(q\) as [37,38] \[ \sqrt{q}=2 \sin \frac{\pi(t-1)}{2(t+1)} \] 1. The theory is minimal only for integer \(q<4\). 2. The value \(q=4\) corresponds to \(c=1\) 3. and for \(q>4\) the Potts phase transition is first order \(\left(q_{c}(2)=4\right)\).

The maximum number of colors allowing the existence of a second order phase transition in two dimensions can be found by simple group theoretic considerations, as we will show in sections 2.3 and 2.7 . The conformal dimensions \(h_{\varepsilon}\) and \(h_{s}\) for the Potts thermal and spin fields can be conjectured for arbitrary $q $ \[ \begin{array}{l} h_{\varepsilon}=h_{2,1}=\frac{t+3}{4 t} \\ h_{s}=h_{\frac{t+1}{2}}\\ \frac{t+1}{2}=\frac{(t+3)(t-1)}{16 t(t+1)} \end{array} \] We observe from (1.32) that for \(q \neq 2,3\) the Verma module of the Potts spin field \(s\) does not contain null vectors and no differential equations are known to be satisfied by its correlation functions.

introduction to percolation

Consider the planar connected graph \(L\) defined in section 1.1. Bond percolation on \(L\) is the random process \({ }^{12}\) in which the edges of \(L\) can be occupied with probability \(p \in[0,1]\). The set of occupied edges defines a graph \(G \subseteq L ,\) see Fig. 1.5,

Figure 1.5: Typical configurations in a bond percolation problem on a square lattice. The bond occupation probabilities are 0.3,0.5 and 0.7 .

with probability measure \(\Lambda( G )\) \[ \Lambda( G )=p^{|E( G )|}(1-p)^{|E( L )|-|E( G )|} \] where

  • The connected components of \(G\) are the percolative clusters.
  • Two vertices \(x, y \in\) \(V\) are connected in the graph \(G\) if there exists a path of occupied edges starting from \(x\) and ending at \(y\).
  • Connected vertices belong to the same cluster.

The probability \(P_{a a}(x, y)\) that the vertices \(x\) and \(y\) belong to the same cluster $a $ \[ P_{a a}(x, y)=\sum_{ G \subseteq L } \Lambda( G ) \theta(x, y \mid G ) \] where

  • \(P_{a a}(x, y)\) is the two-point connectivity in percolation.
  • the function \(\theta(x, y \mid G )=1\) if \(x\) and \(y\) are connected and zero otherwise.

In general, if the letters \(a_{1}, \ldots, a_{n}\) denote particular clusters we can define the \(n\) -point connectivity \(P_{a_{1} \ldots a_{n}}\left(x_{1}, \ldots, x_{n}\right)\) as the probability that the vertex \(x_{i}\) belongs to the cluster \(a_{i} .\) The number of \(n\) -point connectivities is the number of partitions of a set of \(n\) elements, the Bell number \(B_{n}\). Connectivities in the random cluster model will be analyzed in the next chapter.

example: percolative phase transition

We introduce the percolative phase transition with an example. Take \(L\) a Cayley tree with

  • coordination number \(\sigma\)
  • \(N\) sites (vertices)

Fig. \(1.6,\) and consider in the limit \(N \rightarrow \infty\) the probability \(P(p)\) that the origin \(O\) is connected to a point on the boundary. We have \[ P=\sum_{k=1}^{\sigma}\left(\begin{array}{l} \sigma \\ k \end{array}\right) p^{k}(1-p)^{\sigma-k}\left(1-Q^{k}\right), \] with \(P+Q=1\). The probability \(P\) satisfies the algebraic equation \[ P=1-(1-p P)^{\sigma} \] which can be solved graphically.

  1. For \(p \leq p_{c} \equiv 1 / \sigma\) the only solution is \(P=0\)
  2. a unique non-vanishing solution is obtained when \(p>p_{c}\).
  3. The threshold value \(p_{c}\) separates the subcritical phase \(\left(p<p_{c}\right)\) from the supercritical phase \(\left(p>p_{c}\right)\)

Figure 1.6: Left. Percolation problem on a Cayley tree with coordination number \(\sigma=3\). A path of occupied edges connecting the origin \(O\) to the boundary of the tree is colored in red. Right. The function \(P(p)\) obtained solving (1.40) for \(\sigma=3\).

The critical exponent \(\beta\) is defined by \[ P(p) \sim B\left(p-p_{c}\right)^{\beta}, \quad p \rightarrow p_{c}^{+} \] and \(\beta=1\) in a Cayley tree, see Fig. 1.6.

In the supercritical phase the cluster containing the origin also contains an infinite number of sites in the thermodynamic limit \(N \rightarrow \infty\) and it is called the infinite cluster. \(P(p)\) can be also characterized as the average fraction of sites belonging to it. The average size of finite \(^{14}\) clusters containing the origin \(S=\sum_{x \in L } P_{a a}(x, 0)\) is another classical observable in percolation theory. \(S\) diverges at \(p_{c}\) and the critical exponent \(\gamma\) is defined as \[ S \sim \Gamma^{\pm}\left|p-p_{c}\right|^{-\gamma} \] The superscript \(\pm\) on the critical amplitude \(\Gamma^{\pm}\) refers to \(p_{c}\) being approached from below or above. In a Cayley tree \(\gamma=1\). The percolative transition on a Cayley tree is actually an artifact due to the effective infinite dimensionality of the graph. Connectivities are exponentially decaying with the distance also at \(p_{c}\)

hypercubic lattice

On the hypercubic lattice \(L = Z ^{d}(d \geq 2)\) with lattice spacing \(a\), the transition is also signaled by the divergence of the correlation length \(\xi\) \[ \xi \sim f^{\pm}\left|p-p_{c}\right|^{-\nu} \] defined from the behavior of the two-point connectivity in the scaling limit \(\xi \gg a\) near \(p_{c}\) \[ P_{a a}(x, y)=\xi^{2-d-\eta} F(|x-y| / \xi) \] A critical exponent \(\alpha\) is finally introduced for the singular part of the mean number of clusters \(\left\langle N_{c}\right\rangle\) with \[ \frac{\left\langle N_{c}\right\rangle}{N} \sim A^{\pm}\left|p-p_{c}\right|^{2-\alpha} \] \(N\) being the total number of sites of \(L\). Critical exponents \(\alpha, \beta, \gamma\) and \(\nu\) are expected to be universal as well as suitable critical amplitude ratios.

Markov property.

Divide the curve \(\gamma\) in two parts \(\gamma_{1}\) from \(x_{1}\) to \(x\) and \(\gamma_{2}\) from \(x\) to \(x_{2}\), then the conditional measure \[ \mu\left(\gamma_{2} \mid \gamma_{1} ; x_{1}, x_{2}, D\right)=\mu\left(\gamma_{2} ; x, x_{2}, D \backslash \gamma_{1}\right) \] Conformal Invariance.

Consider a conformal mapping \(\Phi\) which maps the interior of \(D\) into the interior of a new domain \(D^{\prime} .\) The points \(x_{1}\) and \(x_{2}\) on the boundary are mapped into the points \(x_{1}^{\prime}\) and \(x_{2}^{\prime}\) on the boundary of \(D^{\prime} .\) The map \(\Phi\) induces a new probability measure for the transformed curve \(\Phi(\gamma), \Phi * \mu\) and it is required \[ \Phi * \mu\left(\gamma ; x_{1}, x_{2}, D\right)=\mu\left(\Phi(\gamma), x_{1}^{\prime}, x_{2}^{\prime}, D^{\prime}\right) \] The formalism of SLE allows a rigorous derivation \(^{17}\) of the critical exponents for two-dimensional percolation that confirm the exact value obtained by physicists through arguments that we recall in the next section.

Percolation as limit of the Potts model

Apart from an inessential prefactor the probability measure for a graph \(G\) in the FK representation (1.5) coincides at \(q=1\) with (1.37) . This simple observation identifies the FK clusters in the random cluster model at \(q=1\) with the percolative clusters \(^{18}\). The geometric phase transition of percolation is mapped onto the formal ferromagnetic phase transition of the Potts model with discrete symmetry group \(S_{q}\) in the limit \(q \rightarrow 1\). To be more explicit, define at integer \(q\) the Potts order parameter \[ \sigma_{\alpha}(x)=q \delta_{s(x), \alpha}-1 \] where

  • \(\alpha=1, \ldots, q\)
  • \(\sum_{\alpha=1}^{q} \sigma_{\alpha}=0 .\)

Take a finite simply connected domain \(D \subset R ^{d}\) and fix \(s(x)=\alpha\) on the boundary of \(D\). Correlation functions computed with such a choice of boundary conditions are denoted with the subscript \(\alpha .\) In the FK representation \(\left\langle\sigma_{\alpha}(x)\right\rangle_{\alpha}\) does not receive any contribution from graphs in which the point \(x\) belongs to clusters which do not touch the boundary. If instead \(x\) belongs to a cluster touching the boundary we simply obtain a factor \(q-1 .\) It follows \[ \left\langle\sigma_{\alpha}\right\rangle_{\alpha}=(q-1) P_{q}(x) \] where

  • \(P_{q}(x)\) is the probability that \(x\) is connected to the boundary of \(D\), computed with the random cluster model measure (1.5).

In the thermodynamic limit in which \(D\) covers the whole plane \(R ^{2}\) the expectation value \(\left\langle\sigma_{\alpha}\right\rangle_{\alpha}\) is a constant. It vanishes in the phase of unbroken \(S_{q}\) symmetry and defines the Potts spontaneous magnetization \(^{20}\) in the broken phase with color \(\alpha .\) The probability \(P_{q}(x)\) is expected to be an analytic function of \(q\) and the limit \[ P \equiv \lim _{q \rightarrow 1} \frac{\left\langle\sigma_{\alpha}\right\rangle_{\alpha}}{q-1} \] is expected to exist and define the order parameter of percolation, introduced in the previous section. Consider now the FK representation of the connected two-point correlation function \[ G_{\alpha \alpha}(x, y)=\left\langle\sigma_{\alpha}(x) \sigma_{\alpha}(y)\right\rangle_{\alpha}-\left\langle\sigma_{\alpha}\right\rangle_{\alpha}^{2} \] computed in the thermodynamic limit with boundary conditions \(\alpha\). The correlation function (1.51) has non-vanishing contributions from graphs \(G\) in which the two points are connected \(^{21}\) or in which the two points are connected to the boundary, see Fig. 1.8. In the thermodynamic limit in the broken phase of color \(\alpha\) the probability that the two points are connected to the boundary coincides with the probability that both points belong to the infinite cluster \(P_{i}(x, y)\). We obtain \(^{22}\) \[ G_{\alpha \alpha}(x, y)=\frac{q-1}{q} P_{a a}(x, y)+(q-1)^{2}\left(P_{i}(x, y)-P^{2}\right) \] and the average size of finite clusters in percolation is \[ S=\lim _{q \rightarrow 1} \frac{\int d^{2} x G_{\alpha \alpha}(x, y)}{q-1} \] The dependence on \(y\) in (1.53) can be eliminated by translational invariance.

Finally we observe that the singular part of the mean cluster number per site in percolation can be computed directly from (1.5) as \[ \frac{\left\langle N_{c}\right\rangle}{N}=\lim _{q \rightarrow 1} \frac{f_{q}^{ sing }}{q-1} \] where \(f_{q}^{\text {sing }}\) is the singular part of the \(q\) -color Potts free energy density and we have taken into account that \(f_{1}=0\). The validity of the relations (1.50),(1.53) and (1.54) relies on the possibility to analytically continue the Potts partition function for arbitrary \(q\). Critical exponents of two-dimensional percolation are then obtained from the basic scaling relations \[ \alpha=2-2 \nu, \quad \nu=\frac{1}{2-X_{\varepsilon}}, \quad \beta=\nu X_{s}, \quad \gamma=2 \nu\left(1-X_{s}\right) \] where \(X_{\varepsilon}\) and \(X_{s}\) are twice the conformal dimensions \(h_{\varepsilon}\) and \(h_{s}\) in \((1.34),\) computed at \(t=2\). Their values are \[ \alpha=-\frac{2}{3}, \quad \beta=\frac{5}{36}, \quad \gamma=\frac{43}{18}, \quad \nu=\frac{4}{3} . \]

  1. According to Langlands et al [15], critical percolation in two dimensions has interesting features in conformal field theory such as the conformal invariance of the three independant crossing probabilites \(1, \Pi_{h}, \Pi_{h v} .\)

  3. Starting from this background, Watts [20] motivated how \(\Pi_{h v}\) can be expressed by a correlation function of boundary operators in the \(Q \rightarrow 1\) limit of the \(Q\) -state Potts model and deduced a differential equation of fith order that agrees with the simulations. Additionally he observed that the three physically relevant solutions already satisfy a third order differential equation.
