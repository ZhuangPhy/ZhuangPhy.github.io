Abstract Superfluidity in liquid \({ }^{3} He\) was discovered at ultra-low temperatures below around \(3 mK\) by Osheroff, Richardson, and Lee in 1972 (Osheroff et al., Phys Rev Lett \(28: 885,1972\); Phys Rev Lett \(29: 920,1972\) ). The \({ }^{3} He\) atom is composed of two protons, one neutron, and two electrons, each of which carries a spin of magnitude \(1 / 2\), and hence is classified as a fermion according to the spin-statistics theorem. Quantum effects in liquid \({ }^{3} He\) is expected to emerge below \(T_{0} \sim 3 K\) according to Table 4.1, and the superfluid transition occurs at about \(10^{-3} T_{ Q }\) to be attributed to the Cooper-pair condensation. As the atom can be regarded roughly as a rigid sphere, it is clearly impossible for a pair of \({ }^{3} He\) atoms to make up an \(s\) -wave bound state that has a high probability of occupying the same position in space. However, they may form a bound state while being separated through a higher \((\ell \geq 1)\) channel of expansion \((8.83)\) to overcome repulsion. Among various theoretical predictions, superfluidity was soon identified to be associated with \(p\) wave \((\ell=1)\) pairing with total spin \(s=1\). Hence, the bound state has a total of \((2 \ell+1)(2 s+1)=9\) internal degrees of freedom, which brings unique features to the \(p\) -wave superfluidity including two distinct phases \(A\) and \(B\) observed in the bulk (see Fig. 13.1). Here, we survey the fundamentals of this superfluidity (Leggett, Rev Mod Phys 47:331, 1975; Vollhardt and Wölfle, The superfluid phases of helium 3 . Taylor & Francis, London, 1990, p 31).

13.1 Effective Pairing Interaction

The \(p\) -wave superfluidity exploits the \(\ell=1\) channel of the expansion \[ V _{\left| k - k ^{\prime}\right|}=\sum_{\ell=0}^{\infty} V _{l}\left(k, k^{\prime}\right) \sum_{m=-\ell}^{\ell} 4 \pi Y_{\ell m}(\hat{ k }) Y_{\ell m}^{*}\left(\hat{ k }^{\prime}\right) \] It follows from \[ Y_{\ell m}(\hat{ k })=\left.\frac{(-1)^{\ell}}{2^{\ell} \ell !} \sqrt{\frac{(2 \ell+1)(\ell+m) !}{4 \pi(\ell-m) !}} \frac{1}{\left(1-z^{2}\right)^{m / 2}} \frac{ d ^{\ell-m}}{ d z^{\ell-m}}\left(1-z^{2}\right)^{\ell}\right|_{z=\cos \theta_{ k }} e ^{ i m \varphi_{ k }} \] that the corresponding spherical harmonic functions are linear in \(\hat{ k } \equiv k /| k |\) \[ Y_{1, \pm 1}(\hat{ k })=\mp \sqrt{\frac{3}{8 \pi}}\left(\hat{k}_{x} \pm i \hat{k}_{y}\right), \quad Y_{10}(\hat{ k })=\sqrt{\frac{3}{4 \pi}} \hat{k}_{z} \] To understand the pairing interaction microscopically, we need a treatment beyond the mean-field level [2] that is outside the scope of this book. Nevertheless, it has been established that the effective interaction near the Fermi surface can also be expressed to a first approximation as \[ V _{1}^{( eff )}\left(k, k^{\prime}\right)= V _{1}^{( eff )} \theta\left(\varepsilon_{ c }-\left|\xi_{k}\right|\right) \theta\left(\varepsilon_{ c }-\left|\xi_{k^{\prime}}\right|\right) \] with \(k_{ B } T_{ c } \ll \varepsilon_{ c } \ll \varepsilon_{ F }\), in exactly the same way as \((9.26)\) for \(s\) -wave pairing. Hence, we retain only the \(\ell=1\) element in \((8.83)\), set \(V _{1}\left(k, k^{\prime}\right) \rightarrow V _{1}^{(\text {eff }}\left(k, k^{\prime}\right)\), and then substitute (13.1). We thereby obtain the \(p\) -wave pairing interaction as \[ V _{\left| k - k ^{\prime}\right|}=4 \pi V _{1}^{( eff )}\left(k, k^{\prime}\right) \sum_{m=-1}^{1} Y_{1 m}(\hat{ k }) Y_{1 m}^{*}\left(\hat{ k }^{\prime}\right)=3 V _{1}^{( eff )}\left(k, k^{\prime}\right) \hat{ k } \cdot \hat{ k }^{\prime} \] ## 13.2 Gap Matrix

The pair potential for homogeneous systems within the mean-field theory is defined generally by \((8.76)\) as a \(2 \times 2\) gap matrix. Let us express it in another way that has been used widely especially for \(p\) -wave pairing. To this end, we notice that any \(2 \times 2\) matrix \(\underline{M}=\left(M_{i j}\right)\) can be expanded in terms of the complete set \((8.42)\), \[ \underline{M} \equiv\left[M_{0} \underline{\sigma}_{0}+ M \cdot \underline{\sigma}\right] i \sigma_{y}=\left[\begin{array}{cc} -M_{x}+ i M_{y} & M_{0}+M_{z} \\ -M_{0}+M_{z} & M_{x}+ i M_{y} \end{array}\right] \] where \(i \sigma_{v}\), has been introduced for convenience. Thus, the four matrix elements \(M_{i j}\left(i, j^{-y}=1,2\right)\) can be expressed alternatively in terms of the four coefficients \(\left(M_{0}, M \right)\). The gap matrix \(\underline{\Delta}( k )\) is also a \(2 \times 2\) matrix, which for \(p\) -wave \((\ell=1)\) pairing should obey the symmetry relation \(\underline{\Delta}^{ T }( k )=\underline{\Delta}( k )\) from \((8.87)\) and \((8.89)\). Hence, the coefficient of \(\underline{\sigma}_{0}\) is 0 in the expansion of \(\underline{\Delta}( k )\) as (13.4). We can also neglect the \(k \equiv| k |\) dependence of \(\underline{\Delta}( k )\) near the Fermi surface for the pairing interaction (13.2). For \(\left|\xi_{k}\right| \leq \varepsilon_{ c }\), we therefore expand the gap matrix for \(p\) -wave pairing to within an overall constant \(\Delta \geq 0\), \[ \Delta( k )=\Delta d (\hat{ k }) \cdot \underline{\sigma} \underline{ i } \underline{\sigma}_{y} \] Explicitly, we have \[ \left[\begin{array}{l} \Delta_{\uparrow \uparrow}( k ) \Delta \uparrow \downarrow( k ) \\ \Delta_{\downarrow \uparrow}( k ) \Delta_{\downarrow \downarrow}( k ) \end{array}\right]=\Delta\left[\begin{array}{cc} -d_{x}(\hat{ k })+ i d_{y}(\hat{ k }) & d_{z}(\hat{ k }) \\ d_{z}(\hat{ k }) & d_{x}(\hat{ k })+ i d_{y}(\hat{ k }) \end{array}\right] . \] With amplitude \(\Delta\) extracted in the expansion, vector \(d\) should obey some normalization condition in terms of angular integration \((8.85) ;\) we choose for convenience \[ \int \frac{ d \Omega_{ k }}{4 \pi}| d (\hat{ k })|^{2}=1 \] It follows from (13.6) that vector \(d (\hat{ k })\) lies in a plane perpendicular to the quantization axis of spin.

As the \(Y_{1 m}(\hat{ k })\) 's are linear in \(\hat{ k }\), we can in general expand \(d\) for \(p\) -wave pairing as \[ d_{\eta}(\hat{ k })=\sum_{\eta^{\prime}} A_{\eta \eta^{\prime}} \hat{k}_{\eta^{\prime}} \quad\left(\eta, \eta^{\prime}=x, y, z\right) \] where \(\left\{A_{\eta \eta^{\prime}}\right\}\) are the expansion coefficients obeying constraint (13.7). To find the equilibrium state, we need to minimize the free energy in terms of not only \(\Delta\) but also \(\left\{A_{\eta \eta^{\prime}}\right\}\). It can be shown that the transition temperature \(T_{ c }\) is the same for any configuration of \(\left\{A_{\eta \eta^{\prime}}\right\}\) within the \(p\) -wave pairing (Problem 13.1). This degeneracy in the free energy is lifted eventually for \(T<T_{ c }\)

13.3 Two Bulk Phases

As already mentioned, liquid \({ }^{3}\) He realizes two bulk superfluid phases distinguished as "A" and "B" in Fig. 13.1, whose gap structures have been confirmed unambiguously (Fig. 13.2). We discuss each of them separately in this and following sections.

B Phase

Although discovered later \([11,12]\), the \(B\) phase has now been established as occupying a large domain in the \(P-T\) phase diagram except for the high-pressure region near \(T_{ c }[15] .\) Its gap structure is identified as the Balian-Werthamer \((B W)\) state [4].

The d vector and gap matrix are expressible, for example, as \[ d (\hat{ k })=\hat{ k } \quad \longleftrightarrow \quad \underline{\Delta}( k )=\Delta\left[\begin{array}{cc} -\hat{k}_{\perp}^{*} & \hat{k}_{z} \\ \hat{k}_{z} & \hat{k}_{\perp} \end{array}\right] \] where \(\hat{k}_{\perp} \equiv \hat{k}_{x}+ i \hat{k}_{y}\), and we have used \((13.6)\) and \((13.7) .\) Substituting \((9.2)\) and \((13.9)\) into \((8.79)\), we find the matrix to be diagonalized, \[ \hat{H}_{ BdG } \equiv\left[\begin{array}{cccc} \xi_{k} & 0 & -\Delta \hat{k}_{\perp}^{*} & \Delta \hat{k}_{z} \\ 0 & \xi_{k} & \Delta \hat{k}_{z} & \Delta \hat{k}_{\perp} \\ -\Delta \hat{k}_{\perp} & \Delta \hat{k}_{z} & -\xi_{k} & 0 \\ \Delta \hat{k}_{z} & \Delta \hat{k}_{\perp}^{*} & 0 & -\xi_{k} \end{array}\right] \] The eigenvalues of this \(4 \times 4\) matrix are easily calculated based on Laplace's expansion of the determinant in terms of cofactors \([3,9] .\) However, we obtain them more readily here by noting that \(H _{ BdG }^{2}\) is proportional to the \(4 \times 4\) unit matrix \(\hat{1}\), \[ \hat{H}_{ BdG }^{2}=E_{k}^{2} \hat{1}, \quad E_{k} \equiv \sqrt{\xi_{k}^{2}+\Delta^{2}} \] It follows from this equality and \((8.43)\) that the eigenvalues of \(H _{ BdG }\) are given by \(\pm E_{k}\) with double degeneracy for each. The eigenvectors corresponding to \(E_{k}\) are obtained from the first and second rows of the eigenvalue equation: \[ \left(\hat{ H }_{ BdG }-E_{k} \hat{1}\right)\left[\begin{array}{l} u _{\bar{\alpha}}( k ) \\ v _{\bar{\alpha}}( k ) \end{array}\right]=\left[\begin{array}{l} 0 \\ 0 \end{array}\right] \quad(\tilde{\alpha}=1,2) \] by comparing them with the first and second rows of \((9.8)\) for \(\tilde{\alpha}=1,2\). We obtain \[ \left[\begin{array}{c} u _{1}( k ) \\ v _{1}( k ) \end{array}\right]=\left[\begin{array}{c} u_{k} \\ 0 \\ -v_{k} \hat{k}_{\perp} \\ v_{k} \hat{k}_{z} \end{array}\right], \quad\left[\begin{array}{c} u _{2}( k ) \\ v _{2}( k ) \end{array}\right]=\left[\begin{array}{c} 0 \\ u_{k} \\ v_{k} \hat{k}_{z} \\ v_{k} \hat{k}_{\perp}^{*} \end{array}\right] \] where \(u_{k}\) and \(v_{k}\) are also given by \((9.6)\) for \(s\) -wave pairing. Substitution of \((13.12)\) into \((8.72)\) yields \(\tilde{\rho}^{(1)}( k )\) in terms of \(\underline{\Delta}( k )\) in (13.9), \[ \underline{\tilde{\rho}}^{(1)}( k )=\frac{\Delta}{2 E_{k}}\left[\begin{array}{cc} -\hat{k}_{\perp}^{*} & \hat{k}_{z} \\ \hat{k}_{z} & \hat{k}_{\perp} \end{array}\right]\left(1-2 \bar{n}_{k}\right)=\underline{\Delta}( k ) \frac{1}{2 E_{k}} \tanh \frac{\beta E_{k}}{2} \] Next we substitute \((13.3)\) and \((13.13)\) into \((8.76)\), transform the sum over \(k ^{\prime}\) into an integral as \((8.90)\), approximate \(N\left(\varepsilon_{k^{\prime}}\right) \approx N\left(\varepsilon_{ F }\right)\), make the change of variable, \(\varepsilon_{k^{\prime}} \rightarrow \xi_{k^{\prime}}\), and perform the angular integration over \(\hat{ k }^{\prime}\) using \[ \int \frac{ d \Omega_{ k }}{4 \pi} \hat{k}_{\eta} \hat{k}_{\eta^{\prime}}=\delta_{\eta \eta^{\prime}} \frac{1}{3} \quad\left(\eta, \eta^{\prime}=x, y, z\right) \] We thereby obtain the gap equation \[ \Delta( k )=-N\left(\varepsilon_{ F }\right) V _{1}^{( eff )} \int_{-\varepsilon_{ c }}^{\varepsilon_{ c }} d \xi \Delta( k ) \frac{1}{2 E} \tanh \frac{\beta E}{2} \] Further, we remove a common "factor" \(\Delta( k )\) and introduce a dimensionless coupling constant: \[ g_{1} \equiv-N\left(\varepsilon_{ F }\right) V _{1}^{( eff )} \] We then find that \((13.15)\) is identical to \((9.30)\) for \(s\) -wave pairing with \(g_{0} \rightarrow g_{1} .\) In particular, the transition temperature can be expressed in terms of \(g_{1}\) above, \[ k_{ B } T_{ c }=\frac{2 e ^{\gamma}}{\pi} \varepsilon_{ c } e ^{-1 / g_{1}} \approx 1.13 \varepsilon_{ c } e ^{-1 / g_{1}} \] It also follows from (13.11) that the thermodynamic properties of the BW state in terms of \(T / T_{ c }\) are completely identical to those for \(s\) -wave pairing.

Finally, of note is the fact that the state \(\underline{R} d ( k )\) obtained from (13.9) by a three-dimensional rotation \(\underline{R}\) is also the BW state. The degeneracy is partially spontaneously broken by the dipole interaction between nuclear spins, which is not included here \([10,15]\). The remaining degeneracy is also lifted by other factors such as surface effects and initial fluctuations.

A Phase

The superfluid phase of \({ }^{3} He\) that was first discovered was the high-temperature highpressure phase called the A phase \([11,12]\), which is now identified as the AndersonBrinkmann-Morel \((A B M)\) state. Having an anisotropic energy gap \([10,15]\), this state was found as a theoretical candidate for the stable \(p\) -wave pairing by Anderson and Morel in \(1961[2]\), and studied in more detail by Anderson and Brinkmann in regard to its stabilization [1].

Given the normalization condition of (13.7), the \(d\) vector of the ABM state may be written \[ d (\hat{ k })=\sqrt{\frac{3}{2}} \hat{k}_{\perp} \hat{ z } \quad \longleftrightarrow \quad \underline{\Delta}( k )=\sqrt{\frac{3}{2}} \Delta\left[\begin{array}{cc} 0 & \hat{k}_{\perp} \\ \hat{k}_{\perp} & 0 \end{array}\right] \] with \(\hat{k}_{\perp} \equiv \hat{k}_{x}+ i \hat{k}_{y}\), for example. Let us substitute \((9.2)\) and \((13.18)\) into \((8.79)\). A couple of comments are in order before closing the section. First, the \(ABM\) state also has a large degeneracy. To be specific, consider (13.18) and rotate both \(\hat{ z }\) in the spin space and \(\hat{k}_{\perp}\) in the orbital space independently around arbitrary axes by arbitrary angles. The state thereby obtained is also an ABM state with the same free energy. Part of the degeneracy is spontaneously broken through dipole interactions between nuclear spins that are not included here \([10,15]\). The remaining degeneracies are also lifted by other effects such as surface effects and initial fluctuations. Second, the complex d vector of (13.18) implicitly describes a Cooper pair with an orbital angular momentum \(\hbar\), which is expected to produce a total angular momentum of \((N / 2) \hbar\) at \(T=0\) due to the macroscopic condensation into identical two-particle bound states \([6,7]\). This effect remains to be confirmed experimentally. 13.4 Gap Anisotropy and Quasiparticle Density of States The gap structures are different between the \(A\) and \(B\) phases; whereas it is isotropic for the \(B\) phase, the gap closes at a couple of points in the A phase. We now see that this difference manifests itself in the quasiparticle density of states at low energies, which are observable by low-temperature thermodynamic experiments. Thus, our main purpose here is to find general means to identify this gap anisotropy in experiments [14].

The quasiparticle density of states is defined by \((9.46)\), which is expressible as \((9.48)\) for \(s\) -wave pairing. Its extension to \(p\) -wave pairing is easily performed by incorporating the angular dependence into the energy gap as \(\Delta \rightarrow\left|\Delta_{ k }\right|\) and averaging the resulting expression over the solid angle, \[ D_{ s }(E)=D\left(\varepsilon_{ F }\right) \int \frac{ d \Omega_{ k }}{4 \pi} \frac{|E|}{\left(E^{2}-\left|\Delta_{ k }\right|^{2}\right)^{1 / 2}} \theta\left(|E|-\left|\Delta_{ k }\right|\right) . \] We consider three \(\left|\Delta_{ k }\right|\) 's given in terms of \(\hat{ k }=\left(\sin \theta_{ k } \cos \varphi_{ k }, \sin \theta_{ k } \sin \varphi_{ k }, \cos \theta_{ k }\right)\) as \[ \left|\Delta_{ k }\right|=\left\{\begin{array}{ll} \Delta & : \text { BW state } \\ \sqrt{3 / 2} \Delta \sin \theta_{ k } & : \text { ABM state } \\ \sqrt{3} \Delta \cos \theta_{ k } & \text { : polar state } \end{array}\right. \] Thus, besides the BW and ABM states given by (13.11) and (13.20), respectively, a novel polar state \(d ( k )=\sqrt{3} \hat{k}_{z}[10,15]\) is included here. Although it is not stabilized as a bulk phase, it has a distinct gap structure in that it closes on the equator of the Fermi surface (Fig. 13.2) to make it worth including in the present considerations. Integral (13.27) for each gap structure can be performed easily, and we obtain (Problem 13.2) \(\frac{D_{ s }(E)}{D\left(\varepsilon_{ F }\right)}=\left\{\begin{array}{ll}\frac{|E|}{\left(E^{2}-\Delta^{2}\right)^{1 / 2}} \theta(|E|-\Delta) & : \text { BW state } \\ \frac{|E|}{2 \Delta_{\max }} \ln \left|\frac{|E|+\Delta_{\max }}{|E|-\Delta_{\max }}\right| & : \text { ABM state } \\ \frac{\pi|E|}{2 \Delta_{\max }}\left[\theta\left(\Delta_{\max }-|E|\right)+\theta\left(|E|-\Delta_{\max }\right) \frac{2}{\pi} \arcsin \frac{\Delta_{\max }}{|E|}\right] & : \text { polar state }\end{array}\right.\)

with \(\Delta_{\max }=\sqrt{3 / 2} \Delta\left(\Delta_{\max }=\sqrt{3} \Delta\right)\) for the ABM (polar) state; each is plotted in Fig. 13.3. At low energies of \(\left|E / \Delta_{\max }\right| \ll 1, D_{ s }(E)\) can be approximated as \[ \frac{D_{ s }(E)}{D\left(\varepsilon_{ F }\right)}=\left\{\begin{array}{ll} 0 & : \text { BW state (full gap) } \\ \left(|E| / \Delta_{\max }\right)^{2} & : \text { ABM state (point nodes) } \\ \pi|E| / 2 \Delta_{\max } & \text { : polar state (line node) } \end{array}\right. \] These relations give the general connection between the low-energy density of states and the dimension of nodes in the gap; they are also valid for anisotropic Fermi surfaces except for the prefactor.

The low-energy density of states manifests itself in temperature dependences of various thermodynamic quantities. First, we consider heat capacity. Let us substitute the latter two expressions of \((13.30)\) into \((9.47)\), rewrite \(\partial \bar{n}(E) / \partial T=\) \(-(E / T) \partial \bar{n}(E) / \partial E\), perform an integration by parts, and express \(C\) in terms of the dimensionless integral (4.39). We thereby find that the low-temperature heat capacity behaves as \[ C(T \rightarrow 0) \propto\left\{\begin{array}{ll} e ^{-\Delta / k B T} & : \text { full gap } \\ T^{3} & \text { : point nodes } \\ T^{2} & \text { : line nodes } \end{array}\right. \] where we have used \((9.49)\) for the full gap. Second, we consider an anisotropic extension of the Yosida function (10.16) given by \[ Y(\hat{ k }, T) \equiv-2 \int_{0}^{\infty} d \xi \frac{\partial \bar{n}\left(E_{ k }\right)}{\partial E_{ k }}, \quad E_{ k } \equiv \sqrt{\xi^{2}+\left|\Delta_{ k }\right|^{2}} \] Its angular average can be transformed by writing \(d \xi=\left( d \xi / d E_{ k }\right) d E_{ k }\), substituting \(d \xi / d E_{ k }=E_{ k } \theta\left(E_{ k }-\left|\Delta_{ k }\right|\right) / \sqrt{E_{ k }^{2}-\left|\Delta_{ k }\right|^{2}}\), exchanging the order of integrations over \(d \Omega_{ k }\) and \(d E_{ k }\), and using \((13.27)\) as \[ \bar{Y}(T \rightarrow 0) \propto\left\{\begin{array}{ll} e ^{-\Delta / k B T} & : \text { full gap } \\ T^{2} & \text { : point nodes } \\ T & \text { : line nodes } \end{array}\right. \] where we have used (10.48) for the full gap. The temperature dependence is observable by measuring the spin susceptibility and penetration depth, as described by \((10.18)\) and \((10.40)\) with \((10.22)\).
