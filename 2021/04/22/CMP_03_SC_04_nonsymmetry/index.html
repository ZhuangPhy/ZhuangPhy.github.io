gauge freedom

To define topological numbers of a line node

Because of the gauge freedom \[ u( k ) \rightarrow e^{i \theta( k )} u( k ) \] \(u( k )\) is not determined uniquely. We have to fix the gauge. Generally, a gauge fixed solution has a singularity on \(S^{1}\), thus it can not be well-defined on the entire \(S^{1}\). Two or more solutions with different gauge fixings are needed to cover the entire \(S^{1}\). We consider a couple of solutions \(u^{(1)}( k )\) and \(u^{(2)}( k )\), and demand that the first component of \(u^{(1)}( k )\) and the second one of \(u^{(2)}( k )\) are real. Because of these different demands, the solutions \(u^{(1)}( k )\) and \(u^{(2)}( k )\) have different singularities from each other. Therefore, we can cover the entire \(S^{1}\) by using them.

Kramers doublet

Let us next consider the Kramers doublet of \(u^{(2)}( k )\), \(\Theta u^{(2)}(- k )^{*}\). By definition, the Kramers doublet has the real first component as well as \(u^{(1)}( k )\). Therefore the following two possibility arises, 1. \(\Theta u^{(2)}(- k )^{*}=u^{(1)}( k )\left(\right.\) or \(\left.-u^{(1)}( k )\right)\), 2. \(\Theta u^{(2)}(- k )^{*} \neq \pm u^{(1)}( k )\).

Here we concentrate on the latter case, since only this is relevant to the topological numbers of the line node \([47]\). In this case, \(\Theta u^{(2)}(- k )^{*}\) has a different singularity from \(u^{(1)}( k )\), thus we can use \(\Theta u^{(2)}(- k )^{*}\) instead of \(u^{(2)}( k )\) to cover the entire region of \(S^{1}\). After all, the following two solutions with the real first component are obtained, \[ u^{(+)}( k ) \equiv u^{(1)}( k ), \quad u^{(-)}( k ) \equiv \Theta u^{(2)}(- k )^{*} \] If the solutions \(u^{(+)}( k )\) and \(u^{(-)}( k )\) are nonsingular on \(U_{+}\) and \(U_{-}\), respectively, \(S^{1}\) is given by the union of \(U_{+}\) and \(U_{-}\) \[ S^{1}=U_{+} \cup U_{-} \]

Majorana fermions in edges and in a vortex core - To be concrete, we consider a

  1. two-dimensional
  2. \(d\) -wave superconductor
  3. with the Rashba spin-orbit (SO)interaction,

though the following argument is basically applicable to any NCS nodal superconductors. The Hamiltonian is given by \[ H =\frac{1}{2} \sum_{ k } \psi_{ k }^{\dagger} H ( k ) \psi_{ k } \] with \[ H ( k )=\left(\begin{array}{cc}\epsilon_{ k }-h \sigma_{ z }+ g _{ k } \cdot \sigma & i \Delta_{ k } \sigma_{y} \\ -i \Delta_{ k } \sigma_{y} & -\epsilon_{ k }+h \sigma_{z}+ g _{ k } \cdot \sigma ^{*}\end{array}\right) \] where

  • ${}{k}=(c{ k }^{}, c_{ k }^{}, c_{- k }, c_{- k }) $
  • $ {k}=-2 t (k{x}+ k_{y} )-$
  • $ g_{ k }=2 (k_{y},-k_{x}, 0),$
  • $ k =(k_{x}, k_{y})$, and \(\sigma =\left(\sigma_{x}, \sigma_{y}, \sigma_{z}\right)\) the Pauli matrices.
  • \(h=\mu_{ B } H_{z}\) is a Zeeman magnetic field.

The gap function is

  • the \(d_{x^{2}-y^{2}}\) -wave type, \(\Delta_{k}=\Delta_{0}\left(\cos k_{x}-\cos k_{y}\right)\),
  • or the \(d_{x y}\) -wave type, \(\Delta_{k}=\Delta_{0} \sin k_{x} \sin k_{y} .\)

We neglect the orbital effect of the magnetic field for a while since it does not change our results qualitatively, as long as \(H_{z}<H_{c 2}\).
