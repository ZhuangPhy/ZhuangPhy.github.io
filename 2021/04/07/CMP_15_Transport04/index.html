The system can then be modelled by the following effective Hamiltonian \[ H=H_{ c }+\sum_{\alpha} H_{\alpha}+\sum_{\alpha} H_{ t , \alpha} \] where

  • \(H_{\alpha}\) is the Hamiltonian of the the lead denoted by \(\alpha\)
  • $ H_{ c }$ is the Hamiltonian of the central superconducting system
  • \(H_{ t , \alpha}\) is the tunnelling Hamiltonian, describing the coupling of each lead and the central region.

Initially at time \(t=-\infty\) we assume that the systems are uncoupled and in equilibrium, i.e. \(H_{ T }=0 .\) The systems are then brought into contact, and since the chemical potentials of the subsystems may not be equal, the system will in general not be in equilibrium and a current will flow between the systems. If the leads are connected to big electron resevoirs such as a battery or a powersupply the chemical potentials of the leads will be constant and after some time, say at \(t=0,\) a steady state current through the central region has been reached.

While we want to study a nanowire with induced superconductivity, we will for the time being simply consider the central region as being an arbitrary superconducting system, which can be modelled by \[ H_{ c }=\sum_{i j} \psi_{ c i}^{\dagger} H _{i j} \psi_{ c j}+\sum_{i j}\left(\psi_{ c i}^{\dagger} \Delta_{i j} \psi_{ c j}^{\dagger}+\psi_{ c i} \Delta_{i j}^{*} \psi_{ c j}\right)+V_{ int }\left(\left\{\psi_{ c i}^{\dagger}\right\},\left\{\psi_{ c j}\right\}\right) \] where the summation indices represents the the quantum numbers, typically spacial position and spin, i.e. \(i=( r , \sigma)\). The corresponding free Hamiltonian, i.e. with \(V_{\text {int }}\left(\left\{\psi_{c i}^{\dagger}\right\},\left\{\psi_{c j}\right\}\right)=0\) can be diagonalized by a Bogoliubov transformation \[ \left(\begin{array}{c} \psi_{ c i} \\ \psi_{ c i}^{\dagger} \end{array}\right)=\sum_{\mu} U _{i \mu}\left(\begin{array}{l} d_{\mu} \\ d_{\mu}^{\dagger} \end{array}\right) \quad \Leftrightarrow \quad\left(\begin{array}{l} d_{\mu} \\ d_{\mu}^{\dagger} \end{array}\right)=\sum_{i} U _{i \mu}^{\dagger}\left(\begin{array}{c} \psi_{ c i} \\ \psi_{ c i}^{\dagger} \end{array}\right), \quad U _{i \mu}=\left(\begin{array}{cc} u_{i \mu} & v_{i \mu} \\ v_{i \mu}^{*} & u_{i \mu}^{*} \end{array}\right) \] where the coefficients \(u_{i \mu}, v_{i \mu}\) are found from solving the corresponding Bogoliubov-deGennes equations. In the eigenbasis the Hamiltonian simply takes the form \[ H_{ c }=\sum_{\mu} \epsilon_{\mu} d_{\mu}^{\dagger} d_{\mu}+V_{ int }\left(\left\{d_{\mu}^{\dagger}\right\},\left\{d_{\mu}\right\}\right) \] where \(\epsilon_{\mu}\) are the positive energy solutions to the BdG equations and \(V_{\text {int }}\left(\left\{d_{\mu}^{\dagger}\right\},\left\{d_{\mu}\right\}\right)\) is the interaction part rewritten in the eigenbasis. The leads will typically be metallic and describing them as a free electron gas is thus a good approximation and this will in fact be required for this method to work. However, in theory we can also include the case where the leads are superconducting as long as this can be described in the \(BCS\) framework. Though we will actually not consider that case we here just briefly show how this general case treated. The BCS Hamiltonians for the leads are \[ H_{\alpha}=\sum_{i j} \psi_{\alpha i}^{\dagger} H _{\alpha i j} \psi_{\alpha j}+\sum_{i j}\left(\psi_{\alpha i} \Delta_{\alpha i j} \psi_{\alpha j}+\psi_{\alpha i}^{\dagger} \Delta_{\alpha i j}^{*} \psi_{\alpha j}^{\dagger}\right) \] As in the case of the central region the eigenbasis is found by solving the BdG-equations and the corresponding eigenstate annihilation operator is \(c_{\alpha k}=\sum_{i}\left(u_{i \alpha k}^{*} \psi_{\alpha i}+v_{i \alpha k} \psi_{\alpha i}^{\dagger}\right)\) where \(k\) labels the eigenstate. The Hamiltonian can then be written \[ H_{\alpha}=\sum_{k} \varepsilon_{\alpha k} c_{\alpha k}^{\dagger} c_{\alpha k} \] As usual we can define a matrix in Nambu space, \(U _{i \alpha k}\) defining the Bogoliubov transformation diagonalization of the lead Hamiltonian. We also ote that in the case of normal metallic leads the hole part of the eigenstates vanish, \(v_{i \alpha k}=0\). The tunnelling Hamiltonian describes the tunnelling of electron between the lead and the central region and is \[ \begin{aligned} H_{ t , \alpha} &=\sum_{i j}\left(t_{\alpha i j} \psi_{\alpha i}^{\dagger} \psi_{ c j}+t_{\alpha i j}^{*} \psi_{ c i}^{\dagger} \psi_{\alpha j}\right) \\ &=\left(\begin{array}{ll} \psi_{\alpha i}^{\dagger} & \psi_{\alpha i} \end{array}\right)\left(\begin{array}{cc} t_{\alpha i j} & 0 \\ 0 & -t_{\alpha i j}^{*} \end{array}\right)\left(\begin{array}{c} \psi_{ c j} \\ \psi_{ c j}^{\dagger} \end{array}\right) \\ &=\left(\begin{array}{ll} \psi_{\alpha i}^{\dagger} & \psi_{\alpha i} \end{array}\right) T _{\alpha i j}\left(\begin{array}{c} \psi_{ c j} \\ \psi_{ c j}^{\dagger} \end{array}\right) \end{aligned} \] In the eigenbasis this becomes where we have defined \[ T _{\alpha k \mu}=\left(\begin{array}{cc} t_{\alpha k \mu} & w_{\alpha k \mu} \\ -w_{\alpha k \mu}^{*} & -t_{\alpha k \mu}^{*} \end{array}\right)=\sum_{i j} U _{i \alpha k}^{\dagger} T _{\alpha i j} U _{j \mu} \] Having rewritten the problem in the eigenbasis of the lead and wire we are now able to write an expression for the current from lead \(\alpha\) and into the central region. The electrical current is the current of positive charge from the lead into the central region which is equal to the number of electrons leaving the wire and getting into the lead per unit time times the elementary charge, therefore when a steady state situation has been obtained at \(t=0\) the current is given by \[ I_{\alpha}=e\left\langle\dot{N}_{\alpha}(0)\right\rangle=\frac{i e}{\hbar}\left\langle\left[H, N_{\alpha}\right](0)\right\rangle \] The number operator \(N_{\alpha}\) commutes with the both lead Hamiltonian and the central region Hamiltonian so we only need to calculate the commutator of the number operator and the tunneling Hamiltonian, which is \[ \begin{aligned} \left[H_{ t \alpha}, N_{\alpha}\right]=&\left[\sum_{k \mu}\left\{t_{\alpha k \mu} c_{\alpha c_{\alpha k^{\prime}}^{\dagger} c_{\alpha k^{\prime}}\right] \\ =& \sum_{k k^{\prime}, \mu}\left\{t_{\alpha k \mu}\left[c_{\alpha k}^{\dagger} d_{\mu}, c_{\alpha k^{\prime}}^{\dagger} c_{\alpha k^{\prime}}\right]\right\} \end{aligned} \] Using that \[ \begin{array}{l} {\left[c_{\alpha k}^{\dagger} d_{\mu}^{(\dagger)}, n_{\alpha k^{\prime}}\right]=-c_{\alpha k} d_{\mu}^{(\dagger)} \delta_{k k}+w_{\alpha k \mu} d_{\mu} c_{\alpha k}\right\} \] Thus the current may be written \[ I_{\alpha}=\frac{i e}{\hbar} \sum_{k \mu}\left\{-t_{\alpha k \mu}\left\langle c_{\alpha k}^{\dagger}
