Primer

start from a tight-binding Hamiltonian \[ H=\sum_{r, r^{\prime}} \psi^{\dagger}(r) H \left(r, r^{\prime}\right) \psi\left(r^{\prime}\right) \]

where

  • \(\psi(r)\) is an \(N_{f}\) -component fermion annihilation operator,
  • index \(r=\left(r_{1}, r_{2}, \ldots, r_{d}\right)\) labels a site on a \(d\) -dimensional lattice (the internal indices are suppressed).
  • Each block in the single particle Hamiltonian \(H \left(r, r^{\prime}\right)\) is an \(N_{f} \times N_{f}\) matrix, satisfying the hermiticity condition \(H ^{\dagger}\left(r^{\prime}, r\right)= H \left(r, r^{\prime}\right) .\)

We assume the total size of the single particle Hamiltonian is \(N_{f} V \times N_{f} V\), where \(V\) is the total number of lattice sites. The components in \(\psi(r)\) can describe, e.g., orbitals or spin degrees of freedom, as well as different sites within a crystal unit cell centered at \(r\).

2.1 Symmetries in band insulators and superconductors

Provided the system has translational symmetry, \(H \left(r, r^{\prime}\right)= H \left(r-r^{\prime}\right)\), with periodic boundary conditions in each spatial direction ( i.e., the system is defined on a torus \(T^{d}\) ), we can perform the Fourier transformation and obtain in momentum space \[ H=\sum_{k \in Bz } \psi^{\dagger}(k) H (k) \psi(k) \] where

  • the crystal momentum \(k\) runs over the first Brillouin zone \(( Bz )\),
  • the Fourier component of the fermion operator and the Hamiltonian are given by

\[ \psi(r)=V^{-1 / 2} \sum_{k \in Bz } e^{i k \cdot r} \psi(k) \]

\[ H (k)=\sum_{r} e^{-i k \cdot r} H (r) \]

respectively. The Bloch Hamiltonian \(H (k)\) is diagonalized by \[ H (k)\left|u^{a}(k)\right\rangle=\varepsilon^{a}(k)\left|u^{a}(k)\right\rangle, \quad a=1, \ldots, N_{f} \]

\[ \begin{array}{|c|cccccccc|ccc|} \hline \text { AZ class } \backslash d & 0 & 1 & 2 & 3 & 4 & 5 & 6 & 7 & T & C & S \\ \hline \hline \text { A } & Z & 0 & Z & 0 & Z & 0 & Z & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 \\ \text { AIII } & 0 & Z & 0 & Z & 0 & Z & 0 & Z & 0 & 0 & 1 \\ \hline \text { AI } & Z & 0 & 0 & 0 & 2 Z & 0 & Z _{2} & Z _{2} & + & 0 & 0 \\ \text { BDI } & Z _{2} & Z & 0 & 0 & 0 & 2 Z & 0 & Z _{2} & + & + & 1 \\ \text { D } & Z _{2} & Z _{2} & Z & 0 & 0 & 0 & 2 Z & 0 & 0 & + & 0 \\ \text { DIII } & 0 & Z _{2} & Z _{2} & Z & 0 & 0 & 0 & 2 Z & - & + & 1 \\ \text { AII } & 2 Z & 0 & Z _{2} & Z _{2} & Z & 0 & 0 & 0 & - & 0 & 0 \\ \text { CII } & 0 & 2 Z & 0 & Z _{2} & Z _{2} & Z & 0 & 0 & - & - & 1 \\ \text { C } & 0 & 0 & 2 Z & 0 & Z _{2} & Z _{2} & Z & 0 & 0 & - & 0 \\ \text { CI } & 0 & 0 & 0 & 2 Z & 0 & Z _{2} & Z _{2} & Z & + & - & 1 \\ \hline \end{array} \]

Hamiltonian for the graphene model \[ H=t \sum_{<i j>\sigma} c_{i \sigma}^{\dagger} c_{j \sigma}+i \lambda_{s o} \sum_{<<i j>>\sigma \delta} \nu_{i j} c_{i \sigma}^{\dagger} s_{\sigma \delta}^{z} c_{j \delta}+\lambda_{\nu} \sum_{i, \sigma} \zeta_{i} c_{i \sigma}^{\dagger} c_{i \sigma} \]

Edge mode: hopping

The Hamiltonian of nearest neighbor hopping from site to site \[ H_{t}=-t \sum_{\sigma, m, n}\left[a_{\sigma}^{\dagger}(m, n) b_{\sigma}(m, n)+a_{\sigma}^{\dagger}(m, n) b_{\sigma}(m-1, n)+a_{\sigma}^{\dagger}(m, n) b_{\sigma}(m, n-1)+H . c .\right] \] the length of strip is infinite in the \(x\) direction so the Hamiltonian can be Fourier transformed in this direction. \[ H=-t \sum_{n, \sigma}\left(a_{\sigma k}^{\dagger}(n) b_{\sigma k}(n)\left(1+e^{i k a}\right)+a_{\sigma k}^{\dagger}(n) b_{\sigma k}(n-1)+\text { H.c. }\right) \] the eigenstates of the Hamiltonian

\[ \mid k, \sigma \rangle =\sum_{n}\left[\alpha_{k, n}\left|a, k, n, \sigma \rangle+\beta_{k, n}\right| b, k, n, \sigma \rangle\right] \] where the symbols \(a\) and \(b\) describe the A and B sublattice basis. Compare the two sides of the Schrödinger equation \(H\left|k, \sigma \rangle=E_{k}\right| k, \sigma \rangle\) We neglect the spin index now since spin up and down give the same result.

The relations between \(\alpha\) and \(\beta\) are \[ \begin{array}{l} E_{k} \alpha_{k, n}=-t\left[\left(1+e^{i k a}\right) \beta_{k, n}+\beta_{k, n-1}\right] \\ E_{k} \beta_{k, n}=-t\left[\left(1+e^{-i k a}\right) \alpha_{k, n}+\alpha_{k, n+1}\right] \end{array} \] The strip of graphene has a finite size \(N\) in the \(y\) direction. The boundary condition is imposed as \[ \alpha_{k,-1}=\beta_{k,-1}=\alpha_{k, N+1}=\beta_{k, N+1}=0 \] Consider \(E=0\) at the boundary \(n=1\) and \(n=N\) \[ \begin{array}{c} \beta_{k, 0}=0 \\ \alpha_{k, N}=0 \end{array} \] The nearest neighbor hopping does not imply either \(\beta_{k, N}=0\) or \(\alpha_{k, 0}=0\).

Also Eqs read as recurrence equations \[ \begin{array}{l} E_{k} \alpha_{k, n}=-t\left[\left(1+e^{i k a}\right) \beta_{k, n}+\beta_{k, n-1}\right] \\ E_{k} \beta_{k, n}=-t\left[\left(1+e^{-i k a}\right) \alpha_{k, n}+\alpha_{k, n+1}\right]\\ \qquad \qquad \Downarrow \end{array} \]

\[ \begin{array}{l} 0=\left(1+e^{i k a}\right) \beta_{k, n}+\beta_{k, n-1} \\ 0=\left(1+e^{-i k a}\right) \alpha_{k, n}+\alpha_{k, n+1} \end{array} \]

The solutions for \(\alpha_{k, n}\) and \(\beta_{k, N-n}\) are \[ \begin{array}{c} \alpha_{k, n}=(-2 \cos (k a / 2))^{n} e^{-i n k a / 2} \alpha_{k, 0} \\ \beta_{k, N-n}=(-2 \cos (k a / 2))^{n} e^{i n k a / 2} \beta_{k, N} \end{array} \] as \(n \rightarrow N, \alpha_{k, n}\) and \(\beta_{k, N-n}\) converge to zero as the behavior of the edge states.

That is, \(|-2 \cos (k a / 2)|<1\). Hence, zero energy edge states are present at \(4 \pi / 3>k a>2 \pi / 3\). The two solutions \(\alpha_{k, n}\) and \(\beta_{k, n}\) are two independent edge states in the top/bottom of the strip in A/B sublattice, respectively.
