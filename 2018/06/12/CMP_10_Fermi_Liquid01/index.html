  2. 第二项代表“准粒子”之间的相互作用

最后我们求出自由能，为此我们需要求熵。

If the density of quasi-particles is low, it is sufficient to expand the energy in the small deviations in particle number \(\delta n_{ p \sigma}=n_{ p \sigma}-n_{ p \sigma}^{(0)}\) from equilibrium. This leads to the Landau energy functional \(\mathcal{E} \left(\left\{n_{ p \sigma}\right\}\right)=E\left(\left\{n_{ p \sigma}\right\}\right)-\mu N,\) where \[ \mathcal{E} = \mathcal{E}_{0}+\sum_{ p \sigma}\left(E_{ p \sigma}^{(0)}-\mu\right) \delta n_{ p \sigma}+\frac{1}{2} \sum_{ p , p ^{\prime}, \sigma, \sigma^{\prime}} f_{ p \sigma, p ^{\prime} \sigma^{\prime}} \delta n_{ p \sigma} \delta n_{ p ^{\prime} \sigma^{\prime}}+\cdots \]

first-order coefficient: isolated quasi-particle

The first-order coefficient \[ \epsilon_{ p \sigma}^{(0)} \equiv E_{ p \sigma}^{(0)}-\mu=\frac{\delta E }{\delta n_{ p \sigma}} \] describes the excitation energy of an isolated quasi-particle

quasi-particle energy

The quasi-particle energy can be expanded linearly in momentum near the Fermi surface: \[ E_{p}^{(0)}=v_{F}\left(p-p_{F}\right)+\mu^{(0)} \] where

  • \(v_{F}\) is the Fermi velocity at the Fermi energy $^{(0)} $
  • \(\mu^{(0)}\) is the chemical potential in the ground state.

Effective mass

The quasi-particle effective mass \(m^{*}\) is then defined in terms of \(v_{F}\) as \[ v_{F}=\left.\frac{d \epsilon_{p}^{(0)}}{d p}\right|_{p=p_{F}}=\frac{p_{F}}{m^{*}} \]

Density of states

We can use this mass to define a quasi-particle density of states \[ \begin{aligned} N ^{*}(\epsilon)&=2 \sum_{ p } \delta\left(\epsilon-\epsilon_{ p }^{(0)}\right) \\&=2 \int \frac{4 \pi p^{2} d p}{(2 \pi \hbar)^{3}} \delta\left(\epsilon-\epsilon_{p}^{(0)}\right) \\&=\frac{p^{2}}{\pi^{2} \hbar^{3}} \frac{d p}{d \epsilon_{p}^{(0)}} \end{aligned} \] Using \(\left.\frac{d \epsilon_{p}^{(0)}}{d p}\right|_{p=p_{F}}=\frac{p_{F}}{m^{*}}\), it follows that \[ N ^{*}(0)=\frac{m^{*} p_{F}}{\pi^{2} \hbar^{3}} \] large effective masses lead to large densities of states.

The second-order coefficients

The second-order coefficients describe the interactions between quasi-particles at the Fermi surface. \[ f_{ p \sigma, p ^{\prime} \sigma^{\prime}}=\left.\frac{\delta^{2} E }{\delta n_{ p \sigma} \delta n_{ p ^{\prime} \sigma^{\prime}}}\right|_{\delta n_{ p ^{\prime \prime} \sigma^{\prime \prime}}=0} \] The Landau interaction can be regarded as an interaction operator that acts on a thin shell of quasi-particle states near the Fermi surface.

quasi-particles scattering

If \(\hat{n}_{ p \sigma}=\psi_{ p \sigma}^{\dagger} \psi_{ p \sigma}\) is the quasi-particle occupancy, where \(\psi_{ p \sigma}^{\dagger}\) is the quasi-particle-creation operator, then one is tempted to write \[ H_{I} \sim \frac{1}{2} \sum_{ p \sigma p ^{\prime} \sigma^{\prime}} f_{ p \sigma, p ^{\prime} \sigma^{\prime}} \hat{n}_{ p \sigma} \hat{n}_{ p ^{\prime} \sigma^{\prime}} \] Written this way, we see that the Landau interaction term is a forward scattering amplitude between quasiparticles whose initial and final momenta are unchanged. In practice, one has to allow for slowly varying quasiparticle densities \(n_{ p \sigma}( x ),\) writing \[ H_{I} \sim \frac{1}{2} \int d^{3} x \sum_{ p \sigma p ^{\prime} \sigma^{\prime}} f_{ p \sigma, p ^{\prime} \sigma^{\prime}} \hat{n}_{ p \sigma}( x ) \hat{n}_{ p ^{\prime} \sigma^{\prime}}( x ) \] where

  • \(n_{ p \sigma}( x )\) is the local quasi-particle density.
  • Using the Fourier transformed density operator \(\hat{n}_{ p \sigma}( q )=\psi_{ p - q / 2 \sigma}^{\dagger} \psi_{ p + q / 2 \sigma}=\int_{ x } e^{-i q \cdot x } n_{ p \sigma}( x ),\)

cut-off

a more correct formulation of the Landau interaction is \[ H_{I}=\frac{1}{2} \sum_{ p \sigma p ^{\prime} \sigma^{\prime},| q |<\Lambda} f_{ p \sigma, p ^{\prime} \sigma^{\prime}}( q ) \hat{n}_{ p \sigma}( q ) \hat{n}_{ p ^{\prime} \sigma^{\prime}}(- q ) \] where

  • \(\Lambda\) is a cut-off that restricts the momentum transfer to values smaller than the thickness of the shell of quasi-particles

The existence of zero-momentum limit $f_{ p ,}=f_{ p }( q =0) $ requires that the interaction has a finite range. however, the Coulomb interaction has the long-range which does not meet this requirement. Therefore, the charged Fermi liquids requires that we separate out the long-range part of the Coulomb interaction.

occupancies perturbation

Interactions mean that quasi-particle energies are sensitive to changes in the quasi-particle occupancies. Suppose the quasi-particle occupancies deviate from the ground state as follows: \(n_{ p \sigma} \rightarrow n_{ p \sigma}+\delta n_{ p \sigma}\). The corresponding change in the total energy is then \[ \begin{aligned} \frac{\delta \mathcal{E} }{\delta n_{ p \sigma}}&=\epsilon_{ p \sigma} \equiv E_{ p \sigma}-\mu\\ &=\epsilon_{ p \sigma}^{(0)}+\sum_{ p ^{\prime} \sigma^{\prime}} f_{ p \sigma, p ^{\prime}, \sigma^{\prime}} \delta n_{ p ^{\prime} \sigma^{\prime}} \end{aligned} \] The second term is the change in the quasi-particle energy induced by the polarization of the Fermi sea.

free energy

To determine thermodynamic properties of the Landau Fermi-liquid, we also need to know the entropy of the fluid. Fortunately, when we turn on interactions adiabatically the entropy is invariant, so that it must maintain the dependence on particle occupancies that it has in the non-interacting system, i.e. \[ S=-k_{B} \sum_{ p , \sigma}\left[n_{ p \sigma} \ln n_{ p \sigma}+\left(1-n_{ p \sigma}\right) \ln \left(1-n_{ p \sigma}\right)\right] \] The full thermodynamics are determined by the free energy \[ \begin{aligned} F&= \mathcal{E} -T S\\ &=E-\mu N-T S\\ &= \mathcal{E}_{0}(\mu)+\sum_{ p \sigma} \epsilon_{ p \sigma}^{(0)} \delta n_{ p \sigma}+\frac{1}{2} \sum_{ p , p ^{\prime}, \sigma, \sigma^{\prime}} f_{ p \sigma, p ^{\prime} \sigma^{\prime}} \delta n_{ p \sigma} \delta n_{ p ^{\prime} \sigma^{\prime}} \\ &\quad+k_{B} T \sum_{ p , \sigma}\left[n_{ p \sigma} \ln n_{ p \sigma}+\left(1-n_{ p \sigma}\right) \ln \left(1-n_{ p \sigma}\right)\right] \end{aligned} \]

  2. \(F_{l}^{ a}\) 反对称项，和磁性有关

The power of the Landau Fermi-liquid theory lies in its ability to parameterize the interactions in terms of a small number of multi-pole parameters called Landau parameters. These parameters describe how the original non-interacting Fermi-liquid theory is renormalized by the feedback effect of interactions on quasi-particle energies. \[ \begin{array}{lll} \text { Property } & \text { Non-interacting } & \text { Landau Fermi-liquid } \\ \hline \text { Fermi momentum } & p_{F} & \text { Unchanged } \\ \text { Density of particles } & 2 \frac{ v _{F S}}{(2 \pi)^{3}} & \text { Unchanged } \\ \text { Density of states } & N (0)=\frac{m p_{F}}{\pi^{2} \hbar^{3}} & N ^{*}(0)=\frac{m^{*} p_{F}}{\pi^{2} \hbar^{3}} \\ \text { Effective mass } & m & m^{*}=m\left(1+F_{1}^{s}\right) \\ \text { Specific heat coefficient } C_{V}=\gamma T & \gamma=\frac{\pi^{2}}{3} k_{B}^{2} N (0) & \gamma=\frac{\pi^{2}}{3} k_{B}^{2} N ^{*}(0) \\ \text { Spin susceptibility } & \chi_{s}=\mu_{F}^{2} N (0) & \chi_{s}=\mu_{F}^{2} \frac{ N ^{*}(0)}{1+F_{0}^{u}} \\ \text { Charge susceptibility } & \chi_{C}= N (0) & \chi_{C}=\frac{ N ^{*}(0)}{1+F_{0}^{s}} \\ \text { Collective modes } & - & \text { Sound }(\omega \tau<<1) \\ & & \text { Zero sound }(\omega \tau>>1) \end{array} \] Table summarizes the key properties of the Landau Fermi-liquid.

Fermi liquid, the physics is invariant under spatial rotations, so that interactions on the Fermi surface only depend on the relative angle \(\theta\) between \(\hat{ p }\) and \(\hat{ p }^{\prime} .\) We write \(\left(\cos \theta=\hat{p} \cdot \hat{p}^{\prime}\right)\) \[ f_{ p , p ^{\prime}}^{s, a}=f^{s, a}(\cos \theta) \] We convert the interaction to a dimensionless function by multiplying it by the quasiparticle density of states \(N^{*}(0)\) \[ F^{s, a}(\cos \theta)= N ^{*}(0) f^{s, a}(\cos \theta) \]

2.1 multipole expansion

These functions can now be expanded as a multipole expansion in terms of Legendre polynomials: \[ F^{s, a}(\cos \theta)=\sum_{l=0}^{\infty}(2 l+1) F_{l}^{s, a} P_{l}(\cos \theta) \] The coefficients \(F_{l}^{s}\) and \(F_{l}^{a}\) are the Landau parameters.

  • The spin-symmetric components \(F_{l}^{s}\) parameterize the non-magnetic part of the interaction
  • The spin-antisymmetric \(F_{l}^{a}\) define the magnetic component of the interaction.

These parameters determine how distortions of the Fermi surface are fed back to modify quasiparticle energies.

calculation the coefficients

The coefficients \(F_{l}^{s, a}\) can get by using the orthogonality relation \[ \frac{1}{2} \int_{-1}^{1} d c \quad P_{l}(c) P_{l^{\prime}}(c)=(2 l+1)^{-1} \delta_{l, l^{\prime}} \] thus \[ \begin{aligned} F_{l}^{s, a}&=\frac{1}{2} \int_{-1}^{1} d c F^{s, a}(c) P_{l}(c) \\ &\equiv\left\langle F^{s, a}(\hat{\Omega}) P_{l}(\hat{\Omega})\right\rangle_{\hat{\Omega}} \end{aligned} \] where

  • \(\langle\ldots\rangle_{\hat{\Omega}}\) denotes an average over solid angle.

It is useful to rewrite this angular average as an average over the Fermi surface. To do this we note that, since \(2 \sum_{ k } \delta\left(\epsilon_{ k }\right)= N ^{*}(0)\) the function \(\frac{2}{ N ^{*}(0)} \delta\left(\epsilon_{ k }\right)\) behaves as a normalized projector onto the Fermi surface, so that \[ \begin{aligned} F_{l}^{s, a}&=\left\langle F^{s, a}(\hat{\Omega}) P_{l}(\hat{\Omega})\right\rangle_{F S}\\ &=\frac{2}{ N ^{*}(0)} \sum_{ p ^{\prime}} F_{ p , p ^{\prime}}^{s, a} P_{l}\left(\cos \theta_{ p , p ^{\prime}}\right) \delta\left(\epsilon_{ p ^{\prime}}\right) \end{aligned} \] and since \(F_{ p , p ^{\prime}}^{s, a}= N ^{*}(0) f_{ p , p ^{\prime}}^{s, a}\) \[ F_{l}^{s, a}=2 \sum_{ p ^{\prime}} f_{ p , p ^{\prime}}^{s, a} P_{l}\left(\cos \theta_{ p , p ^{\prime}}\right) \delta\left(\epsilon_{ p ^{\prime}}\right) \] This form is very convenient for later calculations.

example

Question:

Use first-order perturbation theory to calculate the Landau interaction parameters for a fluid of fermions with a weak interaction described by \[ H=\sum_{ p \sigma} E_{ p } n_{ p \sigma}+\frac{\lambda}{2} \sum_{ p \sigma, p ^{\prime} \sigma^{\prime}, q } V(q) c_{ p - q \sigma}^{\dagger} c_{ p ^{\prime}+ q ^{\prime}}^{\dagger} c_{ p ^{\prime} \sigma^{\prime}} c_{ p \sigma} \] where \(E_{ p }\) is the energy of the non-interacting Fermi gas, \(V(q)=\int \frac{d^{3} q}{(2 \pi)^{3}} e^{-i q \cdot r } V(r)\) is the Fourier transform of the interaction potential \(V(r),\) and \(\lambda<<1\) is a very small coupling constant.

Hint:

use first-order perturbation theory in \(\lambda\) to compute the energy of a state \[ \Psi=\left|n_{ p _{1} \sigma_{1}}, n_{ p _{2} \sigma_{2}}, \ldots\right\rangle \] to leading order in the interaction strength \(\lambda,\) and then read off the terms quadratic in \(n_{ p \sigma}\).

Solution

To leading order in \(\lambda,\) the total energy is given by
\[ \begin{aligned} E&=\langle\Psi|H| \Psi\rangle\\ &=\sum_{ p \sigma} E_{ p } n_{ p \sigma}+\frac{\lambda}{2} \sum_{ p \sigma, p ^{\prime} \sigma^{\prime}, q } V(q)\left\langle\Psi\left|c_{ p - q \sigma}^{\dagger} c_{ p ^{\prime}+ q \sigma^{\prime}}^{\dagger} c_{ p ^{\prime} \sigma^{\prime}} c_{ p \sigma}\right| \Psi\right\rangle \end{aligned} \] The matrix element \(\left\langle\Psi\left|c_{ p - q \sigma}^{\dagger} c_{ p ^{\prime}+ q \sigma^{\prime}}^{\dagger} c_{ p ^{\prime} \sigma^{\prime}} c_{ p \sigma}\right| \Psi\right\rangle\) in the interaction term vanishes unless the two-quasiparticle state annihilated by the two destruction operators has an overlap with the two-particle state created by the two creation operators, i.e. \[ \begin{aligned} \left\langle\Psi\left|c_{ p - q \sigma}^{\dagger} c_{ p ^{\prime}+ q \sigma^{\prime}}^{\dagger} c_{ p ^{\prime} \sigma^{\prime}} c_{ p \sigma}\right| \Psi\right\rangle &=\left\langle p - q , \sigma ; p ^{\prime}+ q , \sigma^{\prime} \mid p , \sigma ; p ^{\prime} \sigma^{\prime}\right\rangle n_{ p \sigma} n_{ p \sigma^{\prime}} \\ &=\left(\delta_{ q =0}-\delta_{ p - q , p ^{\prime}} \delta_{\sigma, \sigma^{\prime}}\right) n_{ p \sigma} n_{ p \sigma^{\prime}} \end{aligned} \] where the second term occurs when the outgoing state is the exchange of the incoming two-quasiparticle state. Inserting we obtain \[ \begin{aligned} E&=\langle\Psi|H| \Psi\rangle\\ &=\sum_{ p \sigma} E_{ p } n_{ p \sigma}+\frac{\lambda}{2} \sum_{ p \sigma, p ^{\prime} \sigma^{\prime}, q } V(q)\left\langle\Psi\left|c_{ p - q \sigma}^{\dagger} c_{ p ^{\prime}+ q \sigma^{\prime}}^{\dagger} c_{ p ^{\prime} \sigma^{\prime}} c_{ p \sigma}\right| \Psi\right\rangle\\ &=\sum_{ p \sigma} E_{ p } n_{ p \sigma}+\frac{\lambda}{2} \sum_{ p \sigma, p ^{\prime} \sigma^{\prime}}\left[V(0)-V\left( p - p ^{\prime}\right) \delta_{\sigma \sigma^{\prime}}\right] n_{ p ^{\prime} \sigma^{\prime}} n_{ p \sigma} \end{aligned} \] enabling us to read off the Landau interaction as \[ f_{ p \sigma, p ^{\prime} \sigma^{\prime}}=\lambda\left[V(q=0)-V\left( p - p ^{\prime}\right) \delta_{\sigma \sigma^{\prime}}\right]+O\left(\lambda^{2}\right) \] It follows that the symmetric and antisymmetric parts of the interaction parameters are

  • \(f_{ p , p ^{\prime}}^{s}=\lambda\left[V(q=0)-\frac{1}{2} V\left( p - p ^{\prime}\right)\right]+O\left(\lambda^{2}\right)\)
  • \(f_{ p , p ^{\prime}}^{a}=-\frac{\lambda}{2} V\left( p - p ^{\prime}\right)+O\left(\lambda^{2}\right)\)

Note the following:

  • The Landau interaction is only well defined if \(V(q=0)\) is finite, which implies that the interaction is short-range.
  • The second term in the interaction corresponds to the "exchange" of identical particles. For a repulsive interaction, this gives rise to an attractive \(f^{a} .\) We can represent the interaction term by the Feynman diagrams

Equilibrium distribution of quasiparticles

Despite interactions, the Landau Fermi-liquid preserves the equilibrium Fermi-Dirac momentum distribution.

stationary

The key idea here is that, in thermal equilibrium, the free energy \(F\) is stationary with respect to small changes \(\delta n_{ p \sigma}\) in quasiparticle occupancies, so that \[ \delta F=\sum_{ p \sigma} \delta n_{ p \sigma}\left[\epsilon_{ p \sigma}+k_{B} T \ln \left(\frac{n_{ p \sigma}}{1-n_{ p \sigma}}\right)\right]+O\left(\delta n_{ p \sigma}^{2}\right)=0 \] Stationarity of the free energy, \(\delta F=0,\) enforces the thermodynamic identity \(\delta F=\delta E -\) \(T \delta S=0,\) or \(d E =T d S .\) This requires that the linear coefficient of \(\delta n_{ p \sigma}\) is zero which implies that the quasiparticle occupancy \[ n_{ p \sigma}=\frac{1}{e^{\beta \epsilon_{ p \sigma}+1}}=f\left(\epsilon_{ p \sigma}\right) \] is determined by the Fermi-Dirac distribution function of its energy. There is a subtlety here, however, for the quantity \(\epsilon_{ p \sigma}\) contains the feedback effect of interactions, as given in \[ \epsilon_{ p \sigma}=\epsilon_{ p \sigma}^{(0)}+\sum_{ p ^{\prime} \sigma^{\prime}} f_{ p \sigma, p ^{\prime}, \sigma^{\prime}} \delta n_{ p ^{\prime} \sigma^{\prime}} \]

low-temperature behavior

Let us first consider the low-temperature behavior in the absence of a field.

In this case, as the temperature is lowered the density of thermally excited quasiparticles will go to zero, and in this limit the quasiparticle distribution function is asymptotically given by \[ n_{ p \sigma}=f\left(\epsilon_{ p }^{(0)}\right) \] In the ground state this becomes a step function \[ \left.n_{ p \sigma}\right|_{T=0}=\theta\left(-\epsilon_{ p }^{(0)}\right)=\theta\left(\mu-E_{ p }^{(0)}\right), \] To obtain the specific heat, we must calculate \(C_{V} d T=d E =\sum_{ p } \epsilon_{ p \sigma}^{(0)} \delta n_{ p \sigma} .\) At low temperatures, \(\delta n_{ p \sigma}=\frac{\partial f\left(\epsilon_{ p }^{(0)}\right)}{\partial T} d T,\) so that \[ C_{V}=\sum_{ p \sigma} \epsilon_{ p \sigma}^{(0)}\left(\frac{\partial f\left(\epsilon_{ p \sigma}^{(0)}\right)}{\partial T}\right) \rightarrow N ^{*}(0) \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} d \epsilon \epsilon\left(\frac{\partial f(\epsilon)}{\partial T}\right) \] where

  • the summation is replaced by an integral over the density of states near the Fermi surface.

Apart from the renormalization of the energies leading to \[ C_{V}=\gamma T, \quad \gamma=\frac{\pi^{2} k_{B}^{2}}{3} N ^{*}(0) \]

3 Feedback effects of interactions

准粒子会对费米球造成形变，这些形变可以通过朗道相互作用对准粒子的进行反馈。

  • 如果是正反馈，那就是不稳定的，不可持续的变换。
  • 如果是负反馈，费米面会在平衡状态附近来回振荡变换。

对于负反馈，果也是因，就形成了自洽方程。

One can visualize the Landau Fermi-liquid as a deformable sphere, like a large water droplet in zero gravity. The Fermi sphere changes shape when the density or magnetization of the fluid is modified, or if a current flows. These deformations act back on the quasiparticles via the Landau interactions, to change the quasiparticle energies. These feedback effects are a generalization of the idea of a Weiss field in magnetism.

When the feedback is positive, it can lead to instabilities, such as the development of magnetism. A Fermi surface can also oscillate collectively about its equilibrium shape. In a conventional gas, density oscillations cannot take place without collisions. In a Landau Fermi-liquid, we will see that the interactions play a non-trivial role that gives rise to collision-less collective oscillations of the Fermi surface, called zero sound (literally zero-collision sound), that are absent in the free Fermi gas.

3.1 feedback effects by change external field

To examine the feedback effects of interactions, let us suppose an external potential or field is applied to induce a polarization of the Fermi surface.

external field & conserved quantity

There are various kinds of external field we can consider.

  1. A simple change in the chemical potential

\[ \delta \epsilon_{ p \sigma}^{0}=-\delta \mu \]

will induce an isotropic enlargement of the Fermi surface.

  1. The application of a magnetic field

\[ \delta \epsilon_{ p \sigma}^{0}=-\sigma \mu_{F} B \]

will induce a spin polarization.

  1. We can also consider the application of a vector potential which couples to the quasiparticle current

\[ \delta \epsilon_{ p \sigma}^{0}=- A \cdot \frac{e p }{m} \]

in a translationally invariant system.

Notice how, in each case, the applied field couples to a conserved quantity (the particle number, the spin, or the current), which is unchanged by interactions. This means that the energy associated with the application of the external field is unchanged by interactions for any quasiparticle configuration \(\left\{n_{ p \sigma}\right\},\) which guarantees that the coupling to the external field is identical to that for non-interacting particles. This is the reason for the appearance of the unrenormalized mass in \(\delta \epsilon_{ p \sigma}^{0}=- A \cdot \frac{e p }{m}\). For each of these cases, there will of course be a feedback effect of the interactions that we now calculate.

From $ { p } ={ p }^{(0)}+{ p ^{} ^{}} f{ p , p ^{}, ^{}} n_{ p ^{} ^{}}$ the change in the quasiparticle energy will now contain two terms

  • one due to direct coupling to the external field
  • the other derived from the induced polarization \(\delta n_{ p \sigma}\) of the Fermi surface:

\[ \delta \epsilon_{ p \sigma}=\delta \epsilon_{ p \sigma}^{(0)}+\sum_{ p ^{\prime} \sigma^{\prime}} f_{ p \sigma p ^{\prime} \sigma^{\prime}} \delta n_{ p ^{\prime} \sigma^{\prime}} \]

In this case, the equilibrium quasiparticle occupancies become \[ n_{ p \sigma}=f\left(\epsilon_{ p }^{(0)}+\delta \epsilon_{ p \sigma}\right)=f\left(\epsilon_{ p }^{(0)}\right)+f^{\prime}\left(\epsilon_{ p }^{(0)}\right) \delta \epsilon_{ p \sigma} \] As the temperature is lowered to zero, the derivative of the Fermi function evolves into a delta function, \(-f^{\prime}(\epsilon) \sim \delta(\epsilon),\) so that the quasiparticle occupancy is given by \[ n_{ p \sigma}=\theta\left(-\epsilon_{ p }^{(0)}\right)+\left[-\delta\left(\epsilon_{ p }^{(0)}\right) \delta \epsilon_{ p \sigma}\right] \] where

  • \(n_{ p \sigma}^{(0)}=\theta\left(-\epsilon_{ p }^{(0)}\right)\)
  • \(\delta n_{ p \sigma}=\left[-\delta\left(\epsilon_{ p }^{(0)}\right) \delta \epsilon_{ p \sigma}\right]\)

\(\delta n_{ p \sigma}=-\delta\left(\epsilon_{ p }^{(0)}\right) \delta \epsilon_{ p \sigma}\) represents the polarization of the Fermi surface, which will feed back into the interaction \(\delta \epsilon_{ p \sigma}=\delta \epsilon_{ p \sigma}^{(0)}+\sum_{ p ^{\prime} \sigma^{\prime}} f_{ p \sigma p ^{\prime} \sigma^{\prime}} \delta n_{ p ^{\prime} \sigma^{\prime}}\) as follows:

The resulting shift in the quasiparticle energies must then satisfy the self-consistency relation: \[ \delta \epsilon_{ p \sigma}=\delta \epsilon_{ p \sigma}^{(0)}-\sum_{ p ^{\prime} \sigma^{\prime}} f_{ p \sigma p ^{\prime} \sigma^{\prime}} \delta\left(\epsilon_{ p ^{\prime}}^{(0)}\right) \delta \epsilon_{ p ^{\prime} \sigma} \] This feedback process preserves the symmetry of the external perturbation, but its strength in a given symmetry channel depends on the corresponding Landau paramater.

  • Thus, isotropic charge and spin polarization of the Fermi surface, shown in Figure \(( a )\) and Figure \(( b ),\) are fed back via the isotropic charge and magnetic Landau parameters \(F_{0}^{s}\) and \(F_{0}^{a}\).
  • When the quasiparticle fluid is set into motion at velocity \(\vec{u},\) this induces a dipolar polarization of the Fermi surface, shown in Figure \(( c ),\) which is fed back via the dipolar Landau parameter \(F_{1}^{s}\). This process is responsible for the renormalization of the effective mass.

multipole symmetry

Consider a change in the quasiparticle potential that has a particular multipole symmetry, so that the "bare" change in quasiparticle energy is \[ \delta \epsilon_{ p \sigma}^{(0)}=v_{l} Y_{l m}(\hat{ p }) \] where

  • \(Y_{l m}\) is a spherical harmonic.

The renormalized response of the quasiparticle energy given by \(\delta \epsilon_{ p \sigma}=\delta \epsilon_{ p \sigma}^{(0)}-\sum_{ p ^{\prime} \sigma^{\prime}} f_{ p \sigma p ^{\prime} \sigma^{\prime}} \delta\left(\epsilon_{ p ^{\prime}}^{(0)}\right) \delta \epsilon_{ p ^{\prime} \sigma}\) must have the same symmetry, but will have a different magnitude \(t_{l}\) : \[ \delta \epsilon_{ p \sigma}=t_{l} Y_{l m}(\hat{ p }) \] When this is fed back through the interaction, according to \(\delta \epsilon_{ p \sigma}=\delta \epsilon_{ p \sigma}^{(0)}-\sum_{ p ^{\prime} \sigma^{\prime}} f_{ p \sigma p ^{\prime} \sigma^{\prime}} \delta\left(\epsilon_{ p ^{\prime}}^{(0)}\right) \delta \epsilon_{ p ^{\prime} \sigma}\) it produces an additional shift in the quasiparticle energy given by \(\sum_{ p ^{\prime} \sigma^{\prime}} f_{ p \sigma, p ^{\prime} \sigma^{\prime}} \delta n_{ p ^{\prime} \sigma^{\prime}}=-F_{l}^{s} t_{l} Y_{l m}(\hat{ p })\) (see Example), so that the total change in the energy is given by \(\delta \epsilon_{ p \sigma}=\left(v_{l}-F_{l}^{s} t_{l}\right) Y_{l m}(\hat{ p })\). Comparing this result with \(\delta \epsilon_{ p \sigma}=t_{l} Y_{l m}(\hat{ p })\) we see that \[ t_{l}=\left(v_{l}-F_{l}^{s} t_{l}\right) \] This is the symmetry-resolved version of \(\delta \epsilon_{ p \sigma}=\delta \epsilon_{ p \sigma}^{(0)}-\sum_{ p ^{\prime} \sigma^{\prime}} f_{ p \sigma p ^{\prime} \sigma^{\prime}} \delta\left(\epsilon_{ p ^{\prime}}^{(0)}\right) \delta \epsilon_{ p ^{\prime} \sigma}\). Consequently \[ t_{l}=\frac{v_{l}}{1+F_{l}^{s}} \] We may interpret \(t_{l}\) as the scattering t-matrix associated with the potential \(v_{l}\).

  1. If \(F_{l}^{s}>0,\) corresponding to a repulsive interaction, negative feedback occurs which causes the response to be suppressed. This is normally the case in the isotropic channel, where repulsive interactions tend to suppress the polarizability of the Fermi surface.
  2. By contrast, if \(F_{l}^{s}<0\), corresponding to an attractive interaction, positive feedback enhances the response.
  3. Indeed, if \(F_{l}^{S}\) drops down to the critical value \(F_{l}^{s}=-1,\) an instability will occur and the Landau Fermi surface becomes unstable to a deformation, a process called a Pomeranchuk instability.

spin polarization

A similar calculation can be carried out for a spin polarization of the Fermi surface, where the shifts in the quasiparticle energies are \[ \delta \epsilon_{ p \sigma}^{(0)}=\sigma v_{l}^{a} Y_{l m}(\hat{ p }), \quad \delta \epsilon_{ p \sigma}=\sigma t_{l}^{a} Y_{l m}(\hat{ p }) \] Now the spin-dependent polarization of the Fermi surface feeds back via the spindependent Landau parameters, so that \[ t_{l}^{a}=\frac{v_{l}^{a}}{1+F_{l}^{a}} \] The isotropic response \((l=0)\) corresponds to a simple spin polarization of the Fermi surface. If spin interactions grow to the point where \(F_{0}^{a}=-1,\) the Fermi surface becomes unstable to the formation of a spontaneous spin polarization. This is called a Stoner instability, and results in ferromagnetism.

3.2 Renormalization of paramagnetism and compressibility

The simplest polarization response functions of a Landau Fermi-liquid are its "charge" and spin susceptibility. \[ \chi_{c}=\frac{1}{V} \frac{\partial N}{\partial \mu}, \quad \chi_{s}=\frac{1}{V} \frac{\partial M}{\partial B} \] where

  • \(V\) is the volume.

Here, we use the term "charge" density to refer to the density response function of the neutral Fermi liquid. These responses involve an isotropic polarization of the Fermi surface. In a neutral fluid, the bulk modulus \(\kappa=-V \frac{d P}{d V}\) is directly related to the charge susceptibility per unit volume, \(\kappa=\frac{n^{2}}{\chi_{c}},\) where \(n=N / V\) is the particle density. Thus a smaller charge susceptibility implies a stiffer fluid. \({ }^{4}\)

When we apply a chemical potential or a magnetic field, the "bare" quasiparticle energies respond isotropically: \[ \delta \epsilon_{ p \sigma}^{(0)}=\delta E_{ p \sigma}^{(0)}-\delta \mu=-\sigma \mu_{ F } B-\delta \mu \] Feedback via the interactions renormalizes the response of the full quasiparticle energy: \[ \delta \epsilon_{ p \sigma}=-\sigma \lambda_{s} \mu_{ F } B-\lambda_{c} \delta \mu \] since these are isotropic responses, the feedback is transmitted through the \(l=0\) Landau parameters, \[ \begin{aligned} \lambda_{s} &=\frac{1}{1+F_{0}^{a}} \\ \lambda_{c} &=\frac{1}{1+F_{0}^{s}} \end{aligned} \] When we apply a pure chemical potential shift, the resulting change in quasiparticle number is \(\delta N=\lambda_{c} N ^{*}(0) \delta \mu,\) so the charge susceptibility is given by \[ \chi_{c}=\lambda_{c} N^{*}(0)=\frac{ N ^{*}(0)}{1+F_{0}^{s}} \] Typically, repulsive interactions cause \(F_{0}^{s}>0,\) reducing the charge susceptibility, making the fluid "stiffer." In \({ }^{3} He , F_{0}^{s}=10.8\) at low pressures, which is roughly ten times stiffer than expected, based on its density of states.

A reverse phenomenon occurs in the spin response of Landau Fermi-liquids. In a magnetic field, the change in the number of up and down quasiparticles is \(\delta n_{\uparrow}=\) \(-\delta n_{\downarrow}=\frac{1}{2} N ^{*}(0) \mu_{F} B .\) The resulting change in magnetization is \(\delta M=\mu_{F}\left(\delta n_{\uparrow}-\delta n_{\downarrow}\right)=\) \(\lambda_{s} \mu_{F}^{2} N ^{*}(0) B,\) so the spin susceptibility is \[ \chi_{s}=\lambda_{s} \mu_{F}^{2} N^{*}(0)=\frac{\mu_{ F }^{2} N ^{*}(0)}{1+F_{0}^{a}} \] There are a number of interesting points to be made here:

  • The Wilson ratio, defined as the ratio of \(\chi_{s} / \gamma\) in the interacting system to that in the non-interacting system, is given by

\[ W=\frac{\left(\frac{\chi}{\gamma}\right)}{\left(\frac{\chi}{\gamma}\right)_{0}}=\frac{1}{1+F_{0}^{a}} \]

In the context of ferromagnetism, this quantity is often referred to as the Stoner enhancement factor. In Landau Fermi-liquids with strong ferromagnetic exchange interactions between fermions, \(F_{0}^{a}\) is negative, enhancing the Pauli susceptibility. This is the origin of the enhancement of the Pauli susceptibility in liquid \({ }^{3} He ,\) where \(W \sim 3 .\) In palladium metal \(( Pd ), W=10\) is even more substantially enhanced [8]

  • When a Landau Fermi-liquid is tuned to the point where \(F_{0}^{a} \rightarrow-1, \chi \rightarrow \infty,\) leading to a ferromagnetic instability, called a Stoner instability. This is an example of a ferromagnetic quantum critical point, a point where quantum zero-point fluctuations of the magnetization develop infinite-range correlations in space and time. At such a point, the Wilson ratio will diverge.

3.3 Mass renormalization

Using this formulation of the interacting Fermi gas, Landau was able to link the renormalization of quasiparticle mass to the dipole component of the interactions \(F_{1}^{s}\). As the fermion moves through the medium, the backflow of the surrounding fluid enhances its effective mass according to the relation \[ m^{*}=m\left(1+F_{1}^{s}\right) \] Another way to understand quasiparticle mass renormalization is to consider the current carried by a quasiparticle. Whether we are dealing with neutral or physically charged quasiparticles, the total number of particles is conserved and we can ascribe a particle current \(v _{F}= p _{F} / m^{*}\) to each quasiparticle. We can rewrite this current in the form \[ v _{F}=\frac{ p _{F}}{m^{*}}=\frac{ p _{F}}{m} -\frac{ p _{F}}{m}\left(\frac{F_{1}^{s}}{1+F_{1}^{s}}\right) \] where

  • \(\frac{ p _{F}}{m}\) is the bare current associated with the original particle
  • \(\frac{ p _{F}}{m}\left(\frac{F_{1}^{s}}{1+F_{1}^{s}}\right)\) is backflow of the surrounding Fermi sea

Mass renormalization increases the density of states from \(N(0)=\frac{m p_{F}}{\pi^{2}} \rightarrow N ^{*}(0)=\) \(\frac{m^{*} p_{F}}{\pi^{2}},\) i.e. it has the effect of compressing the spacing between the fermion energy levels, which increases the number of quasiparticles that are excited at a given temperature by a factor \(m^{*} / m:\) this enhances the linear specific heat: \[ C_{V}^{*}=\frac{m^{*}}{m} C_{V} \] where

  • \(C_{V}\) is the Sommerfeld value for the specific heat capacity.

Experimentally, the specific heat of \({ }^{3} He\) is enhanced by a factor of \(2.8,\) from which we know that \(m^{*} \approx 3 m\). Landau's original derivation depends on the use of Galilean invariance. Here we use an equivalent derivation, based on the observation that backflow is a feedback response to the dipolar distortion of the Fermi surface which develops in the presence of a current. This enables us to calculate the mass renormalization in an analogous fashion to the renormalization of the spin susceptibility and compressibility, except that now we must introduce the conjugate field to current - that is, a vector potential. To this end, we imagine that each quasiparticle carries a conserved charge \(q=1\) and that the flow of quasiparticles is coupled to a fictitious vector potential \(q A \equiv A _{N} .\) The microscopic Hamiltonian in the presence of the vector potential is then given by \[ H\left[ A _{N}\right]=\sum_{\sigma} \int d^{3} x \frac{1}{2 m} \psi_{\sigma}^{\dagger}(x)\left[\left(-i \hbar \nabla- A _{N}\right)^{2}\right] \psi_{\sigma}(x)+\hat{V} \] where

  • \(\hat{V}\) contains the translationally invariant interactions.

Notice that the effect of \(A _{N}\) is to change the momentum of each particle by \(- A _{N},\) so that \(H\left[ A _{N}\right]\) corresponds to the Hamiltonian transformed into a Galilean reference frame moving at speed \(u = A _{N} / m\). Landau's original derivation used the Galilean equivalence of the Fermi liquid to compute the mass renormalization. since the vector potential \(A _{N}\) is coupled to a conserved quantity, the momentum, we can treat it in the same way as a chemical potential or magnetic field. The linear term in \(A _{N}\) in the total energy is \(\delta \hat{H}=- A _{N} \cdot \frac{\hat{ p }}{m},\) where \(\hat{ P }\) is the conserved total momentum operator. For a non-interacting system the change in the total energy for a small vector potential at fixed particle occupancies \(n_{ p \sigma}\) is \[ \delta E=\langle\delta H\rangle=-\frac{\langle P \rangle}{m} \cdot A _{N}=-\sum_{ p \sigma}\left(\frac{ p }{m} \cdot A _{N}\right) n_{ p \sigma} \] Provided the momentum is conserved, this is also the change in the energy of the interacting Fermi liquid, at fixed quasiparticle occupancy, i.e. without backflow. In this way, we see that turning on the vector potential changes \[ \epsilon_{ p \sigma}^{(0)} \rightarrow \epsilon_{ p \sigma}^{(0)}+\delta \epsilon_{ p \sigma}^{(0)} \] where \[ \delta \epsilon_{ p \sigma}^{(0)}=-\frac{ p }{m} \cdot A _{N}=-A_{N} \frac{p_{F}}{m} \cos \theta \] Here

  • \(\theta\) is the angle between the vector potential and the quasiparticle momentum.

Thus the vector potential introduces a dipolar potential around the Fermi surface. Notice how the conservation of momentum guarantees that it is the bare mass \(m\) that enters into \(\delta \epsilon_{ p \sigma}^{(0)}\) Now when we take account of the feedback effect caused by the redistribution of quasiparticles in response to this potential, the quasiparticle energy becomes \(E_{ p -q A }=\frac{\left( p - A _{N}\right)^{2}}{2 m^{*}}\) Here, the replacement of \(p \rightarrow p -q A _{N}= p - A _{N}\) is guaranteed because the quasiparticle carries the same conserved charge \(q=1\) as the original particles. In this way, we see that, in the presence of backflow, the change in quasiparticle energy \[ \delta \epsilon_{ p \sigma}=-\frac{ p }{m^{*}} \cdot A _{N}=-A_{N} \frac{p_{F}}{m^{*}} \cos \theta \] involves the renormalized mass \(m^{*}\). since the vector potential induces a dipolar perturbation to the Fermi surface, we conclude that backflow feedback effects involve the spin-symmetric \(l=1\) Landau parameter \(F_{1}^{s}\) \[ \delta \epsilon_{ p \sigma}=\left(\frac{1}{1+F_{1}^{s}}\right) \delta \epsilon_{ p \sigma}^{(0)} \] Inserting \(\delta \epsilon_{ p \sigma}^{(0)}=-A_{N} \frac{p_{F}}{m} \cos \theta\) and \(\delta \epsilon_{ p \sigma} =-A_{N} \frac{p_{F}}{m^{*}} \cos \theta\) into this relation, we obtain \[ \frac{m}{m^{*}}=\frac{1}{1+F_{1}^{s}} \] or \(m^{*}=m\left(1+F_{1}^{s}\right)\)

Note the following:

  • The Landau mass renormalization formula relies on the conservation of particle current when the interactions are adiabatically turned on. In a crystal lattice, although crystal momentum is still conserved, particle current is not conserved and at present there is no known way of writing down an expression for \(\delta \epsilon_{ p \sigma}^{(0)}\) and \(\delta \epsilon_{ p \sigma}\) in terms of crystal momentum that would permit the derivation of a mass renormalization formula for electrons in a crystal.
  • since \(F_{1}^{s}= N ^{*}(0) f_{1}^{s}\) involves the renormalized density of states \(N ^{*}(0)=\frac{m^{*} p_{F}}{\pi^{2}},\) the renormalized mass \(m^{*}\) actually appears on both sides of \((6.83) .\) If we use (6.39) to rewrite \(F_{1}^{s}=\frac{m^{*}}{m} N(0) f_{1}^{s},\) where \(N(0)=\frac{m p_{F}}{\pi^{2}}\) is the unrenormalized density of states, we can then solve for \(m^{*}\) in terms of \(m\) to obtain

\[ m^{*}=\frac{m}{1-N(0) f_{1}^{s}} \]

This expression predicts that \(m^{*} \rightarrow \infty\) at \(N(0) f_{1}^{s}=1,\) i.e. that the quasiparticle density of states and hence the specific heat coefficient will diverge if the interactions become too strong. This possibility was first anticipated by Neville Mott, who predicted that, in presence of large interactions, fermions will localize, a phenomonon now called a Mott transition.

There are numerous examples of heavy-electron systems which lie close to such a localization transition, in which \(m_{e}^{*} / m_{e}>>1\). Quasiparticle masses in excess of \(1000 m _{e}\) have been observed via specific heat measurements. In practice, the transition where the mass diverges is usually associated with the development of some other sort of order, such as antiferromagnetism or solidification. since the phase transition occurs at zero temperature in the absence of thermal fluctuations, it is an example of a quantum phase transition. Such mass divergences have been observed in a variety of different contexts in charged electron systems, but they have also been observed as a second-order quantum phase transition, at the Mott localization transition which accompanies the solidification of two-dimensional liquid ${ }^{3} He $

4 Collective modes

The most common collective mode of a fluid or a gas is sound. Conventional sound results from collisions among particles which redistribute momentum within the fluid. As such, sound is a low-frequency phenomenon that operates at frequencies much smaller than the typical quasiparticule scattering rate \(\tau^{-1},\) i.e. \(\omega<<\tau^{-1}\) or \(\omega \tau<<1 .\) One of the startling predictions of Landau Fermi-liquid theory is the existence of a collionless collective mode that operates at high frequencies, \(\omega \tau>>1\) : zero sound. Zero sound is associated with collective oscillations of the Fermi surface and does not involve collisions. Whereas conventional sound travels at a speed below the Fermi velocity, zero sound is "supersonic," traveling at speeds in excess of the Fermi velocity. Historically, the observation of zero sound in liquid \({ }^{3}\) He clinched Landau Fermi-liquid theory, firmly establishing it as a foundation of fermionic many-body physics.

Let us now contrast zero sound and "first sound." Conventional sound is associated with oscillations in the density of a fluid, and hydrodynamics tells us that \[ u_{1}^{2}=\frac{\kappa}{\rho}=\frac{\kappa}{m n} \] where \(\rho=m n\) is the density of the fluid and \(\kappa=-V \frac{\partial P}{\partial V}\) is the bulk modulus. From our previous discussion, \(\kappa=\frac{n^{2}}{\chi_{c}}\) and \(\chi_{c}= N ^{*}(0) /\left(1+F_{0}^{s}\right),\) so the velocity of first sound in a Fermi liquid is given by \[ u_{1}^{2}=\frac{n}{m \chi_{c}}=\frac{n}{m N ^{*}(0)}\left(1+F_{0}^{s}\right) \] Replacing \(n=\frac{p_{F}^{2}}{3 \pi^{2}}, N ^{*}(0)=\frac{m^{*} p_{F}}{\pi^{2}},\) and \(m=m^{*} /\left(1+F_{1}^{s}\right)\) we obtain \[ u_{1}^{2}=\frac{v_{F}^{2}}{3}\left(1+F_{0}^{s}\right)\left(1+F_{1}^{s}\right) \] In the non-interacting limit, \(u_{1}=v_{F} / \sqrt{3}\) is smaller than the Fermi velocity. To understand zero sound we need to consider variations in the quasiparticle distribution function \(n_{ p }( x , t) .\) Provided that the characteristic frequency \(\omega\) is much smaller than the Fermi energy, \(\omega<<\epsilon_{F},\) and the characteristic wavevector is much smaller than the Fermi wavevector, \(q<<k_{F},\) fluctuations in the quasiparticle occupancy can be treated semiclassically, which leads to a Boltzmann equation, \[ \frac{D n_{ p \sigma}}{D t}=I\left[\left\{n_{ p \sigma}\right\}\right] \] where \[ \frac{D n_{ p \sigma}}{D t}=\frac{\partial n_{ p \sigma}}{\partial t}+\dot{ x } \cdot \nabla_{ x } n_{ p \sigma}+\dot{ p } \cdot \nabla_{ p } n_{ p \sigma} \] is the total rate of change of the quasiparticle occupancy \(n_{ p \sigma}( x , t),\) taking into account the movement of quasiparticles through phase space. \(I\) is the collision rate. In a semiclassical treatment, the rate of change of momentum and position are determined from Hamilton's equations, \(\dot{ p }=-\nabla_{ x } \epsilon_{ p }\) and \(\dot{ x }=\nabla_{ p } \epsilon_{ p },\) so that \[ \frac{D n_{ p \sigma}}{D t}=\frac{\partial n_{ p \sigma}}{\partial t}+\nabla_{ p } \epsilon_{ p } \cdot \nabla_{ x } n_{ p \sigma}-\nabla_{ x } \epsilon_{ p \sigma} \cdot \nabla_{ p } n_{ p \sigma} \] We now consider small fluctuations of the Fermi surface defined by \[ n_{ p }( x , t)=f\left(\epsilon_{ p }^{(0)}\right)+e^{i q \cdot x -i \omega t} \alpha_{ p \sigma} \] where \(\alpha_{ p \sigma}\) is the amplitude of the fluctuations. The terms contributing to the total rate of change \(D n_{ p \sigma} / D t\) are of order \(O(\omega \delta n),\) whereas the collision term \(I[n] \sim O\left(\tau^{-1} \delta n\right)\) is of the order of the collision rate \(\tau^{-1}\). In the high-frequency limit, \(\omega \tau>>1,\) the collision terms can then be neglected, leading to the collisionless Boltzmann equation: \[ \frac{\partial n_{ p \sigma}}{\partial t}+\nabla_{ p } \epsilon_{ p } \cdot \nabla_{ x } n_{ p \sigma}-\nabla_{ x } \epsilon_{ p \sigma} \cdot \nabla_{ p } n_{ p \sigma}=0 \] For small periodic oscillations in the Fermi surface, the first two terms can be written \[ \frac{\partial n_{ p \sigma}}{\partial t}+\nabla_{ p } \epsilon_{ p } \cdot \nabla_{ x } n_{ p \sigma}=-i\left(\omega- v _{F} \cdot q \right) \alpha_{ p \sigma} e^{i q \cdot x -i \omega t} \] the last term, the position dependence of the quasiparticle energies derives from interactions \[ \begin{aligned} \nabla_{x} \epsilon_{ p \sigma} &=\sum_{\sigma^{\prime}} \int_{ p ^{\prime}} f_{ p \sigma, p ^{\prime} \sigma^{\prime}} \nabla_{x} n_{ p ^{\prime}, \sigma^{\prime}} \\ &=i q e^{i q \cdot x -i \omega t} \sum_{\sigma^{\prime}} \int_{ p ^{\prime}} f_{ p \sigma, p ^{\prime} \sigma^{\prime}} \alpha_{ p ^{\prime}, \sigma^{\prime}} \end{aligned} \] Replacing \(\nabla_{ p } n_{ p \sigma}=\frac{\partial f}{\partial \epsilon} v _{F},\) the collisionless Boltzmann equation becomes \[ \left(\omega- v _{F} \cdot q \right) \alpha_{ p \sigma}+v_{F} \cdot q \left(-\frac{d f}{d \epsilon}\right) \sum_{\sigma^{\prime}} \int_{ p ^{\prime}} f_{ p \sigma, p ^{\prime} \sigma^{\prime}} \alpha_{ p ^{\prime}, \sigma^{\prime}}=0 \] For a mode propagating at speed \(u , \omega=u q .\) If we express \(v _{F} \cdot q =v_{F} q \cos \theta_{ p },\) and write the mode velocity as a factor \(s\) times the Fermi velocity, \(u=s v_{F},\) then this becomes \[ \left(s-\cos \theta_{ p }\right) \alpha_{ p \sigma}+\cos \theta_{ p }\left(-\frac{d f}{d \epsilon}\right) \sum_{\sigma^{\prime}} \int_{ p ^{\prime}} f_{ p \sigma, p ^{\prime} \sigma^{\prime}} \alpha_{ p ^{\prime}, \sigma^{\prime}}=0 \] We see that the fluctuations in occupancy associated with a zero sound mode, \(\alpha_{ p \sigma}=\) \(\eta_{\sigma}(\hat{ p })\left(-\frac{d f}{d \epsilon}\right),\) are proportional to the energy derivative of the Fermi function, and thus confined to within an energy scale \(T\) of the Fermi surface. The function \(\eta_{\sigma}(\hat{ p })\) describes the distribution around the Fermi surface, and this function satisfies the self-consistent relation \[ \eta_{\sigma}( p )=\frac{\cos \theta_{ p }}{2\left(s-\cos \theta_{ p }\right)} \sum_{\sigma^{\prime}} \int \frac{d \Omega_{ p ^{\prime}}}{4 \pi} F_{ p \sigma, p ^{\prime} \sigma^{\prime} \eta_{\sigma}\left(\hat{ p }^{\prime}\right)} \] For spin-independent zero sound waves, the right-hand side only involves \(F^{s}\) and can be written \[ \eta( p )=\frac{\cos \theta_{ p }}{\left(s-\cos \theta_{ p }\right)} \int \frac{d \Omega_{ p ^{\prime}}}{4 \pi} F_{ p , p ^{\prime}}^{s} \eta\left(\hat{ p }^{\prime}\right) \] To illustrate the solution of this equation, consider the case where the interaction is entirely isotropic and spin-independent, so that the only non-vanishing Landau parameter is \(F_{0}^{s}\). In this case, the angular function is spin-independent and given by \[ \eta(\theta)=A \frac{\cos (\theta)}{s-\cos (\theta)} \] where \(A\) is a constant. Substituting this form into the integral equation, we obtain the following formula for \(s=u / v_{F}\) \[ A=\int_{-1}^{1} \frac{d \cos \theta}{2} \frac{\cos \theta}{s-\cos \theta} A F_{0}^{s}=A F_{0}^{s}\left[-1+\frac{s}{2} \ln \left(\frac{s+1}{s-1}\right)\right] \] so that \[ \frac{s}{2} \ln \left(\frac{s+1}{s-1}\right)-1=\frac{1}{F_{0}^{s}} \] For large \(s,\) the function on the left-hand side vanishes asymptotically as \(1 /\left(3 s^{2}\right),\) and since the right-hand side vanishes at large interaction \(F_{0}^{s},\) it follows that for large interaction strength the zero sound velocity is much greater than \(v_{F}:\) \[ u=s v_{F}=v_{F} \sqrt{\frac{F_{0}^{s}}{3}} \quad\left(F_{0}^{s}>>1\right) \] For small interaction strength, \(s \rightarrow 1\) and the zero sound velocity approaches the Fermi velocity.

Experimentally, zero sound has been observed by a variety of methods. Low-frequency zero sound couples directly to vibrations at the wall of the fluid, and can be detected directly as a propagating density mode. Zero sound can also be probed at higher frequencies using neutron and \(X\) -ray scattering. Neutron scattering experiments find that, at high frequencies, the zero sound mode re-enters the particle-hole continuum where, as a damped excitation, it acquires a roton minimum similar to collective modes in bosonic \({ }^{4}\) He.
