BdG Equations in a Tight-Bind Model

[TOC]

The tight-binding model, from which the BdG equations are derived, has the origin from such strongly correlated models as the \(t-J\) model for hightemperature cuprates.

The derived BdG equations in the tight-binding model have been used for

  • unconventional superconductors with narrow electron band
  • \(s\) -wave superconductors

The calculated quantities range from the local density states to the superfluid density

Hamiltonian

mean-field Hamiltonian

The effective mean-field Hamiltonian with a singlet pairing symmetry is now given by: \[ H _{e f f}=\sum_{i j, \sigma \sigma^{\prime}} c_{i \sigma}^{\dagger} \tilde{h}_{i \sigma, j \sigma^{\prime}} c_{j \sigma^{\prime}}+\sum_{i j}\left[\Delta_{i j} c_{i \uparrow}^{\dagger} c_{j \downarrow}^{\dagger}+\Delta_{i j}^{*} c_{j \downarrow} c_{i \uparrow}\right]+E_{c o n s t} \] Where

  • the effective single-particle Hamiltonian

\[ \tilde{h}_{i \sigma, j \sigma^{\prime}}=h_{i \sigma, j \sigma^{\prime}}-\left(\mu+\frac{U}{2}-U\left\langle n_{i \bar{\sigma}}\right\rangle\right) \delta_{i j} \delta_{\sigma \sigma^{\prime}} \]

  • the singlet-pairing potential

\[ \begin{aligned} \Delta_{i j} &=\frac{V}{2}\left(\left\langle c_{i \uparrow} c_{j \downarrow}\right\rangle-\left\langle c_{i \downarrow} c_{j \uparrow}\right\rangle\right) \\ \Delta_{i j}^{*} &=\frac{V}{2}\left(\left\langle c_{j \downarrow}^{\dagger} c_{i \uparrow}^{\dagger}\right\rangle-\left\langle c_{j \uparrow}^{\dagger} c_{i \downarrow}^{\dagger}\right\rangle\right) \end{aligned} \]

  • the constant energy term

\[ E_{\text {const}}=-U\left\langle n_{i \uparrow}\right\rangle\left\langle n_{i \downarrow}\right\rangle+\sum_{i j} \frac{\left|\Delta_{i j}\right|^{2}}{V} \]

commutation relations

We can obtain the commutation relations: \[ \begin{aligned} \left[c_{i \uparrow}, H _{e f f}\right]_{-}&=\sum_{j \sigma^{\prime}} \tilde{h}_{i \uparrow j \sigma^{\prime}} c_{j \sigma^{\prime}}+\sum_{j} \Delta_{i j} c_{j \downarrow}^{\dagger} \\ \left[c_{i \uparrow}^{\dagger}, H _{e f f}\right]_{-}&=-\sum_{j \sigma^{\prime}} \tilde{h}_{j \sigma^{\prime}, i \uparrow} c_{j \sigma^{\prime}}^{\dagger}-\sum_{j} \Delta_{i j}^{*} c_{j \downarrow} \\ \left[c_{i \downarrow}, H _{e f f}\right]_{-}&=\sum_{j \sigma^{\prime}} \tilde{h}_{i \downarrow j \sigma^{\prime}} c_{j \sigma^{\prime}}-\sum_{j} \Delta_{j i} c_{j \uparrow}^{\dagger} \\ \left[c_{i \downarrow}^{\dagger}, H _{e f f}\right]_{-}&=-\sum_{j \sigma^{\prime}} \tilde{h}_{j \sigma^{\prime}, i \downarrow} c_{j \sigma^{\prime}}^{\dagger}+\sum_{j} \Delta_{j i}^{*} c_{j \uparrow} \end{aligned} \]

quasiparticle operators

the electron field operator can be expressed as a linear combination of electron- and hole-like quasiparticle excitations, \[ \begin{aligned} c_{i \sigma}=\sum_{n}^{\prime}\left(u_{i \sigma}^{n} \gamma_{n}-\sigma v_{i \sigma}^{n *} \gamma_{n}^{\dagger}\right)\\ c_{i \sigma}^{\dagger}=\sum_{n}^{\prime}\left(u_{i \sigma}^{n *} \gamma_{n}^{\dagger}-\sigma v_{i \sigma}^{n} \gamma_{n}\right) \end{aligned} \] where

  • \(\sigma=\pm 1\) denotes the up and down spin orientations.
  • The operators \(\gamma_{n}^{\dagger}\left(\gamma_{n}\right)\) create (annihilate) a Bogoliubov quasiparticle at state \(n .\)
  • The prime sign above the summation in the transformation means only those states with positive energy are counted.

The quasiparticle operators satisfy the anti-commutation relations: \[ \begin{array}{l} \left\{\gamma_{n}, \gamma_{m}^{\dagger}\right\}=\delta_{n m} \\ \left\{\gamma_{n}, \gamma_{m}\right\}=\left\{\gamma_{n}^{\dagger}, \gamma_{m}^{\dagger}\right\}=0 \end{array} \] These relations also guarantee the anti-commutation relations among the original electronic field operators.

diagonalize

With the above canonical transformation, the Hamiltonian is diagonalized in the following form: \[ H_{e f f}=\sum_{n} E_{n} \gamma_{n}^{\dagger} \gamma_{n}+E_{ const }^{\prime} \] where the index \(n\) also includes the pseudo-spin state index.

BdG equations

The quasiparticle operators satisfy the following commutation relations, \[ \begin{array}{l} {\left[\gamma_{n}^{\dagger}, H_{e f f}\right]_{-}=-E_{n} \gamma_{n}^{\dagger}} \\ {\left[\gamma_{n}, H_{e f f}\right]_{-}=E_{n} \gamma_{n}} \end{array} \] we compare the coefficients of the terms with \(\gamma_{n}\) and \(\gamma_{n}^{\dagger}\) and arrive at \[ \begin{aligned} E_{n} u_{i \uparrow}^{n}&=\sum_{j \sigma^{\prime}} \tilde{h}_{i \uparrow, j \sigma^{\prime}} u_{j \sigma^{\prime}}+\sum_{j} \Delta_{i j} v_{j \downarrow}^{n}\\ E_{n} u_{i \downarrow}^{n} &=\sum_{j \sigma^{\prime}} \tilde{h}_{i \downarrow j \sigma^{\prime}} u_{j \sigma^{\prime}}^{n}+\sum_{j} \Delta_{j i} v_{j \uparrow}^{n} \\ E_{n} v_{i \uparrow}^{n} &=-\sum_{j \sigma^{\prime}} \sigma^{\prime} \tilde{h}_{i \uparrow, j \sigma^{\prime}}^{*} v_{j \sigma^{\prime}}^{n}+\sum_{j} \Delta_{i j}^{*} u_{j \downarrow}^{n} \\ E_{n} v_{i \downarrow}^{n} &=\sum_{j \sigma^{\prime}} \sigma^{\prime} \tilde{h}_{i \downarrow, j \sigma^{\prime}}^{*}, v_{j \sigma^{\prime}}^{n}+\sum_{j} \Delta_{j i}^{*} u_{j \uparrow}^{n} \end{aligned} \]

matrix form

This set of BdG equations can be cast into a matrix form: \[ \sum_{j} \hat{M}_{i j} \hat{\phi}_{j}=E_{n} \hat{\phi}_{i} \] where \[ \hat{M}_{i j}=\left[\begin{array}{cccc} \tilde{h}_{i \uparrow j \uparrow} & \tilde{h}_{i \uparrow, j \downarrow} & 0 & \Delta_{i j} \\ \tilde{h}_{i \downarrow, j \uparrow} & \tilde{h}_{i \downarrow, j \downarrow} & \Delta_{j i} & 0 \\ 0 & \Delta_{i j}^{*} & -\tilde{h}_{i \uparrow, j \uparrow}^{*} & \tilde{h}_{i \uparrow, j \downarrow}^{*} \\ \Delta_{j i}^{*} & 0 & \tilde{h}_{i \downarrow j \uparrow}^{*} & -\tilde{h}_{i \downarrow, j \downarrow}^{*} \end{array}\right] \] and \[ \hat{\phi}_{i}=\left(\begin{array}{c} u_{i \uparrow} \\ u_{i \downarrow} \\ v_{i \uparrow} \\ v_{i \downarrow} \end{array}\right) \]

self-consistency conditions

This set of BdG equations is subjected to the self-consistency conditions: \[ \begin{aligned} n_{i \uparrow} &=\sum_{n}^{\prime}\left[\left|u_{i \uparrow}^{n}\right|^{2} f\left(E_{n}\right)+\left|v_{i \uparrow}^{n}\right|^{2} f\left(-E_{n}\right)\right] \\ n_{i \downarrow} &=\sum_{n}\left[\left|u_{i \downarrow}^{n}\right|^{2} f\left(E_{n}\right)+\left|v_{i \downarrow}^{n}\right|^{2} f\left(-E_{n}\right)\right] \end{aligned} \] and \[ \Delta_{i j}=\frac{V}{4} \sum_{n}^{\prime}\left[\left(u_{i \uparrow}^{n} v_{j \downarrow}^{n *}+u_{j \downarrow}^{n} v_{i \uparrow}^{n *}\right)+\left(u_{i \downarrow}^{n} v_{j \uparrow}^{n *}+u_{j \uparrow}^{n} v_{i \downarrow}^{*}\right)\right] \tanh \left(\frac{E_{n}}{2 k_{B} T}\right) \] where

  • \(f(E)\) is the Fermi-Dirac distribution function

Symmetry

Theorem: If \(\left(u_{i \uparrow}^{n}, v_{i \downarrow}^{n}, u_{i \downarrow}^{n}, v_{i \uparrow}^{n}\right)\) is the solution to the BdG equations with eigenvalue \(E_{n},\) then \(\left(-v_{i \uparrow}^{n *}, u_{i \downarrow}^{n *}, v_{i \downarrow}^{n *},-u_{i \uparrow}^{n *}\right)\) is the solution to the same equations with eigenvalue \(-E_{n}\)

Using this symmetry property, rewrite the self-consistency conditions \[ \begin{array}{l} n_{i \uparrow}=\sum_{n}\left|u_{i \uparrow}^{n}\right|^{2} f\left(E_{n}\right) \\ n_{i \downarrow}=\sum_{n}\left|v_{i \downarrow}^{n}\right|^{2} f\left(-E_{n}\right) \end{array} \] and \[ \Delta_{i j}=\frac{V}{4} \sum_{n}\left[u_{i \uparrow}^{n} v_{j \downarrow}^{n *}+u_{j \uparrow}^{n} v_{i \downarrow}^{*}\right] \tanh \left(\frac{E_{n}}{2 k_{B} T}\right) \]

Absence of SOC & spin-flip terms

In the absence of spin-orbit coupling and other spin-flip scattering terms, that is \(\tilde{h}_{i \uparrow, j \downarrow}=\tilde{h}_{i \downarrow, j \uparrow}=0,\) the BdG equations become block-diagonalized into two sets of equations: \[ \left\{\begin{array}{l} E_{\tilde{n} 1} u_{i \uparrow}^{\tilde{n} 1}=\sum_{j} \tilde{h}_{i \uparrow, j \uparrow} u_{j \uparrow}^{\tilde{n} 1}+\sum_{j} \Delta_{i j} v_{j \downarrow}^{\tilde{n} 1} \\ E_{\tilde{n} 1} v_{i \downarrow}^{\tilde{n} 1}=-\sum_{j} \tilde{h}_{i \downarrow, j \downarrow}^{*} v_{j \downarrow}^{\tilde{n} 1}+\sum_{j} \Delta_{j i}^{*} u_{j \uparrow}^{\tilde{n} 1} \end{array}\right. \] and \[ \left\{\begin{array}{l} E_{\tilde{n} 2} u_{i \downarrow}^{\tilde{n} 2}=\sum_{j} \tilde{h}_{i \downarrow, j \downarrow} u_{j \downarrow}^{\tilde{n} 2}+\sum_{j} \Delta_{j i} v_{j \uparrow}^{\tilde{n} 2} \\ E_{\tilde{n} 2} v_{i \uparrow}^{\tilde{n} 2}=-\sum_{j} \tilde{h}_{i \uparrow, j \uparrow}^{*} v_{j \uparrow}^{n}+\sum_{j} \Delta_{i j}^{*} u_{j \downarrow}^{n} \end{array}\right. \]

Canonical Transformation

This block-diagonalization structure leads to a distinct nature of the canonical transformation: \[ \begin{aligned} c_{i \uparrow} &=\sum_{\tilde{n}}^{\prime}\left(u_{i \uparrow}^{\tilde{n} 1} \gamma_{\tilde{n} 1}-v_{i \uparrow}^{\tilde{n} 2 *} \gamma_{\tilde{n} 2}^{\dagger}\right) \\ c_{i \uparrow}^{\dagger}&=\sum_{\tilde{n}}^{\prime}\left(u_{i \uparrow}^{\tilde{n} 1 *} \gamma_{\tilde{n} 1}^{\dagger}-v_{i \uparrow}^{\tilde{n} 2} \gamma_{\tilde{n} 2}\right) \\ c_{i \downarrow} &=\sum_{\tilde{n}}\left(u_{i \downarrow}^{\tilde{n} 2} \gamma_{\tilde{n} 2}+v_{i \downarrow}^{\tilde{n} 1 *} \gamma_{\tilde{n} 1}^{\dagger}\right) \\ c_{i \downarrow}^{\dagger}&=\sum_{\tilde{n}}\left(u_{i \downarrow}^{\tilde{n} 2 *} \gamma_{\tilde{n} 2}^{\dagger}+v_{i \downarrow}^{\tilde{n} 1} \gamma_{\tilde{n} 1}\right) \end{aligned} \]

reducing matrix

The block diagonalization and the symmetry property suggest that we merely need to solve one sets subject to the self-consistency condition: \[ \begin{aligned} n_{i \uparrow} &=\sum_{\tilde{n}}\left|u_{i \uparrow}^{\tilde{n} 1}\right|^{2} f\left(E_{\tilde{n} 1}\right) \\ n_{i \downarrow} &=\sum_{\tilde{n}}\left|v_{i \downarrow}^{\tilde{n} 1}\right|^{2} f\left(-E_{\tilde{n} 1}\right) \end{aligned} \] and \[ \Delta_{i j}=\frac{V}{4} \sum_{\tilde{n}}\left[u_{i \uparrow}^{\tilde{n} 1} v_{j \downarrow}^{\tilde{n} 1 *}+u_{j \uparrow}^{\tilde{n} 1} v_{i \downarrow}^{\tilde{n} 1 *}\right] \tanh \left(\frac{E_{\tilde{n} 1}}{2 k_{B} T}\right) \] Therefore

by reducing the diagonalization of a \(4 N\) by \(4 N\) matrix down to that of a \(2 N\) by \(2 N\) matrix, the computational efficiency is improved significantly.

Here \(N\) would be \(N_{x} N_{y}\) for a two-dimensional lattice while \(N_{x} N_{y} N_{z}\) for a three-dimensional lattice with \(N_{x}, N_{y},\) and \(N_{z}\) being the linear dimension of the system. When it is obvious that no spin-flip effect occurs, the reduced set of BdG equations, Eq. ( 2.30 ), will be used with the hat symbol on the energy eigenvalue index and the set index dropped. It will be encountered frequently in the later discussions.

BdG equations for \(s\) -wave SC

It is straightforward for us to obtain the BdG equations for an \(s\) -wave superconductor in the lattice model. There we first replace \(U\) by \(-U(U>0)\) so that the Hamiltonian becomes \[ H =\sum_{i j, \sigma \sigma^{\prime}} c_{i \sigma}^{\dagger}\left[h_{i \sigma, j \sigma^{\prime}}-\mu \delta_{i j} \delta_{\sigma \sigma^{\prime}}\right] c_{j \sigma^{\prime}}-U \sum_{i} n_{i \uparrow} n_{i \downarrow} \] which leads to the BdG equations: \[ \begin{aligned} E_{n} u_{i \uparrow}^{n} &=\sum_{j \sigma^{\prime}} \tilde{h}_{i \uparrow j \sigma^{\prime}} u_{j \sigma^{\prime}}+\Delta_{i i} v_{i \downarrow}^{n} \\ E_{n} u_{i \downarrow}^{n} &=\sum_{j \sigma^{\prime}} \tilde{h}_{i \downarrow j \sigma^{\prime}} u_{j \sigma^{\prime}}^{n}+\Delta_{i i} v_{i \uparrow}^{n} \\ E_{n} v_{i \uparrow}^{n} &=-\sum_{j \sigma^{\prime}} \sigma^{\prime} \tilde{h}_{i \uparrow, j \sigma^{\prime}}^{*} v_{j \sigma^{\prime}}^{n}+\Delta_{i i}^{*} u_{j \downarrow}^{n} \\ E_{n} v_{i \downarrow}^{n} &=\sum_{j \sigma^{\prime}} \sigma^{\prime} \tilde{h}_{i \downarrow, j \sigma^{\prime}}^{*} v_{j \sigma^{\prime}}^{n}+\Delta_{i i}^{*} u_{i \uparrow}^{n} \end{aligned} \] Here \[ \tilde{h}_{i \sigma, j \sigma^{\prime}}=h_{i \sigma, j \sigma^{\prime}}-\mu \delta_{i j} \delta_{\sigma \sigma^{\prime}} \] and the self-consistency condition \[ \Delta_{i i}=\frac{U}{2} \sum_{n}^{\prime}\left[u_{i \uparrow}^{n} v_{i \downarrow}^{n *}+u_{i \downarrow}^{n} v_{i \uparrow}^{n *}\right] \tanh \left(\frac{E_{n}}{2 k_{B} T}\right) \]

Absence terms

In the absence of spin-orbit coupling and spin-flip scattering, the BdG equations become \[ \left\{\begin{array}{l} E_{\tilde{n} 1} u_{i \uparrow}^{\tilde{n} 1}=\sum_{j} \tilde{h}_{i \uparrow, j \uparrow} u_{j \uparrow}^{\tilde{n} 1}+\Delta_{i i} v_{i \downarrow}^{\tilde{n} 1} \\ E_{\tilde{n} 1} v_{i \downarrow}^{\tilde{n} 1}=-\sum_{j} \tilde{h}_{i \downarrow, j \downarrow}^{*} v_{j \downarrow}^{\tilde{n} 1}+\Delta_{i i}^{*} u_{i \uparrow}^{\tilde{n} 1} \end{array}\right. \] and \[ \left\{\begin{array}{l} E_{\tilde{n} 2} u_{i \downarrow}^{\tilde{n} 2}=\sum_{j} \tilde{h}_{i \downarrow, j \downarrow} u_{j \downarrow}^{\tilde{n} 2}+\Delta_{i i} v_{i \uparrow}^{\tilde{n} 2} \\ E_{\tilde{n} 2} v_{i \uparrow}^{\tilde{n} 2}=-\sum_{j} \tilde{h}_{i \uparrow, j \uparrow}^{*} v_{j \uparrow}^{n}+\Delta_{i i}^{*} u_{i \downarrow}^{\tilde{n} 2} \end{array}\right. \] with \[ \Delta_{i i}=\frac{U}{2} \sum_{\tilde{n}} u_{i \uparrow}^{\tilde{n} 1} v_{j \downarrow}^{\tilde{n} 1 *} \tanh \left(\frac{E_{\tilde{n} 1}}{2 k_{B} T}\right) \]

Physical quantities

The BdG solutions can describe

  1. single particle properties

  2. two-particle correlations

For example

  • local density of states is a direct measure of single particle properties
  • eigenfunctions can be used to study the two-particle correlations

In particular

  • the optical superconductivity reveals the information about the coherent peak
  • the superfluid stiffness and its temperature dependence set a criterion for superconductivity.

Both quantities are directly measurable in experiments. Theoretically, the formulae for these quantities, as derived from the BdG method, are also valid for inhomogeneous superconductors.

Local Density of States

From Eqs above \[ \begin{array}{l} n_{i \uparrow}=\sum_{n}^{\prime}\left[\left|u_{i}^{n}\right|^{2} f\left(E_{n}\right)+\left|v_{i \uparrow}^{n}\right|^{2} f\left(-E_{n}\right)\right] \\ n_{i \downarrow}=\sum_{n}^{\prime}\left[\left|u_{i \downarrow}^{n}\right|^{2} f\left(E_{n}\right)+\left|v_{i \downarrow}^{n}\right|^{2} f\left(-E_{n}\right)\right] \end{array} \] we extract the expression for the local density of states: \[ \rho_{i \sigma}(E)=\sum_{n}^{\prime}\left[\left|u_{i \sigma}^{n}\right|^{2} \delta\left(E_{n}-E\right)+\left|v_{i \sigma}\right|^{2} \delta\left(E_{n}+E\right)\right] \] and the corresponding thermalized density of states \[ \rho_{i \sigma}(E)=-\sum_{n}\left[\left|u_{i \sigma}^{n}\right|^{2} f^{\prime}\left(E_{n}-E\right)+\left|v_{i \sigma}\right|^{2} f^{\prime}\left(E_{n}+E\right)\right] \]

Bond Current

The bond current can be derived in the following way. The Heisenberg equation of motion for \(\left\langle n_{i}\right\rangle=\left\langle n_{i \uparrow}\right\rangle+\left\langle n_{i \downarrow}\right\rangle\) is: \[ \begin{aligned} i \hbar \frac{\partial\left\langle n_{i}\right\rangle}{\partial t} &=\left\langle\left[n_{i}, H\right]_{-}\right\rangle \\ &=\left\langle\left\{-\sum_{j \neq i, \sigma, \sigma^{\prime}}\left[-t\left(j \sigma^{\prime}, i \sigma\right) c_{j \sigma^{\prime}}^{\dagger} c_{i \sigma}+t\left(i \sigma, j \sigma^{\prime}\right) c_{i \sigma}^{\dagger} c_{j \sigma^{\prime}}\right]\right\}\right\rangle \end{aligned} \] The electrical current operator from site \(j\) to site \(i\) is then found to be: \[ \hat{J}_{i j}=\frac{e}{i \hbar} \sum_{\sigma, \sigma^{\prime}}\left[t\left(i \sigma, j \sigma^{\prime}\right) c_{i \sigma}^{\dagger} c_{j \sigma^{\prime}}-t\left(j \sigma^{\prime}, i \sigma\right) c_{j \sigma^{\prime}}^{\dagger} c_{i \sigma}\right] \] and the average bond current is given by: \[ J_{i j}=\frac{e}{i \hbar} \sum_{\sigma, \sigma^{\prime}} \sum_{n}^{\prime}\left\{t\left(i \sigma, j \sigma^{\prime}\right)\left[u_{i \sigma}^{n *} u_{j \sigma^{\prime}}^{n} f\left(E_{n}\right)+\sigma \sigma^{\prime} v_{i \sigma}^{n} v_{j \sigma^{\prime}}^{n *}\left(1-f\left(E_{n}\right)\right)\right]- c.c. \right\} \]

symmetry

Using the symmetry property of the BdG equations, we can also write the physical quantities in the following form: \[ \begin{array}{c} n_{i \sigma}=\sum_{n}\left|u_{i \sigma}^{n}\right|^{2} f\left(E_{n}\right)=\sum_{n}\left|v_{i \sigma}^{n}\right|^{2}\left[1-f\left(E_{n}\right)\right] \\ J_{i j}=\frac{e}{i \hbar} \sum_{\sigma, \sigma^{\prime}} \sum_{n}\left\{t\left(i \sigma, j \sigma^{\prime}\right) u_{i \sigma}^{n *} u_{j \sigma^{\prime}}^{n} f\left(E_{n}\right)- c.c. \right\} \\ =\frac{e}{i \hbar} \sum_{\sigma, \sigma^{\prime}} \sum_{n}\left\{t\left(i \sigma, j \sigma^{\prime}\right) \sigma \sigma^{\prime} v_{i \sigma}^{n} v_{j \sigma^{\prime}}^{n *}\left(1-f\left(E_{n}\right)\right)- c.c. \right\} \end{array} \] and \[ \rho_{i \sigma}(E)=-\sum_{n}\left|u_{i \sigma}^{n}\right|^{2} f^{\prime}\left(E_{n}-E\right)=-\sum_{n}\left|v_{i \sigma}^{n}\right|^{2} f^{\prime}\left(E_{n}+E\right) \] These new formulations are especially useful when we study the ferromagnetic impurity or Zeeman effect because the wavefunction of quasiparticles can be solved in a \(2 \times 2\) spin space.

two-particle correlations

we give the derivation of the superfluid density for the model with the Hamiltonian \[ H =\sum_{i j, \sigma} c_{i \sigma}^{\dagger}\left[-t_{i j}-\left(\mu+\frac{U}{2}-U_{i}^{ imp }\right) \delta_{i j}\right] c_{j \sigma}+U \sum_{i} n_{i \uparrow} n_{i \downarrow}-\frac{V}{2} \sum_{i \neq j} n_{i} n_{j} \] Here the impurity potential is represented by \(U_{i}^{ imp }\) In the presence of an electric field pointing to the \(x\) -direction, \[ E_{x}\left( r _{i}, t\right)=\Xi_{x} e^{i\left( q \cdot r _{i}-\omega t\right)} \] The current density along the \(x\) -direction is defined as \[ J_{x}\left( r _{i}, t\right)=\sigma_{x x}\left( r _{i}, \omega\right) E_{x}\left( r _{i}, t\right) \] where \(\sigma_{x x}\left( r _{i}, \omega\right)\) is the local conductivity.

Gauge

In the Coulomb gauge, which dictates \(\nabla \cdot A =0\) and the scalar potential \(\phi=0,\) the electric field can be expressed in terms of a vector potential \[ A_{x}\left( r _{i}, t\right)=-\frac{i}{\omega} E_{x}\left( r _{i}, t\right) \] Therefore, the total Hamiltonian becomes \[ H_{ t }=-\sum_{i j, \sigma} \tilde{t}_{i j} c_{i \sigma}^{\dagger} c_{j \sigma}+\sum_{i \sigma}\left(U_{i}^{ imp }-\frac{U}{2}-\mu\right) c_{i \sigma}^{\dagger} c_{i \sigma}+U \sum_{i} n_{i \uparrow} n_{i \downarrow}-\frac{V}{2} \sum_{i j} n_{i} n_{j} \] where \[ \tilde{t}_{i j}=t_{i j} e^{i \phi_{i j}(t)} \] with the gauge phase \(\phi_{i j}(t)=e A_{i j}=e A_{x}\left( r _{i}, t\right)\left(x_{i}-x_{j}\right) .\) Hereafter the charge \(e\) for electrons includes the sign. In the linear response limit, we expand this hopping term up to the second order of \(A_{x}\left( r _{i}, t\right)\) \[ \tilde{t}_{i j} \approx t\left[1+i \phi_{i j}+\frac{1}{2 !}\left(i \phi_{i j}\right)^{2}\right] \] The total Hamiltonian can then be rewritten as: \[ H_{ t }=H-i \sum_{i j, \sigma} \phi_{i j}(t) t_{i j} c_{i \sigma}^{\dagger} c_{j \sigma}+\frac{1}{2} \sum_{i j, \sigma} \phi_{i j}^{2}(t) t_{i j} c_{i \sigma}^{\dagger} c_{j \sigma} \]

hopping integral

Alternative derivation

An alternative derivation of the current density operator is to start with the electric polarization operator: \[ P =e \sum_{i} r _{i} n_{i} \] with its \(x\) -component \[ P_{x}=e \sum_{i} x_{i} n_{i} \] The time-derivative is \[ \begin{aligned} J_{x}^{Q}\left( r _{i}\right) &=i\left[H_{t}, P_{x}\right] \\ &=i e \sum_{i j, \sigma} x_{i}\left[\tilde{t}_{i j} c_{i \sigma}^{\dagger} c_{j \sigma}-\tilde{t}_{j i} c_{j \sigma}^{\dagger} c_{i \sigma}\right] \\ &=i e \sum_{i j, \sigma}\left(x_{i}-x_{j}\right) \tilde{t}_{i j} c_{i \sigma}^{\dagger} c_{j \sigma} \\ &=i e \sum_{i j, \sigma}\left(x_{i}-x_{j}\right) t_{i j}\left(1+i \phi_{i j}\right) c_{i \sigma}^{\dagger} c_{j \sigma} \\ &=e J_{x}^{P}\left( r _{i}\right)+e^{2} K_{x}\left( r _{i}\right) A_{x}\left( r _{i}, t\right) \end{aligned} \]

expectation value

Now let us calculate the expectation value of the current density operator. In the linear response theory, the statistical operator in the interaction picture is given by: \[ \hat{\rho}_{I}(t)=\hat{\rho}_{I}(-\infty)-\frac{i}{\hbar} \int_{-\infty}^{t}\left[H_{I}^{\prime}\left(t^{\prime}\right), \hat{\rho}_{I}(-\infty)\right]_{-} d t^{\prime} \] The expectation of a physical variable is found to be: \[ \begin{aligned} \langle\hat{O}\rangle &=\operatorname{Tr}\left[\hat{\rho}_{I}(-\infty) \hat{O}_{I}\right]-\frac{i}{\hbar} \int_{-\infty}^{t} \operatorname{Tr}\left\{\hat{\rho}_{I}(-\infty)\left[\hat{O}_{I}(t), H_{I}^{\prime}\left(t^{\prime}\right)\right]_{-}\right\} d t^{\prime} \\ &=\left\langle\hat{O}_{I}\right\rangle_{0}-\frac{i}{\hbar} \int_{-\infty}^{t}\left\langle\left[\hat{O}_{I}(t), H_{I}^{\prime}\left(t^{\prime}\right)\right]_{-}\right\rangle_{0} d t^{\prime} \end{aligned} \] where \[ \hat{O}_{I}=e^{i H t} \hat{O} e^{-i H t} \] and \[ H_{I}^{\prime}(t)=e^{i H t} H^{\prime}(t) e^{-i H t} \] with the term proportional to \(A^{2}(t)\) in \(H^{\prime}(t)\) neglected. The particle current density is then given by \[ \left\langle J_{x}^{P}\left( r _{i}\right)\right\rangle=-i \int_{-\infty}^{t}\left\langle\left[J_{x}^{P}\left( r _{i}, t\right), H_{I}^{\prime}(t)\right]\right\rangle \] where the particle operator and the perturbation term in the Heisenberg picture with respect to the unperturbed Hamiltonian \(H\) are given by, respectively, \[ J_{x}^{P}\left( r _{i}, t\right)=e^{i H t} J_{x}^{P}\left( r _{i}\right) e^{-i H t} \] We notice that the first term in \(H^{\prime}(t)\) can be rewritten as \[ -e \sum_{i, \sigma} A_{x}\left( r _{i}, t\right) J_{x}^{P}\left( r _{i}\right)=\frac{i e}{\omega} J_{x}^{P}(- q ) \Xi_{x} e^{-i \omega t} \] where the Fourier transform of the particle density operator is given by \[ J_{x}^{P}( q )=\sum_{i} e^{-i q \cdot r _{i}} J_{x}^{P}\left( r _{i}\right) \] We then obtain \[ \left\langle J_{x}^{P}\left( r _{i}\right)\right\rangle=\frac{e}{\omega} E_{x}\left( r _{i}, t\right) e^{-i q \cdot r _{i}} \int_{-\infty}^{t} d t^{\prime} e^{i \omega\left(t-t^{\prime}\right)}\left\langle\left[J_{x}^{P}\left( r _{i}, t\right), J_{x}^{P}\left(- q , t^{\prime}\right)\right]\right\rangle \] Finally, the current density is found to be \[ \begin{aligned} \left\langle J_{x}^{Q}\left( r _{i}\right)\right\rangle=& \frac{e^{2}}{\omega} E_{x}\left( r _{i}, t\right) e^{-i q \cdot r _{i}} \int_{-\infty}^{t} d t^{\prime} e^{i \omega\left(t-t^{\prime}\right)}\left\langle\left[J_{x}^{P}\left( r _{i}, t\right), J_{x}^{P}\left(- q , t^{\prime}\right)\right]\right\rangle \\ &-\frac{i e^{2}}{\omega}\left\langle K_{X}\left( r _{i}\right)\right\rangle E_{x}\left( r _{i}, t\right) \end{aligned} \]

local conductivity

The local conductivity is then given by \[ \begin{aligned} \sigma_{x x}\left( r _{i}, \omega\right)=& \frac{\left\langle J_{x}^{Q}\left( r _{i}\right)\right\rangle}{E_{x}\left( r _{i}, t\right)} \\ =& \frac{e^{2}}{\omega} e^{-i q \cdot r _{i}} \int_{-\infty}^{t} d t^{\prime} e^{i \omega\left(t-t^{\prime}\right)}\left\langle\left[J_{x}^{P}\left( r _{i}, t\right), J_{x}^{P}\left(- q , t^{\prime}\right)\right]\right\rangle \\ &-\frac{i e^{2}}{\omega}\left\langle K_{x}\left( r _{i}\right)\right\rangle \end{aligned} \] To eliminate the atomic fluctuations, we take an average over the spatial variable \(r _{i}\) and obtain the conductivity \[ \sigma_{x x}( q , \omega)=\frac{e^{2}}{N \omega} \int_{-\infty}^{t} d t^{\prime} e^{i \omega\left(t-t^{\prime}\right)}\left\langle\left[J_{x}^{P}( q , t), J_{x}^{P}\left(- q , t^{\prime}\right)\right]\right\rangle-\frac{i e^{2}}{\omega}\left\langle K_{x}\right\rangle \] where \[ \left\langle K_{x}\right\rangle=\frac{1}{N} \sum_{i}\left\langle K_{x}\left( r _{i}\right)\right\rangle \]

frequency-dependent

since the correlation function is only a function of the time difference \(t-t^{\prime},\) we can express the conductivity as \[ \begin{aligned} \sigma_{x x}( q , \omega) &=\frac{e^{2}}{N \omega} \int_{0}^{\infty} d t e^{i \omega t}\left\langle\left[J_{x}^{P}( q , t), J_{x}^{P}(- q , 0)\right]\right\rangle-\frac{i e^{2}}{\omega}\left\langle K_{x}\right\rangle \\ &=\frac{e^{2}}{i \omega}\left[\frac{i}{N} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} d t \theta(t) e^{i \omega t}\left\langle\left[J_{x}^{P}( q , t), J_{x}^{P}(- q , 0)\right]\right\rangle+\left\langle K_{x}\right\rangle\right] \\ &=\frac{e^{2}}{i \omega}\left[-\Pi_{x x}( q , \omega)+\left\langle K_{x}\right\rangle\right] \end{aligned} \] where we define the retarded correlation function of the particle current operator as \[ \Pi_{x x}( q , t)=-\frac{i}{N} \theta(t)\left\langle\left[J_{x}^{P}( q , t), J_{x}^{P}(- q , 0)\right]\right\rangle \] with its Fourier transform \[ \Pi_{x x}( q , \omega)=\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} d t e^{i \omega t} \Pi_{x x}( q , t) \] The frequency-dependent, uniform electrical conductivity is given by taking the limit \(q \rightarrow 0\) \[ \sigma_{x x}(\omega)=\frac{e^{2}}{i \omega}\left[-\Pi_{x x}( q =0, \omega)+\left\langle K_{x}\right\rangle\right] \] Be reminded that the order of these limits cannot be reversed.

transverse conductivity

Similarly the transverse conductivity can also be derived as: \[ \sigma_{x y}( q , \omega)=\frac{e^{2}}{i \omega}\left[-\Pi_{x y}( q , \omega)\right] \]
