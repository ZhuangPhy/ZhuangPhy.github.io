The Quasi-particle Concept

In Sect. \(3.3,\) we discussed relatively simple, exactly solvable model systems, which strictly speaking do not require the Green's function formalism. They were intended merely to introduce the solution techniques. The full scope of the method becomes clear only when it is applied to the treatment of interacting systems. In most such cases, to be sure, we will then no longer be able to treat the many-body problem in a mathematically strict manner. Approximations are unavoidable and must be tolerated. The concept of \[ quasi-particles \] has proven to be extraordinarily useful in this connection, and we shall consider it in detail in the present section. To be more concrete, we will first keep interacting electron systems in mind. The extension to other many-body systems will cause no difficulties.

We wish to investigate which statements about interacting electron systems can be made using Green's functions. To this end, we must first define the operators (or combinations of operators) \(A\) and \(B\) which are to be used to construct the Green's function being considered. In most practical cases, the type of this function is quite unambiguously fixed by the physical problem and by the representation of the model Hamiltonian used.

One-Electron Green's Functions

As in \((2.55),\) the Hamiltonian of a system of \(N_{ e }\) mutually-interacting electrons in the Bloch representation is given by: \[ H=\sum_{k \sigma} \varepsilon(k) a_{k \sigma}^{+} a_{k \sigma}+\frac{1}{2} \sum_{k p q \atop \sigma \sigma^{\prime}} v_{k p}(q) a_{k+q \sigma}^{+} a_{p-q \sigma^{\prime}}^{+} a_{p \sigma^{\prime}} a_{k \sigma} \] We limit our considerations to the electrons of a single energy band, so that we can suppress the band indices. For the so-called Bloch energies \(\varepsilon(k),\) from (2.14) and (2.21) we have: \[ \varepsilon( k )=\int d ^{3} r \psi_{k}^{*}( r )\left[-\frac{\hbar^{2}}{2 m} \Delta+V( r )\right] \psi_{k}( r ) \] \(\psi_{k}( r )\) is a Bloch function and \(V( r )\) is the periodic lattice potential. We treat \(\varepsilon( k )\) in the following as a given model parameter. We calculated the Coulomb matrix element in (2.54): \[ v_{k p}(q)=\frac{e^{2}}{4 \pi \varepsilon_{0}} \iint d ^{3} r_{1} d ^{3} r_{2} \frac{\psi_{k+q}^{*}\left( r _{1}\right) \psi_{p-q}^{*}\left( r _{2}\right) \psi_{p}\left( r _{2}\right) \psi_{k}\left( r _{1}\right)}{\left| r _{1}- r _{2}\right|} \] With a constant lattice potential \(V( r ) \equiv\) const, this becomes \[ v_{k p}(q) \frac{e^{2}}{V(r)= const } v_{0}(q)=\frac{e^{2}}{\varepsilon_{0} V q^{2}} \] Frequently, one uses the model Hamiltonian (3.311) also in its Wannier representation (cf. e.g. (2.115)): \[ H=\sum_{i j \sigma} T_{i j} a_{i \sigma}^{+} a_{j \sigma}+\frac{1}{2} \sum_{i j k l \atop \sigma \sigma^{\prime}} v(i j ; k l) a_{i \sigma}^{+} a_{j \sigma^{\prime}}^{+} a_{l \sigma^{\prime}} a_{k \sigma} \] The so-called hopping integrals \[ T_{i j}=\int d ^{3} r \omega^{*}\left( r - R _{i}\right)\left\{-\frac{\hbar^{2}}{2 m} \Delta+V( r )\right\} \omega\left( r - R _{j}\right) \] are related to the Bloch energies via a Fourier transform \(\varepsilon(k)\) (cf. (2.113)). \(\omega\left( r - R _{i}\right)\) is the Wannier function centered at \(R_{i}\). \[ \begin{aligned} v(i j ; k l)=& \frac{e^{2}}{4 \pi \varepsilon_{0}} \iint d ^{3} r_{1} d ^{3} r_{2} \omega^{*}\left( r _{1}- R _{i}\right) \omega^{*}\left( r _{2}- R _{j}\right) \\ & \cdot \frac{1}{\left| r _{1}- r _{2}\right|} \omega\left( r _{2}- R _{l}\right) \omega\left( r _{1}- R _{k}\right) \end{aligned} \] In this section, we show that the one-electron Green's function, already introduced in Eq. (3.193) \[ \begin{aligned} G_{k \sigma}^{\alpha}(E) \equiv &\left\langle\left\langle a_{k \sigma} ; a_{k \sigma}^{+}\right\rangle\right\rangle_{E}^{\alpha} \\ G_{i j \sigma}^{\alpha}(E) \equiv &\left\langle\left\langle a_{i \sigma} ; a_{j \sigma}^{+}\right\rangle\right\rangle_{E}^{\alpha} \\ & \alpha=\text { ret, } adv , c \quad(\varepsilon=-) \end{aligned} \] or the equivalent one-electron spectral density \[ \begin{array}{l} S_{k \sigma}(E)=\frac{1}{2 \pi} \int_{-\infty}^{+\infty} d \left(t-t^{\prime}\right) \exp \left(-\frac{ i }{\hbar} E\left(t-t^{\prime}\right)\right)\left\langle\left[a_{k \sigma}(t), a_{k \sigma}^{+}\left(t^{\prime}\right)\right]_{+}\right\rangle \\ S_{i j \sigma}(E)=\frac{1}{2 \pi} \int_{-\infty}^{+\infty} d \left(t-t^{\prime}\right) \exp \left(-\frac{ i }{\hbar} E\left(t-t^{\prime}\right)\right)\left\langle\left[a_{i \sigma}(t), a_{j \sigma}^{+}\left(t^{\prime}\right)\right]_{+}\right\rangle \end{array} \] determine the entire equilibrium thermodynamics, even for systems of interacting electrons. This of course presumes that one was able in some manner to compute these functions.

To show this, we first write the equation of motion of the \(k\) -dependent Green's function, for which we require the following commutator: \[ \begin{aligned} \left[a_{k \sigma}, H \right]_{-}=&(\varepsilon( k )-\mu) a_{k \sigma}+\\ &+\sum_{p, q \atop \sigma^{\prime}} v_{p, k+q}( q ) a_{p+q \sigma^{\prime}}^{+} a_{p \sigma^{\prime}} a_{k+q \sigma} \end{aligned} \] (Its derivation can be carried out by the reader as an exercise!). With the higherorder Green's function \[ { }^{\alpha} \Gamma_{p k ; q}^{\sigma^{\prime} \sigma}(E) \equiv\left\langle\left\langle a_{p+q \sigma^{\prime}}^{+} a_{p \sigma^{\prime}} a_{k+q \sigma} ; a_{k \sigma}^{+}\right\rangle\right\rangle_{E}^{\alpha} \] the equation of motion is then given by: \[ (E-\varepsilon(k)+\mu) G_{k \sigma}^{\alpha}(E)=\hbar+\sum_{p, q, \sigma^{\prime}} v_{p, k+q}(q)^{\alpha} \Gamma_{p k ; q}^{\sigma^{\prime} \sigma}(E) \] The unknown function \(\Gamma\) on the right-hand side prevents a direct solution of this equation. However, we postulate that the following decomposition is permitted: \[ \begin{aligned} \left\langle\left\langle\left[a_{k \sigma}, H - H _{0}\right]_{-} ; a_{k \sigma}^{+}\right\rangle\right\rangle_{E}^{\alpha} &=\sum_{p, q, \sigma^{\prime}} v_{p, k+q}(q) \Gamma_{p k ; q}^{\sigma^{\prime} \sigma}(E) \equiv \\ & \equiv \Sigma_{\sigma}^{\alpha}(k, E) G_{k \sigma}^{\alpha}(E) \end{aligned} \] This equation defines the so-called \[ \text { self-energy, } \quad \Sigma_{\sigma}^{\alpha}(k, E) \] which we wish to discuss in more detail. With it, the equation of motion (3.324) can be formally solved in a simple fashion: \[ G_{k \sigma}^{\alpha}(E)=\frac{\hbar}{E-\left(\varepsilon(k)-\mu+\Sigma_{\sigma}^{\alpha}(k, E)\right)} \] Comparing this expression with that for the non-interacting system \((3.197),\) we see that the entire influence of the particle interactions is contained in the self-energy \(\Sigma_{\sigma}(k, E) .\) As a rule, this is a complex-valued function of \((k, E),\) whose real part determines the energy and whose imaginary part the lifetime of the quasi-particles, which we have yet to define.

We can reformulate Eq. (3.326) to some extent. We denote the one-electron Green's function of the non-interacting electrons by \(G_{k c}^{(0)}(E) ;\) then from (3.326) it follows that: \[ \begin{aligned} G_{k \sigma}(E)=\hbar\left\{\hbar\left[G_{k \sigma}^{(0)}(E)\right]^{-1}-\Sigma_{\sigma}(k, E)\right\}^{-1} \\ \Longrightarrow\left\{\left[G_{k \sigma}^{(0)}(E)\right]^{-1}-\frac{1}{\hbar} \Sigma_{\sigma}(k, E)\right\} G_{k \sigma}(E)=1 \end{aligned} \] Here, we have suppressed the index \(\alpha\) to make the expressions clearer. Finally, we arrive at the so-called

Dyson equation \[ G_{k \sigma}(E)=G_{k \sigma}^{(0)}(E)+\frac{1}{\hbar} G_{k \sigma}^{(0)}(E) \Sigma_{\sigma}(k, E) G_{k \sigma}(E) \]

Our goal will be to obtain at least an approximate determination of the self-energy. Inserting an approximate expression for \(\Sigma_{\sigma}(k, E)\) into the Dyson equation already implies summation over an infinite partial series. We recall however that for the derivation of \((3.327),\) we had to postulate the decomposition (3.325) of the higherorder Green's function.

The Electronic Self-Energy

In this section, we wish to obtain an overview of the general structures of the fundamental quantities self-energy, Green's functions, and spectral density. Our starting point is the representation (3.326) of the single-particle Green's function, whereby in the case of the self-energy, we are dealing in general with a complex quantity: \[ \Sigma_{\sigma}^{\alpha}(k, E)=R_{\sigma}^{\alpha}(k, E)+ i I_{\sigma}^{\alpha}(k, E) \] The index \(\alpha\) stands for retarded, advanced, or causal. The corresponding selfenergies are quite distinct. Thus, for example, according to (3.186) and (3.187) for a real spectral density, we have: \[ \left(G_{k \sigma}^{ adv }(E)\right)^{*}=G_{k \sigma}^{ ret }(E) \] This implies \[ \left(\Sigma_{\sigma}^{ adv }(k, E)\right)^{*}=\Sigma_{\sigma}^{ ret }(k, E) \] The relationship is thus simple. We may limit our considerations without loss of generality to the retarded functions. We then leave off the supplement \(+ i 0^{+},\) if \(I_{\sigma} \neq\) 0. We omit the index "ret" on the self-energy in the following. First, we rearrange Eq. (3.326) slightly: \[ G_{k \sigma}^{ ret }(E)=\hbar \frac{\left\{E-\left(\varepsilon(k)-\mu+R_{\sigma}(k, E)\right)\right\}+ i I_{\sigma}(k, E)}{\left\{E-\left(\varepsilon(k)-\mu+R_{\sigma}(k, E)\right)\right\}^{2}+I_{\sigma}^{2}(k, E)} \] From (3.154), we then have for the spectral density: \[ S_{k \sigma}(E)=-\frac{\hbar}{\pi} \frac{I_{\sigma}(k, E)}{\left\{E-\left(\varepsilon(k)-\mu+R_{\sigma}(k, E)\right)\right\}^{2}+I_{\sigma}^{2}(k, E)} \] With (3.146), we could have given the general spectral representation of the spectral density, which - for the case of the one-electron spectral density which interests us here-becomes: \[ \begin{aligned} S_{k \sigma}(E)=& \frac{\hbar}{\Xi} \sum_{n, m}\left|\left\langle E_{n}\left|a_{k \sigma}^{+}\right| E_{m}\right\rangle\right|^{2} e ^{-\beta E_{n}}\left( e ^{\beta E}+1\right) \\ & \cdot \delta\left[E-\left(E_{n}-E_{m}\right)\right] \end{aligned} \] \(S_{k \sigma}(E)\) is thus non-negative for all \((k, \sigma, E)\). This, however, according to (3.331), gives \[ I_{\sigma}(k, E) \leq 0 \] for the imaginary part of the self-energy (retarded!). We now want to investigate the expression (3.331) somewhat more precisely. Without explicit knowledge of \(R_{\sigma}(k, E)\) and \(I_{\sigma}(k, E),\) we nevertheless expect in the usual case to find more or less prominent maxima in the spectral density at the resonance energies \(E_{i \sigma}(k)\) defined by \[ E_{i \sigma}(k) \stackrel{!}{=} \varepsilon(k)-\mu+R_{\sigma}\left(k, E_{i \sigma}(k)\right) ; \quad i=1,2,3, \ldots \] Here, we must distinguish between two cases. Case A: Let \[ I_{\sigma}(k, E) \equiv 0 \] hold in a certain energy range, which contains the resonance \(E_{i \sigma} .\) Then, in \((3.331),\) we must go to the limit \(I_{\sigma} \rightarrow-0^{+} .\) Using the formulation of the \(\delta\) -function as a limiting expression, \[ \delta\left(E-E_{0}\right)=\frac{1}{\pi} \lim _{x \rightarrow 0} \frac{x}{\left(E-E_{0}\right)^{2}+x^{2}} \] we then find: \[ S_{k \sigma}(E)=\hbar \delta\left[E-\left(\varepsilon(k)-\mu+R_{\sigma}(k, E)\right)\right] \] We then make use of \[ \delta[f(x)]=\sum_{i} \frac{1}{\left|f^{\prime}\left(x_{i}\right)\right|} \delta\left(x-x_{i}\right) ; \quad f\left(x_{i}\right)=0 \] which allows us to write the following expression in place of (3.337) : \[ \begin{array}{l} S_{k \sigma}(E)=\hbar \sum_{i=1}^{n} \alpha_{i \sigma}(k) \delta\left(E-E_{i \sigma}(k)\right) \\ \alpha_{i \sigma}(k)=\left|1-\frac{\partial}{\partial E} R_{\sigma}(k, E)\right|_{E=E_{i \sigma}}^{-1} \end{array} \] The sums extend over those resonances \(E_{i \sigma}\) which lie within the energy range where (3.335) is valid.

Case B: We assume \[ I_{\sigma}(k, E) \neq 0 \] where, however, \[ \left|I_{\sigma}(k, E)\right| \ll\left|\varepsilon(k)-\mu+R_{\sigma}(k, E)\right| \] is valid within a certain neighbourhood of the resonance \(E_{i \sigma} .\) Then we can expect a prominent maximum at the energy \(E=E_{i \sigma} .\) To see this, we expand the expression \[ F_{\sigma}(k, E) \equiv \varepsilon(k)-\mu+R_{\sigma}(k, E) \] around the resonance position and terminate the series after the linear term: \[ \begin{aligned} F_{\sigma}(k, E) &=F_{\sigma}\left(k, E_{i \sigma}\right)+\left.\left(E-E_{i \sigma}\right) \frac{\partial F_{\sigma}}{\partial E}\right|_{E=E_{i \sigma}}+\cdots \\ &=E_{i \sigma}(k)+\left.\left(E-E_{i \sigma}\right) \frac{\partial R_{\sigma}}{\partial E}\right|_{E=E_{i \sigma}}+\cdots \end{aligned} \] This means that: \[ \begin{aligned} \left(E-\varepsilon( k )+\mu-R_{\sigma}( k , E)\right)^{2} & \simeq\left(E-E_{i \sigma}\right)^{2}\left(1-\left.\frac{\partial R_{\sigma}}{\partial E}\right|_{E=E_{i \sigma}}\right)^{2}=\\ &=\alpha_{i \sigma}^{-2}( k )\left(E-E_{i \sigma}( k )\right)^{2} \end{aligned} \] We insert this expression into \((3.331) .\) Let us now further assume that \(I_{\sigma}(k, E)\) in the neighbourhood of the resonance \(E_{i \sigma}\) is a continuous, only weakly varying function of \(E,\) so that to a good approximation in the energy region of interest, we can set \[ I_{\sigma}(k, E) \approx I_{\sigma}\left(k, E_{i \sigma}(k)\right) \equiv I_{i \sigma}(k) \] then the spectral density can be approximated as follows: \[ S_{k \sigma}^{(i)}(E) \approx-\frac{\hbar}{\pi} \frac{\alpha_{i \sigma}^{2}(k) I_{i \sigma}(k)}{\left(E-E_{i \sigma}(k)\right)^{2}+\left(\alpha_{i \sigma}(k) I_{i \sigma}(k)\right)^{2}} \] Under the given assumptions, the spectral density has a Lorentz shape in the neighbourhood of the resonance \(E_{i \sigma}\). Note however, that for this to hold, in particular (3.342) must be fulfilled. This condition can however unfortunately be verified only after a complete solution of the problem is at hand. It thus remains speculative for the time being, but is satisfactorily confirmed by a number of examples of concrete applications. However, we shall also encounter systems for which the shape of the spectral density is found to be quite different from the Lorentz form, i.e. for which (3.342) is not obeyed (Fig. 3.4).

Fig. 3.4 The qualitative shape of the spectral density for a system of interacting Fermions

As a rule, however, according to our preliminary considerations, the spectral density will be a linear combination of weighted Lorentz and \(\delta\) -functions.

This structure of the spectral density will lead us in the next section to the concept of the quasi-particle. In this connection, it is interesting to examine the behaviour of the time-dependent spectral density, which one sometimes calls the propagator. (Occasionally, this term is also used for the time-dependent Green's function.) In the case of a non-interacting particle system, it represents a non-damped harmonic oscillation, as one can see from Eq. \((3.207) .\) This is true in the system of interacting particles only for case \(A ,\) when the spectral density can be written as a \(\delta\) -function (3.337). From (3.339), it then namely follows that: \[ S_{k \sigma}\left(t-t^{\prime}\right)=\frac{1}{2 \pi} \sum_{i=1}^{n} \alpha_{i \sigma}(k) \exp \left(-\frac{ i }{\hbar} E_{i \sigma}(k)\left(t-t^{\prime}\right)\right) \] The frequencies of oscillation are then determined by the resonance energies \(E_{i \sigma}(k)\)

For case \(B\), in contrast, the Lorentz peaks require exponentially damped oscillators. To see this, we assume for the moment that (3.345) holds to a good approximation over the entire energy range. Then we can write: \[ \begin{aligned} S_{k \sigma}^{(i)}\left(t-t^{\prime}\right) \approx & \frac{1}{4 \pi^{2} i } \int_{-\infty}^{+\infty} d E \exp \left(-\frac{ i }{\hbar} E\left(t-t^{\prime}\right)\right) \alpha_{i \sigma}( k ) \\ &\cdot\{ \frac{1}{E-\left(E_{i \sigma}( k )- i \alpha_{i \sigma}( k ) I_{i \sigma}( k )\right)}-\\ &\left.-\frac{1}{E-\left(E_{i \sigma}( k )+ i \alpha_{i \sigma}( k ) I_{i \sigma}( k )\right)}\right\} \end{aligned} \] We solve the integrals using the residual theorem. The spectral weights are positive definite, so that due to (3.333), we must have: \[ \alpha_{i \sigma}(k) I_{i \sigma}(k) \leq 0 \] The first term thus has a pole in the upper half-plane, and the second term has a pole in the lower half-plane. If we choose the following integration paths,

then the exponential function in (3.347) guarantees that the semicircle which is closed at infinity makes no contribution. According to the residual theorem, for \(t-t^{\prime}>0\) then only the second term makes a contribution to the integral in (3.347) whilst for \(t-t^{\prime}<0,\) only the first term contributes. This yields finally: \[ \begin{aligned} S_{k \sigma}^{(i)}\left(t-t^{\prime}\right) \approx & \frac{1}{2 \pi} \alpha_{i \sigma}( k ) \exp \left(-\frac{ i }{\hbar} E_{i \sigma}( k )\left(t-t^{\prime}\right)\right) \\ & \cdot \exp \left(-\frac{1}{\hbar}\left|\alpha_{i \sigma}( k ) I_{i \sigma}( k )\right|\left|t-t^{\prime}\right|\right) \end{aligned} \] The time-dependent spectral density indeed represents a damped oscillation, whose frequency again corresponds to a resonance \(E_{i \sigma},\) whilst the damping is essentially determined by the imaginary part of the self-energy.

Thus, in general for interacting systems, we can expect that \(S_{k \sigma}\left(t-t^{\prime}\right)\) will consist of a superposition of damped and undamped oscillations, whose frequencies correspond to the resonance energies \(E_{i \sigma}\). The resulting time dependence can then become quite complicated.

Precisely this qualitative picture of the time dependence of the spectral density will lead us in the next section to the concept of the quasi-particle, which is typical of many-body theory.

Quasi-particles

In this section, we will draw some preliminary conclusions. What is in fact the \(n e w\) aspect of the Green's function formalism as compared to conventional methods? What do Green's functions or spectral densities have to do with quasi-particles? What are quasi-particles after all? We presume that they are related to the more or less prominent resonance peaks in the spectral density which we discussed in the last section. We want to clarify this point qualitatively by considering a special case \[ T=0 ; \quad|k|>k_{ F } ; \quad t>t^{\prime} \] \(k_{ F }\) denotes the Fermi wavevector. We presume the system to be in its ground state \(\left|E_{0}\right\rangle .\) Upon adding a \(( k , \sigma)\) electron at the time \(t,\) the state \[ \left|\varphi_{0}(t)\right\rangle=a_{k \sigma}^{+}(t)\left|E_{0}\right\rangle \] is created, which must not necessarily be an eigenstate of the Hamiltonian. Owing to \(| k |>k_{ F },\) only one of the two terms in the definition of the propagator (3.127) \(S_{k \sigma}\left(t-t^{\prime}\right)\) can contribute. We therefore have \[ 2 \pi S_{k \sigma}\left(t-t^{\prime}\right)=\left\langle\varphi_{0}(t) \mid \varphi_{0}\left(t^{\prime}\right)\right\rangle \] With this, the time-dependent spectral density acquires a clear interpretation. \(2 \pi S_{k \sigma}\left(t-t^{\prime}\right)\) is the probability amplitude that the state \(\left|\varphi_{0}\right\rangle,\) which was formed from the ground state \(\left|E_{0}\right\rangle\) by adding a \((k, \sigma)\) electron at the time \(t^{\prime},\) still exists at a time \(t>t^{\prime} . S_{k \sigma}\left(t-t^{\prime}\right)\) thus characterises the time evolution (propagation)

of an additional \(( k , \sigma)\) electron in the \(N\) -particle system. If we had presupposed \(|k|<k_{ F },\) then \(S_{k \sigma}\left(t-t^{\prime}\right)\) would describe the propagation of a hole. We must now distinguish between two typical cases: \[ \left|\left\langle\varphi_{0}(t) \mid \varphi_{0}\left(t^{\prime}\right)\right\rangle\right|^{2}=\text { const } \Longleftrightarrow \text { stationary state } \] \[ \left|\left\langle\varphi_{0}(t) \mid \varphi_{0}\left(t^{\prime}\right)\right\rangle\right|^{2} \underset{t-t^{\prime} \rightarrow \infty}{\longrightarrow} 0 \Longleftrightarrow \text { state with a finite lifetime. } \]

First, we again consider

(1) Non-interacting electrons

described by \[ H _{0}=\sum_{k, \sigma}(\varepsilon( k )-\mu) a_{k \sigma}^{+} a_{k \sigma} \] One readily obtains \[ \left[ H _{0}, a_{k \sigma}^{+}\right]_{-}=(\varepsilon( k )-\mu) a_{k \sigma}^{+} \] with which we find \[ \begin{aligned} H _{0}\left(a_{k \sigma}^{+}\left|E_{0}\right\rangle\right) &=a_{k \sigma}^{+} H _{0}\left|E_{0}\right\rangle+\left[ H _{0}, a_{k \sigma}^{+}\right]_{-}\left|E_{0}\right\rangle=\\ &=\left(E_{0}+\varepsilon( k )-\mu\right)\left(a_{k \sigma}^{+}\left|E_{0}\right\rangle\right) \end{aligned} \] In this special case, we thus have \(a_{k \sigma}^{+}\left|E_{0}\right\rangle\) which is an eigenstate of \(H _{0}\). Continuing, we obtain: \[ \begin{aligned} \left|\varphi_{0}(t)\right\rangle &=\exp \left(\frac{ i }{\hbar} H _{0} t\right) a_{ k \sigma}^{+} \exp \left(-\frac{ i }{\hbar} H _{0} t\right)\left|E_{0}\right\rangle=\\ &=\exp \left(-\frac{ i }{\hbar} E_{0} t\right) \exp \left(\frac{ i }{\hbar} H _{0} t\right)\left(a_{ k \sigma}^{+}\left|E_{0}\right\rangle\right)=\\ &=\exp \left(\frac{ i }{\hbar}(\varepsilon( k )-\mu) t\right)\left(a_{k \sigma}^{+}\left|E_{0}\right\rangle\right) \end{aligned} \] Due to \(|k|>k_{ F }\) and \(\left\langle E_{0} \mid E_{0}\right\rangle=1,\) we also have: \[ \left\langle E_{0}\left|a_{k \sigma} a_{k \sigma}^{+}\right| E_{0}\right\rangle=\left\langle E_{0} \mid E_{0}\right\rangle-\left\langle E_{0}\left|a_{k \sigma}^{+} a_{k \sigma}\right| E_{0}\right\rangle=1 \] We then finally obtain: \[ \left\langle\varphi_{0}(t) \mid \varphi_{0}\left(t^{\prime}\right)\right\rangle=\exp \left[-\frac{ i }{\hbar}(\varepsilon( k )-\mu)\left(t-t^{\prime}\right)\right] \] The propagator \(S_{k \sigma}^{(0)}\left(t-t^{\prime}\right)\) thus represents an undamped harmonic oscillation, as we have already seen by another method. Its frequency corresponds to an exact excitation energy of the system, namely \((\varepsilon( k )-\mu) .\) Because of \[ \left|\left\langle\varphi_{0}(t) \mid \varphi_{0}\left(t^{\prime}\right)\right\rangle\right|^{2}=1 \] this is a stationary state (see Fig. 3.5 ).
