1 Quantum statistical physics

1.1. Classical statistical physics

Classical statistical physics tells us that in the grand canonical ensemble, the expectation value of a thermal dynamic quantity, which will be called X here, can be written as: \[ \langle X\rangle=\frac{\sum_{u} X_{n} \exp \left[-\beta\left(E_{n}-\mu N_{n}\right]\right]}{\sum_{n} \exp \left[-\beta\left(E_{n}-\mu N_{n}\right)\right]} \] where

  • \(\beta=1 / T\) (here the Boltzmann constant \(k_{B}\) is set to unity)
  • \(\mu\) is the chemical potential.
  • Here, we sum over all possible states of the system ( \(\Sigma_{n}\) ).
  • For the n-th state, the total energy of the whole system is \(E_{n}\);
  • the particle number is \(N_{n}\) and the value of the quantity \(X\) is \(X_{n}\).

1.2. Quantum statistical physics

For a quantum system (at finite temperature \(T\) ), we can rewrite this formula in terms of quantum operators \[ \langle X\rangle=\frac{\sum_{n}\langle n|\hat{X} \exp [-\beta(\hat{H}-\mu \hat{N})]| n\rangle}{\sum_{n}\langle n|\exp [-\beta(\hat{H}-\mu \hat{N})]| n\rangle}=\frac{\operatorname{Tr}(\hat{X} \exp [-\beta(\hat{H}-\mu \hat{N})]\}}{\operatorname{Tr}\{\exp [-\beta(\hat{H}-\mu \hat{N})]\}} \] where

  • \(\Sigma_{n}\) sums over a complete orthonormal basis of the Hilbert space \(\{|n\rangle\} .\)
  • $ $ is the Hamiltonian.
  • \(\hat{N}\) is the particle number operator
  • \(\hat{X}\) is the quantum operator of the quantity that we want to compute.
  • The sum \(\sum_{n}\langle n|\ldots| n\rangle\) is also known as a trace Tr.

It is easy to verify that the above formula for \(\langle X\rangle\) is independent of the choice of basis. Here, we will not prove this operator formula, because it is not part of the main focus of this lecture. Instead, we will only demonstrate that by choosing a proper basis, this (quantum) operator formula recovers the classical formula shown above, and therefore, the operator formula is a natural quantum generalization of classical statistical physics.

eigen-energy basis

If \([\hat{H}, \hat{N}]=0,\) we can choose the common eigenstates of \(\hat{H}\) and \(\hat{N}\) as our basis. In this basis, we have \[ \hat{H}|n\rangle=E_{n}|n\rangle \] and \[ \hat{N}|n\rangle=N_{n}|n\rangle \] where \(E_{n}\) is the eigen-energy and \(N_{n}\) is the eigen-value of the particle number operator. Using this basis, it is easy to check that \[ \exp [-\beta(\hat{H}-\mu \hat{N})]|n\rangle=\exp \left[-\beta\left(E_{n}-\mu N_{n}\right)\right]|n\rangle \] and therefore \[ \begin{aligned} \langle X\rangle&=\frac{\sum_{n}\langle n|\hat{X} \exp [-\beta(\hat{H}-\mu \hat{N})]| n\rangle}{\sum_{n}\langle n|\exp [-\beta(\hat{H}-\mu \hat{N})]| | n\rangle} \\&=\frac{\sum_{n}\langle n|\hat{X}| n\rangle \exp \left[-\beta\left(E_{n}-\mu N_{n}\right)\right]}{\sum_{n}\langle n | n\rangle \exp \left[-\beta\left(E_{n}-\mu N_{n}\right)\right]} \\&=\frac{\sum_{n} X_{n} \exp \left[-\beta\left(E_{n}-\mu N_{n}\right)\right]}{\sum_{n} \exp \left[-\beta\left(E_{n}-\mu N_{n}\right)\right]} \end{aligned} \] Here, we define \(X_{n}=\langle n|\hat{X}| n\rangle\)

particle conservation

Note: the quantum field theory used in particle physics is the \(\mathrm{T} \rightarrow 0\) limit of this finite temperature theory.

Q: What happens if \([\hat{H}, \hat{N}] \neq 0 ?\)

A: If \([\hat{H}, \hat{N}] \neq 0,\) it implies that in this system, the particle number is not conserved. As will be discussed in later chapters, particle conservation law is related with the \(U(1)\) phase symmetry for charge neutral particles (or the \(U(1)\) gauge symmetry for charged particles). The absences of particle conservation implies the breaking of phase/gauge symmetry, which means that the system is a superfluid/superconductor.

2. Second quantization

The terminology of "second quantization" is due to historical reasons. We are NOT quantizing something for a second time. We are just using a new basis to handle indistinguishable particles.

Q: Why do we want to use second quantization? A: It is the most convenient way to handle a large number of indistinguishable particles.

2.1. wavefunctions for distinguishable particles

It is easy to write down quantum wavefunctions for systems composed by distinguishable particles. For example, if we have two distinguishable particles, particle one in state \(\psi_{1}\) and particle two in \(\psi_{2}\), the wavefunction is \(\Psi\left(r_{1}, r_{2}\right)=\psi_{1}\left(r_{1}\right) \psi_{2}\left(r_{2}\right)\) If we have \(n\) distinguishable particles, the wavefunction can be written as \(\Psi\left(r_{1}, r_{2}, r_{3}, \ldots, r_{n}\right)=\psi_{1}\left(r_{1}\right) \psi_{2}\left(r_{2}\right) \ldots \psi_{n}\left(r_{n}\right)\) Here, the ith particle is in the quantum state \(\psi_{i}\) for \(i=1,2, \ldots\) n.

摘要

微扰理论的物理图像是“准粒子”。准粒子和电子拥有一样的电荷和自旋，但是准粒子的色散和有效质量都会重整化。

因为在费米狄拉克分布中存在极点。，所以这部分为计算积分提供的数学准备：复变函数中的留数定理。这个定理说的是，环路积分的值和这个环路中包含的极点的留数有关。

Hartree-Fock近似的部分

  1. 我们一开始就证明了我们可以把相互作用的粒子当作自由粒子处理，只是粒子的色散从 \(\epsilon(k)\) 变成了 \(\epsilon(k)-\int d q v(k-q)\langle n(q)\rangle\)。

  5. 最后我们计算了在Random Phase approximation下的Green funtion，得到准粒子重整化的那些色散和有效质量。

1 Can we apply the free fermion approximation to a real metal?

忽略费米子相互作用的情况容易计算，但是对于真正的金属材料，为了更好的接近实验，我们可以考虑微弱相互作用。 对此我们把物理量进行微扰展开。一阶微扰就是Hartree-Fock近似，一阶微扰中的第1项Hartree term是常数，第2项Fock term很重要，但是需要更繁杂的计算。

Can we ignore interactions in a real metal? Experiments says yes (free fermion models work very well), but the theoretical answer is much more involving.

1.1. Strong interaction vs. weak interaction

Q: Does interaction get stronger when electron density is higher?

naive answer

A naive answer: Interaction is proportional to \(1 / r,\) where \(r\) is the separation between two electrons. Higher density means that the average separation between electrons is smaller, so higher density means stronger interaction.

In an electron gas, the average distance between electrons \(r_{s}\) can be estimated as \[ \frac{V}{N}=\frac{4 \pi}{3} r_{S}{ }^{3} \] where \(V / N\) is the average volume occupied by one electron, and the density \(n\) is \(n=N / V .\) Thus \[ r_{s}=\left(\frac{3 V}{4 \pi N}\right)^{1 / 3}=\left(\frac{3}{4 \pi n}\right)^{1 / 3} \] The average Coulomb interaction one electron feels can be estimated as \[ \frac{E_{\text {int }}}{N} \propto \frac{e^{2}}{r_{s}}=e^{2}\left(\frac{4 \pi}{3} n\right)^{1 / 3} \propto n^{1 / 3} \] Small \(r_{s}\) (or high density \(n\) ) means stronger interaction.

opposite answer

However, the truth is opposite, because

\[ H=H_{ KE }+H_{ Int } \]

We need to compare interaction with the kinetic energy to determine whether we have a strongly correlated system or a weakly correlated system.

  • Strong correlation means \(H_{ Int }>> H _{ KE }\)
  • weak correlation means \(H_{ KE }>> H _{ int }\).

The total kinetic energy in a Fermi gas is also a function of \(r_{s}\) (or say density) \[ \begin{aligned} K . E .&=2 V \int_{\epsilon<\epsilon_{F}} \frac{d^{3} k}{(2 \pi)^{3}} \frac{k^{2}}{2 m}\\&=2 V \int_{0}^{k_{F}} \frac{k^{4} d k}{4 \pi^{2} m}\\&=V \frac{k_{F}^{5}}{10 \pi^{2} m} \end{aligned} \] The particle number is \[ \begin{aligned} N&=2 V \int_{\epsilon<\epsilon F} \frac{d^{3} k}{(2 \pi)^{3}} \\&=2 V \int_{0}^{k_{F}} \frac{k^{2} d k}{2 \pi^{2} m} \\&=V \frac{k_{F}^{3}}{3 \pi^{2}} \end{aligned} \] So ,$ K . E .$ per particle \[ \begin{aligned} \frac{K . E}{N}&=\frac{V \frac{k_{F}^{5}}{10 \pi^{2} m}}{V \frac{k_{F}^{3}}{3 \pi^{2} m}} \\&=\frac{3 k_{F}^{2}}{10 m} \propto \frac{1}{m r_{S}^{2}} \end{aligned} \] More precisely, \[ k_{F}=\left(3 \pi^{2} \frac{N}{V}\right)^{1 / 3} \] Comparing with \(r_{s}=\left(\frac{3 V}{4 \pi N}\right)^{1 / 3}=\left(\frac{3}{4 \pi n}\right)^{1 / 3}\) \[ k_{F}=\frac{1}{r_{s}}\left(\frac{9 \pi}{4}\right)^{1 / 3} \] So kinetic energy per particle is \[ \begin{aligned} \frac{K \cdot E}{N}&=\frac{3 k_{F}^{2}}{10 m}\\&=\frac{3}{10}\left(\frac{9 \pi}{4}\right)^{2 / 3} \frac{1}{m r_{S}^{2}} \end{aligned} \] The ratio between \(K\). E. and interaction energy, \[ \begin{aligned} \frac{\text { Interaction }}{K . E .} \propto &\frac{e^{2}}{r_{S}} / \frac{1}{m r_{S}^{2}} \\&=r_{S} / \frac{1}{m e^{2}}\\&=\frac{r_{S}}{a_{0}} \end{aligned} \] Here \(a_{0}=1 / m e^{2}=0.529 \AA .\)

  1. When \(r_{S}>> a _{0}\) (low density), we have a strongly correlated system with the energy of interactions \(>> K\). E.
  2. When \(r_{s}<< a _{0}\) (high density), we have a weakly correlated systems with K.E.\(>>\) interaction.

Lower density means stronger interaction.

1.2. Perturbation theory

the diagrammatic expansion of Feynman is in fact a power-law expansion of interaction strength \(V\). Because \(\frac{\text { Interaction }}{K E .} \sim \frac{r_{S}}{a_{0}},\) the small parameter in this expansion is in fact \(r_{S} / a_{0}\) \[ G=G_{0}+\# \frac{r_{S}}{a_{0}}+\#\left(\frac{r_{S}}{a_{0}}\right)^{2}+\#\left(\frac{r_{S}}{a_{0}}\right)^{3}+\ldots \]

  1. If \(r_{S}<< a _{0}\) (the high density limit), this expansion converge, i.e. higher order terms are small and can be dropped. In this regime, the free fermion approximation (ignoring interactions) is obviously a good approximation and the error bar is about \(r_{S} / a_{0}\).

  2. If \(r_{S}>> a _{0}\) (the low density regime), one should use interactions the non-perturbed Hamiltonian and treat the kinetic energy as perturbation. There, to the leading order, we can set the kinetic energy to zero. The ground state is easy to find. Because electrons want to say as far away from each other as possible to reduce the interaction energy, in \(2 D\), they just form a close pack structure (a triangular lattice). This state is known as a Wigner crystal. Please notice that it is NOT a crystal of atoms. It is a crystal made by electrons, and the lattice back ground is irrelevant here.

Reality: In typical metals, \(2<r_{S} / a_{0}<6\). Neither too small, nor too large. We can not ignore interactions, neither can be ignore the kinetic energy.

With such a strong interaction, we cannot ignore interactions. So let's go to the first order and see what we get.

1.3 Hartree-Fock approximation

The first order perturbation theory here is known as the Hartree-Fock approximation.

In this section, we compute the first order perturbation theory using quantum field theory we learned in the previous chapter. The final conclusion of this section is, after we include the first order corrections, the theory is even worse. In fact, when we compare the theoretical results with experimental data, the free fermion theory works much better, and the Hartree-Fock approximation generates wrong results. This is due to the special property of \(1 / r\) interactions.

Coulomb interaction in the momentum space

What is the Coulomb interaction in the \(k -\omega\) space, \(V\left(q, i \Omega_{n}\right) ?\) \[ V(r)=\frac{e^{2}}{r} \] Define \(U(r)=\frac{e}{r},\) which is the potential induced by a point charge \(e .\) Maxwell told us that \[ \nabla^{2} U(r)=-4 \pi e \delta(r) \] In q-space \[ -q^{2} U(q)=-4 \pi e \] Therefore, \[ U(q)=4 \pi \frac{e}{q^{2}} \] Notice that \(V(r)=e U(r),\) so \(V(q)=e U(q)\) \[ V(q)=\frac{4 \pi e^{2}}{q^{2}} \] This is our \(V\left(q, i \Omega_{n}\right)\) \[ V\left(q, i \Omega_{n}\right)=V(q)=\frac{4 \pi e^{2}}{q^{2}} \]

  • Note 1: \(V\left(q, i \Omega_{n}\right)\) is independent of \(i \Omega_{n}\). This is because that Coulomb interaction is instantaneous (a good approximation in non-relativistic systems).
  • Note 2 \(: V(q)\) is a singular function at \(q \rightarrow 0\).

Hartree-Fock term

Consider these diagrams

Using Dyson's equation, we find that \[ G^{ HF }\left(k, i \omega_{n}\right)=\frac{1}{\omega-\epsilon(k)-\Sigma_{ HF }\left(k, i \omega_{n}\right)} \] where \[ \begin{aligned} \Sigma_{ HF }\left(k, i \omega_{n}\right)=&\mp \int \frac{d q}{(2 \pi)^{3}} \frac{1}{\beta} \sum_{i \Omega_{n}} V(0,0) G^{(0)}\left(q, i \Omega_{n}\right)\\&-\int \frac{d q}{(2 \pi)^{3}} \frac{1}{\beta} \sum_{i \Omega_{n}} G^{(0)}\left(k+q, i \omega_{n}+i \Omega_{n}\right) V\left(q, i \Omega_{n}\right) \end{aligned} \]

  1. The first term (the Hartree term) is a constant, independent of \(k\) and \(\omega_{n}\). This term just shift the single particle energy \(\epsilon(k)\) by a constant, which has no physical impact.

  2. The second term (the Fock term) is more important.

\[ \begin{aligned} \Sigma_{F}\left(k, i \omega_{n}\right)&=\frac{3 k_{F}^{2}}{10 m} \\&=-\int \frac{d q}{(2 \pi)^{3}} \frac{1}{\beta} \sum_{i \Omega_{n}} G^{(0)}\left(k+q, i \omega_{n}+i \Omega_{n}\right) V\left(q, i \Omega_{n}\right) \\&=-\int \frac{d q}{(2 \pi)^{3}} \frac{1}{\beta} \sum_{i \Omega_{n}} G^{(0)}\left(k+q, i \Omega_{n}\right) V\left(q, i \Omega_{n}-i \omega_{n}\right) \end{aligned} \]

2 Frequency sum

因为在费米狄拉克分布中存在极点。，所以这部分为计算积分提供的数学准备：复变函数中的留数定理。这个定理说的是，环路积分的值和这个环路中包含的极点的留数有关。

Fock term \[ \Sigma_{F}\left(k, i \omega_{n}\right)=-\int \frac{d q}{(2 \pi)^{3}} \frac{1}{\beta} \sum_{i \Omega_{n}} G^{(0)}\left(k+q, i \Omega_{n}\right) V\left(q, i \Omega_{n}-i \omega_{n}\right) \] The sum of frequency here \(\sum_{i \Omega_{n}}\) sums over all fermionic Matsubara frequencies (because \(i \Omega_{n}\) is the frequency of a fermionic Green's function \(G^{(0)}\) ).

In general, if we consider an arbitrary function \(f\left(i \Omega_{n}\right)\) and sums over all fermionic Matsubara frequencies, we get \[ \sum_{i \Omega_{n}} f\left(i \Omega_{n}\right)=\sum_{n} f\left(\frac{2 n+1}{\beta} \pi-i \mu\right) \] Q: How should we compute the sum?

2.1 Cauchy's residue theorem

For a complex function \(f(z)\), there is a very famous and very important integral formula, known as Cauchy's residue theorem. We can use this formula to compute the frequency sum. Let's consider a complex function \(f(z)\) and a contour on the complex plan \(C\). We assign a direction for the contour \(C\) and compute the integral \(\oint_{C} f(z) .\) Here, the integral is computed along the direction of \(C .\)

Inside and outside

a contour separate the complex plane into two regions: inside and outside. If we follow the direction of the contour, the region on the right hand side of the contour is called "inside" and the other side is "outside". Notice that if we flip the direction of the contour \(C ,\) we flip inside and outside.

Here, we emphasize that inside and outside are relative concepts. The inside of \(C\) is the same as the outside of \(-C\).

power series at singularity

Assume that inside/outside the contour \(C, f(z)\) is an analytic function except for some discrete singularity points \(z_{m},\) at which \(f\left(z_{m}\right) \rightarrow \infty .\) At each singularity point, we write \(f(z)\) as a power series. \[ f(z)=\ldots+\frac{a_{-n}}{\left(z-z_{m}\right)^{n}}+\ldots+\frac{a_{-1}}{z-z_{m}}+a_{0}+a_{1}\left(z-z_{m}\right)+\ldots+a_{n}\left(z-z_{m}\right)^{n}+\ldots \] The value of \(a_{-1}\) is known as the residue at \(z_{m}, \operatorname{Res}\left(f, z_{m}\right)\). Another way to define the residue is \[ \operatorname{Res}\left(f, z_{m}\right)=\lim _{z \rightarrow z_{m}}\left(z-z_{m}\right) f(z) \]

Cauchy's residue theorem

One can prove that \[ \oint_{C} f(z)=2 \pi i \sum_{m \text { inside } C} \operatorname{Res}\left(f, z_{m}\right) \] and this is known as the Cauchy's residue theorem. We can do the same thing for contour - \(C\) (flip the direction of \(C\) ) \[ \oint_{-C} f(z)=2 \pi i \sum_{m \text { inside }-C} \operatorname{Res}\left(f, z_{m}\right) \] Notice that \(\oint_{C} f(z)=-\oint_{-C} f(z)\) and inside of \(-C\) is just outside of \(C\) \[ \oint_{C} f(z)=-2 \pi i \sum_{m \text { outside } C} \operatorname{Res}\left(f, z_{m}\right) \] This proves one conclusion, which we will use later \[ \sum_{m \text { inside } C} \operatorname{Res}\left(f, z_{m}\right)=-\sum_{m \text { outside } C} \operatorname{Res}\left(f, z_{m}\right) \]

This singularity points are known as the poles, and thus Cauchy told us that the integral of f(z) are purely determined by the residue at the poles.

2.2 Residue of the Fermi distribution function

Now consider the Fermi distribution function, \[ f(z)=\frac{1}{\exp [\beta(z-\mu)]+1} \]

singularity points

Q: What are the singularity points of \(f(z) ?\)

A: fermionic Matsubara frequencies

This is because for \(f(z),\) the singular points must satisfy \(\exp [\beta(z-\mu)]+1=0\) \[ \exp [\beta(z-\mu)]=-1=\exp [(2 n+1) \pi i] \] where \(n\) is an integer, so \[ \beta(z-\mu)=(2 n+1) \pi i \] thus \[ z=\frac{(2 n+1) \pi i}{\beta}+\mu=i \omega_{n} \]

the residue

Q: What is the residue? A: $ (f, _{n})=-1 / $

This is because \[ \begin{aligned} f(z)&=\frac{1}{\exp [\beta(z-\mu)]+1} \\&=\frac{1}{\exp \left[\beta\left(z-i \omega_{n}\right)+\left(i \omega_{n}-\mu\right)\right]+1} \\&=\frac{1}{\exp \left[\beta\left(z-i \omega_{n}\right)\right] \exp \left[\left(i \omega_{n}-\mu\right)\right]+1} \\&=\frac{1}{1-\exp \left[\beta\left(z-i \omega_{n}\right)\right]} \\&=\frac{1}{1-\left\{1+\beta\left(z-i \omega_{n}\right)+O\left(z-i \omega_{n}\right)^{2}\right\}} \\&=-\frac{1}{\beta\left(z-i \omega_{n}\right)+O\left(z-i \omega_{n}\right)^{2}} \\&=-\frac{1}{\beta\left(z-i \omega_{n}\right)}+O\left(z-i \omega_{n}\right)^{0} \end{aligned} \]

2.3. Integrals that involve \(f(z)\)

Now, lets consider an arbitrary function \(g(z)\) and we assume that \(g(z)\) is an analytic function near \(i \omega_{n}\). We draw a small circle around each \(i \omega_{n}\) on the complex plane and define our contour \(C\) as the combination of all these small circles. We choose the direction such that all of \(i \omega_{n}\) are inside our contour \(C\). Now we consider the integral \(\oint_{C} f(z) g(z),\) where \(f(z)\) is the Fermi distribution function. \[ \oint_{C} f(z) g(z)=2 \pi i \sum_{m \text { inside } C} \operatorname{Res}\left(f g, z_{m}\right) \] If the circles are small enough, \(g(z)\) would not give as any singularity inside \(C\). Therefore, all the singularity points comes from \(f(z),\) which is singular at \(i \omega_{n}\). \[ \oint_{C} f(z) g(z)=2 \pi i \sum_{i \omega_{n}} \operatorname{Res}\left(f g, i \omega_{n}\right) \] Because \(g(z)\) is analytic around \(i \omega_{n}\), all the residue comes from the singularity of \(f(z)\) \[ \begin{aligned} \oint_{C} f(z) g(z)&=2 \pi i \sum_{i \omega_{n}} \operatorname{Res}\left(f g, i \omega_{n}\right) \\&=2 \pi i \sum_{i \omega_{n}} g\left(i \omega_{n}\right) \operatorname{Res}\left(f, i \omega_{n}\right) \\&=-\frac{2 \pi i}{\beta} \sum_{i \omega_{n}} g\left(i \omega_{n}\right) \end{aligned} \] This can be proved using \[ \begin{aligned} \operatorname{Res}\left(f g, i \omega_{n}\right)&=\lim _{z \rightarrow i \omega_{n}}\left(z-i \omega_{n}\right) f(z) g(z) \\&=g\left(i \omega_{n}\right) \lim _{z \rightarrow i \omega_{n}}\left(z-i \omega_{n}\right) f(z) \\&=g\left(i \omega_{n}\right) \operatorname{Res}\left(f, i \omega_{n}\right) \\&=-\frac{1}{\beta}g\left(i \omega_{n}\right) \end{aligned} \] Therefore \[ \frac{1}{\beta} \sum_{i \omega_{n}} g\left(i \omega_{n}\right)=-\frac{1}{2 \pi i} \oint_{C} f(z) g(z) \] This means that if we want to compute the frequency sum for a function \(g(z),\) as long as \(g(z)\) is analytic around \(i \omega_{n}\), we can turn the sum into an integral. In fact, we can do better than that, notice that \(\oint_{C}=-\oint_{-C}\) \[ \frac{1}{\beta} \sum_{i \omega_{n}} g\left(i \omega_{n}\right)=-\frac{1}{2 \pi i} \oint_{C} f(z) g(z)=\frac{1}{2 \pi i} \oint_{-C} f(z) g(z) \] Here, we can use Cauchy's residue theorem again, but for - \(C\). \[ \begin{aligned} \frac{1}{\beta} \sum_{i \omega_{n}} g\left(i \omega_{n}\right)&=\frac{1}{2 \pi i} \oint_{-C} f(z) g(z) \\&=\sum_{m \text { inside }-C} \operatorname{Res}\left(f g, z_{m}\right) \\&=\sum_{m \text { outside } C} \operatorname{Res}\left(f g, z_{m}\right) \end{aligned} \] Here, because \(f(z)\) is analytic outside \(C ,\) all the residue should come from the singular points of function \(g(z)\). \[ \frac{1}{\beta} \sum_{i \omega_{n}} g\left(i \omega_{n}\right)=\sum_{m \text { outside } C} \operatorname{Res}\left(f g, z_{m}\right)=\sum_{m \text { outside } C} f\left(z_{m}\right) \operatorname{Res}\left(g, z_{m}\right) \] This is the way that we use to compute frequency sums.

To compute \(\frac{1}{\beta} \sum_{i \omega_{n}} g\left(i \omega_{n}\right)\)

  • Step 1 : analytic continuation: \(g(z)\)
  • Step 2 : find the pole of \(g(z)\) and compute \(\sum_{m \text { outside } C} f\left(z_{m}\right) \operatorname{Res}\left(g, z_{m}\right),\) where \(f(z)\) is the Fermi distribution function.

Q: How about bosons?

A: One should use the Bose distribution function. \[ f(z)=\frac{1}{\exp [\beta(z-\mu)]-1} \] Note: if \(g(z)\) has branch cut, one needs to make sure that we avoid the branch cut, which leads to additional contributions. This effect is important for some calculations but will not be discussed in this lecture.

3 Hartree-Fock approximation

the Green function

\[ G^{ HF }\left(k, i \omega_{n}\right)=\frac{1}{\omega-\epsilon(k)-\Sigma_{ HF }\left(k, i \omega_{n}\right)} \]

  • where

\[ \begin{aligned} \Sigma_{ HF }\left(k, i \omega_{n}\right)=&\mp \int \frac{d q}{(2 \pi)^{3}} \frac{1}{\beta} \sum_{i \Omega_{n}} V(0,0) G^{(0)}\left(q, i \Omega_{n}\right)\\&-\int \frac{d q}{(2 \pi)^{3}} \frac{1}{\beta} \sum_{i \Omega_{n}} G^{(0)}\left(k+q, i \omega_{n}+i \Omega_{n}\right) V\left(q, i \Omega_{n}\right) \end{aligned} \]

Now let's compute the frequency sum of the Fock term \[ \Sigma_{F}\left(k, i \omega_{n}\right)=-\int \frac{d q}{(2 \pi)^{3}} \frac{1}{\beta} \sum_{i \Omega_{n}} G^{(0)}\left(k+q, i \Omega_{n}\right) V\left(q, i \Omega_{n}-i \omega_{n}\right) \]

  • where

\[ \frac{1}{\beta} \sum_{i \Omega_{n}} G^{(0)}\left(k+q, i \Omega_{n}\right) V(q)=\sum_{m \text { outside } C} f\left(z_{m}\right) V(q) \operatorname{Res}\left[G^{(0)}(k+q, z), z_{m}\right] \]

Here, \(V\) is independent of \(\omega\) and thus cannot give us any pole on the complex \(\omega\) plane. The residue can only comes from \(G^{(0)}(k+q, z)=\frac{1}{z-\epsilon(k+q)} .\) This function has only one singular point at \(z=\epsilon(k+q)\) and the residue here is obviously \(1 .\) \[ \frac{1}{\beta} \sum_{i \Omega_{n}} G^{(0)}\left(k+q, i \Omega_{n}\right) V(q)=f[\epsilon(k+q)] V(q)=f[\epsilon(k+q)] V(q) \] Therefore, \[ \begin{aligned} \Sigma_{F}\left(k, i \omega_{n}\right)&=-\int \frac{d q}{(2 \pi)^{3}} V(q) f[\epsilon(k+q)]\\ &=-\int \frac{d q}{(2 \pi)^{3}} V(k-q) f[\epsilon(q)]\\ &=-\int \frac{d q}{(2 \pi)^{3}} V(k-q)\langle n(q)\rangle \end{aligned} \] In the last step, I used the fact that \(f[\epsilon(q)]\) is the average occupation number for the quantum state with momentum \(q\). So the Green's function is \[ G_{ HF }\left(k, i \omega_{n}\right)=\frac{1}{i \omega_{n}-\epsilon(k)-\Sigma_{ HF }(k)} \]

This means that within the Hartree-Fock approximation (first order perturbation theory), we can still think of our interacting particles as "free particles", but the dispersion relation is shifted from \(\epsilon(k)\) into \(\epsilon(k)-\int d q v(k-q)\langle n(q)\rangle\)

3.1 mean-field & H-F approximation

Consider a system with charge distribution \(n(r),\) the potential energy felt by an electron at \(r_{0}\) is \[ \delta E =\int d r v\left(r-r_{0}\right) n(r) \] So interactions with other electrons shift the energy of the electron we study by \(\delta E =\int d q v\left(r-r_{0}\right) n(r) .\) The HF approximation is the momentum space version of this formula. In a real system, one electron feel the electric field induced by other electrons. This field varies with time \(t\), because other electrons are moving. The H-F approximation assumes that we can substitute this varying electric by its average value, which is time-independent. Therefore, in the H-F approximation, we treat all other electrons as a static (not moving) and uniform background. This charge background generate a static field, which shift the energy of our electron. Because we only consider the average field, this approximation is also known as the mean-field approximation. \[ \begin{aligned} \Sigma_{F}\left(k, i \omega_{n}\right)&=-\int \frac{d q}{(2 \pi)^{3}} V(k-q)\langle n(q)\rangle\\ &=-4 \pi \int d q \frac{e^{2}}{(|\vec{k}-\vec{q}|)^{2}}\langle n(q)\rangle \end{aligned} \] Here, the minus sign comes from the anti-commutator.

zero temperature

At \(T=0,\langle n(q)\rangle=1\) for \(q<k_{F}\) and \(\langle n(q)\rangle=0\) for \(q>k_{F},\) so \[ \begin{aligned} \Sigma_{F}\left(k, i \omega_{n}\right)&=-4 \pi e^{2} \int \frac{d q}{(2 \pi)^{3}} \frac{1}{(|\vec{k}-\vec{q}|)^{2}}\langle n(q)\rangle\\ &=-4 \pi e^{2} \int_{q<q_{F}} \frac{d q}{(2 \pi)^{3}} \frac{1}{(|\vec{k}-\vec{q}|)^{2}} \end{aligned} \] Let's assign the direction of \(k\) to be the \(z\) direction \[ \begin{aligned} \Sigma_{F}\left(k, i \omega_{n}\right)&=-4 \pi e^{2} \int \frac{d q}{(2 \pi)^{3}} \frac{1}{(|\vec{k}-\vec{q}|)^{2}}\langle n(q)\rangle\\ &=-4 \pi e^{2} \int_{0}^{k_{F}} \frac{q^{2} d q}{(2 \pi)^{3}} \int_{0}^{\pi} \sin \theta d \theta \int_{0}^{2 \pi} d \phi \frac{q^{2}}{(k-q \cos \theta)^{2}+q^{2} \sin ^{2} \theta}\\ &=-8 \pi^{2} e^{2} \int_{0}^{k_{F}} \frac{d q}{(2 \pi)^{3}} \int_{-1}^{1} d \cos \theta \frac{e^{2}}{k^{2}+q^{2}-2 k q \cos \theta}\\ &=-\frac{e^{2}}{\pi} \int_{0}^{k_{F}} d q\left(-\frac{q}{2 k}\right) \ln \left|\frac{k^{2}+q^{2}-2 k q}{k^{2}+q^{2}-2 k q}\right|\\ &=-\frac{e^{2}}{\pi} \int_{0}^{k_{F}} d q \frac{q}{k} \ln \left|\frac{k+q}{k-q}\right|\\ &=\frac{e^{2}}{\pi} k_{F}\left(1+\frac{k_{F}^{2}-k^{2}}{2 k k_{F}} \ln \left|\frac{k_{F}+k}{k_{F}-k}\right|\right) \end{aligned} \] Define \(x=k / k_{F}\) \[ \begin{aligned} \Sigma_{F}\left(k, i \omega_{n}\right)&=-\frac{e^{2}}{\pi} k_{F}\left(1+\frac{k_{F}^{2}-k^{2}}{2 k k_{F}} \ln \left|\frac{k_{F}+k}{k_{F}-k}\right|\right)\\ &=-\frac{e^{2}}{\pi} k_{F}\left(1+\frac{1-x^{2}}{2 x} \ln \left|\frac{1+x}{1-x}\right|\right) \end{aligned} \] So our new particles have dispersion \[ \begin{aligned} \epsilon_{ eff }(k)&=\epsilon(k)+\Sigma(k)\\ &=\epsilon(k)-\frac{e^{2}}{\pi} k_{F}\left(1+\frac{k_{F}^{2}-k^{2}}{2 k k_{F}} \ln \left|\frac{k_{F}+k}{k_{F}-k}\right|\right) \end{aligned} \] So the effective velocity of our particles are \[ \begin{aligned} v(k)&=\frac{d \epsilon_{ eff }(k)}{d k} \\&=\frac{d \epsilon(k)}{d k}-\frac{e^{2}}{\pi} k_{F} \frac{d}{d k}\left(1+\frac{k_{F}^{2}-k^{2}}{2 k k_{F}} \ln \left|\frac{k_{F}+k}{k_{F}-k}\right|\right) \\&=v_{ non - interacting }(k)-\frac{e^{2} k_{F}}{\pi} \frac{2 k k_{F}-\left(k^{2}+k_{F}^{2}\right) \ln \left|\frac{k_{F}+k}{k_{F}-k}\right|}{2 k^{2} k_{F}} \end{aligned} \] The Fermi velocity is \[ \begin{aligned} v_{F}&=\lim _{k \rightarrow k_{F}} v(k) \\&=v_{F, \text { non-interacting }}-\frac{e^{2}}{\pi}\left(1-\ln \left|\frac{2 k_{F}}{k_{F}-k}\right|\right)_{k \rightarrow k_{F}} \\& \approx \lim _{k \rightarrow k_{F}} \ln \left|k_{F}-k\right| \\&=\infty \end{aligned} \] Within the H-F approximation, the Fermi velocity is infinite, which is in direct contradiction to experimental results.

Question

Q: What's wrong?

A: The \(1 / r\) interaction is a long-range interaction (non-local). It results in \(v(q=0)=\infty,\) which makes the diagrammatic expansion diverge.

Naively speaking, this means that particles with \(1 / r\) interaction is always strongly-correlated (perturbation theory fails, no matter how weak the interaction is). However, it turns out that there is one important physics here, which actually makes the interaction shortrange and weak, and thus the power-law expansion becomes sound again.

3.2 Screening

To understand why the free theory (the 0 th order approximation) works better than the Hartree-Fock approximation (the 1 st order approximation), one needs to take into account a very important phenomenon: screening. This effect is covered by higher order diagrams, which we ignored in the Hartree-Fock approximation. (This is why the HF approximation fails).

Interactions between electrons in a solid

For two electrons in vacuum, the Coulomb's law tells us that the interaction energy between these two electrons is \[ V(r)=e^{2} / r \]

Q: What's the Coulomb's law inside a solid material?

A: It depends on whether the material is a metal or an insulator.

For an insulator, we know that the Coulomb's law is \[ V(r)=\frac{1}{\epsilon} \frac{e^{2}}{r} \] where \(\epsilon\) is the dielectric constant (permittivity) of the solid. Here, inside an insulator, the solid medium modifies the interaction strength by a factor \(1 / \epsilon\), but the potential still decays as \(1 / r\).

Screening

For a metal, the screening effect changes the \(1 / r\) interaction into a short range interaction which decays exponentially.

Q: What is the \(E\) field in a metal? (consider only the static \(E\) field) \(A : E=0\)

This is because we have Ohm's law \[ j=\sigma E \] In equilibrium, \(j=0,\) so \(E=0 .\) This means that the electric field is reduced dramatically in a solid, and therefore we expect the interaction strength between two charge particles to become much weaker in a metal.

To see this, we consider two test charge with charge \(e\) inside a metal. Because electrons can move in a metal, electrons will redistribute after we introduces these two test charge. For a positive test charge, electrons will be attracted to the test charge and thus the total electric charge near the test charge (test charge+electrons) is almost zero. (Same will happen for negative charge). In other words, conducting electrons "screen" the test charge, which reduces the interaction dramatically. This effect can be described as \[ V(q, \omega)=\frac{V^{(0)}(q)}{\epsilon(q, \omega)} \] Here

  • \(V(q, \omega)\) is the effective interactions that electrons inside a metal feel in the momentum-frequency space.

  • \(V^{(0)}(q)=4 \pi e^{2} / q^{2}\) is the Coulomb potential in vacuum. The denominator \(\epsilon(q, \omega)\) is the dielectric function.

  1. For insulators, the dielectric constant is the \(\omega \rightarrow 0\) and \(q \rightarrow 0\) limit of \(\epsilon(q, \omega) .\)
  2. For a metal, we will show below that \(\epsilon(q=0, \omega=0)=\infty,\) which makes the effective potential \(V(q, \omega)\) finite at \(q=0\) and \(\omega=0 .\)

If \(V(q, \omega)\) is finite at \(q=0\) and \(\omega=0,\) the effective interaction \(V(r, \omega=0)\) decays exponentially as a function of \(r\) (instead of \(1 / r) .\) In other words, in a metal the e-e interactions are short-range interactions (not \(1 / r\) ).

In H-F, we use the \(1 / r\) interaction, instead of the correct effective interaction. And this is the reason why the theory fails.

  • This formula only contains the first order perturbation in terms of the effective interaction strength \(V,\) but it includes infinite diagrams in terms of \(V^{(0)} .\)
  • The physical meaning here is, if you substitute the interaction \(V^{(0)}\) by the effective interaction \(V,\) then the Fock diagram contains a lot more higher order contributions. We can further improve the accuracy by including more and more terms in effective interaction \(V\).
  • Here, the strategy is, we treat interaction (dashed line) as a Green's function (for some bosonic particles), and then we plot all higher order corrections to the interaction strength. The sum gives us an effective interaction.
  • When we compute any physical observables, e.g. the Fermion Green's function, we will not use the bare interaction \(V^{(0)},\) but the effective interaction \(V .\) The physical meaning is that inside a metal, electrons will screen the Coulomb interaction, and thus effectively the interaction between electrons is \(V,\) instead of \(V^{(0)} .\) And please notice that here we didn't include any more diagram. It is just a way to organize the diagrams to make the calculation more efficient.

Here we compute \(V\) by considering diagrams shown above. This approximation (ignoring all other diagrams) are called the Random Phase Approximation (RPA). \[ \begin{aligned} V^{ RPA }\left(q, i \omega_{n}\right)=&V^{(0)}\left(q, i \omega_{n}\right)+V^{(0)}\left(q, i \omega_{n}\right) \Pi^{(0)}\left(q, i \omega_{n}\right) V^{(0)}\left(q, i \omega_{n}\right)\\ &+V^{(0)}\left(q, i \omega_{n}\right) \Pi^{(0)}\left(q, i \omega_{n}\right) V^{(0)}\left(q, i \omega_{n}\right) \Pi^{(0)}\left(q, i \omega_{n}\right) V^{(0)}\left(q, i \omega_{n}\right) \ldots \end{aligned} \] Here, the leading order correction is \(\Pi^{(0)}\) is called a bubble. This name comes from the fact that the Feynman diagram for \(\Pi^{(0)}\) looks like a bubble.

\[ \begin{aligned} V \left(q, i \omega_{n}\right)=&V^{(0)}\left(q, i \omega_{n}\right)+V^{(0)}\left(q, i \omega_{n}\right) \Pi\left(q, i \omega_{n}\right) V^{(0)}\left(q, i \omega_{n}\right)\\ &+V^{(0)}\left(q, i \omega_{n}\right) \Pi^{(0)}\left(q, i \omega_{n}\right) V^{(0)}\left(q, i \omega_{n}\right) \Pi^{(0)}\left(q, i \omega_{n}\right) V^{(0)}\left(q, i \omega_{n}\right) \ldots \end{aligned} \] where

Using Dyson's equation, we find \[ \begin{aligned} V\left(q, i \omega_{n}\right)&=\frac{V^{(0)}\left(q, \dot{i} \omega_{n}\right)}{1-\Pi\left(q, i \omega_{n}\right) V^{(0)}\left(q, i \omega_{n}\right)} \\&=\frac{1}{V^{(0)}\left(q, i \omega_{n}\right)^{-1}-\Pi\left(q, i \omega_{n}\right)} \end{aligned} \] Notice that \[ \begin{aligned} V\left(q, i \omega_{n}\right)&=\frac{V^{(0)}\left(q, i \omega_{n}\right)}{1-\Pi\left(q, i \omega_{n}\right) V^{(0)}\left(q, i \omega_{n}\right)} \\&=\frac{V^{(0)}\left(q, \dot{i} \omega_{n}\right)}{\epsilon\left(q, \dot{i} \omega_{n}\right)} \end{aligned} \] This tells us that the dielectric function for a metal is \[ \begin{aligned} \epsilon\left(q, i \omega_{n}\right)&=1-\Pi\left(q, i \omega_{n}\right) V^{(0)}\left(q, i \omega_{n}\right) \\&=1-\frac{4 \pi e^{2}}{q^{2}} \Pi\left(q, i \omega_{n}\right) \end{aligned} \] As will be shown later, in the limit of \(\omega \rightarrow 0\) and \(q \rightarrow 0, \Pi(q, \omega)\) of a metal goes to a finite constant value \[ \lim _{q \rightarrow 0} \epsilon(q, \omega=0)=O\left(\frac{1}{q^{2}}\right)=\infty \] i.e. the dielectric constant in a metal is infinite. since both \(V^{(0)}(q)\) and \(\epsilon(q, \omega=0)\) diverge as \(1 / q^{2}\) in the small \(q,\) the effective interaction is NOT singular at \(\omega=0\) and \(q=0\) \[ V(q, \omega=0)=\frac{V^{(0)}(q)}{\epsilon(q, \omega=0)}=\frac{O\left(1 / q^{2}\right)}{O\left(1 / q^{2}\right)}=\text { finite at } q \rightarrow 0 \]

This tell us that screening effect helps us remove the singularity in the interaction strength at small q. This will cure all the problem we had in the HF approximation.

3.3 the Random Phase approximation

Here we use the \(RPA\), where we ignore all higher order term in \(\Pi\left(q, i \omega_{n}\right)\) beyond the bubble diagram. \[ \Pi\left(q, i \omega_{n}\right)=\Pi^{(0)}\left(q, i \omega_{n}\right)+O\left(V^{1}\right) \]

\[ \Pi^{(0)}\left(q, i \omega_{n}\right) =\int \frac{d k}{(2 \pi)^{d}} \frac{1}{\beta} \sum_{i \Omega_{n}} G^{(0)}\left(k, i \Omega_{n}\right) G^{(0)}\left(k+q, i \Omega_{n}+i \omega_{n}\right) \]

Once again, we compute the frequency sum using the Fermi distribution function \[ \frac{1}{\beta} \sum_{i \omega_{n}} g\left(i \omega_{n}\right)=\sum_{m \text { outside } C} f\left(z_{m}\right) \operatorname{Res}\left(g, z_{m}\right) \] Here, \(g\left(i \Omega_{n}\right)=G^{(0)}\left(k, i \Omega_{n}\right) G^{(0)}\left(k+q, i \Omega_{n}+i \omega_{n}\right)\) \[ \begin{aligned} g(z)=G^{(0)}(k, z) G^{(0)}\left(k+q, z+i \omega_{n}\right)=\frac{1}{z-\epsilon(k)} \frac{1}{z+i \omega_{n}-\epsilon(k+q)} \end{aligned} \] Obviously, \(g(z)\) has two singularity points: \(z_{1}=\epsilon(k)\) and \(z_{2}=\epsilon(k+q)-i \omega_{n} .\) Residue at \(z_{1}\) can be found using \[ \begin{aligned} \operatorname{Res}\left[g(z), z_{1}\right]&=\lim _{z \rightarrow z_{1}} g(z)\left(z-z_{1}\right) \\&=\lim _{z \rightarrow z_{1}} \frac{1}{z-z_{1}} \frac{1}{z-z_{2}}\left(z-z_{1}\right)\\&=\lim _{z \rightarrow z_{1}} \frac{1}{z-z_{2}}\\&=\frac{1}{z_{1}-z_{2}} \end{aligned} \] Similarly, we find that \(\operatorname{Res}\left[g(z), z_{2}\right]=\frac{1}{z_{2}-z_{1}},\) therefore \[ \begin{aligned} \frac{1}{\beta} \sum_{i \Omega_{n}} g\left(i \Omega_{n}\right)&=\sum_{m \text { outside } C} f\left(z_{m}\right) \operatorname{Res}\left(g, z_{m}\right) \\&=\frac{f\left(z_{1}\right)}{z_{1}-z_{2}}+\frac{f\left(z_{2}\right)}{z_{2}-z_{1}} \\&=\frac{f\left(z_{1}\right)-f\left(z_{2}\right)}{z_{1}-z_{2}} \\&=\frac{f[\epsilon(k)]-f[\epsilon(k+q)]}{i \omega_{n}+\epsilon(k)-\epsilon(k+q)} \end{aligned} \] So \[ \begin{aligned} \Pi^{(0)}\left(q, i \omega_{n}\right)&= \Delta \\&=\int \frac{d k}{(2 \pi)^{d}} \frac{1}{\beta} \sum_{i \Omega_{n}} G^{(0)}\left(k, i \Omega_{n}\right) G^{(0)}\left(k+q, i \Omega_{n}+i \omega_{n}\right) \\&=\int \frac{d k}{(2 \pi)^{d}} \frac{f[\epsilon(k)]-f[\epsilon(k+q)]}{i \omega_{n}+\epsilon(k)-\epsilon(k+q)} \end{aligned} \] This integral can be computed analytically at \(T=0\) (see Mahan, page 437 ). Here, we will only look at the limit of \(\omega \rightarrow 0\) and \(q \rightarrow 0 .\) At \(\omega=0\) and \(q \rightarrow 0\) \[ \begin{aligned} \Pi^{(0)}(q, \omega=0)&=\int \frac{d k}{(2 \pi)^{d}} \frac{f[\epsilon(k)]-f[\epsilon(k+q)]}{\epsilon(k)-\epsilon(k+q)} \\&=\int \frac{d k}{(2 \pi)^{d}} \frac{d f(\epsilon)}{d \epsilon} \\&=-\int \frac{d k}{(2 \pi)^{d}} \frac{d f(\epsilon)}{d \mu} \end{aligned} \] Here, we used the fact that \[ \frac{d}{d \epsilon} \frac{1}{\exp [\beta(\epsilon-\mu)]+1}=-\frac{d}{d \mu} \frac{1}{\exp [\beta(\epsilon-\mu)]+1} \] Therefore, \[ \begin{aligned} \Pi^{(0)}(q, \omega=0)&=-\int \frac{d k}{(2 \pi)^{d}} \frac{d f(\epsilon)}{d \mu} \\&=-\frac{d}{d \mu} \int \frac{d k}{(2 \pi)^{d}} f(\epsilon) \\&=-\frac{d N}{d \mu}=-N(0) \end{aligned} \] Here, \(N=\int \frac{d k}{(2 \pi)^{d}} f(\epsilon)\) is the total number of particles. \(\frac{d N}{d \mu}\) is the density of states \(N(0)\) at the Fermi level. At \(T=0,\) for a Fermi liquid, \[ \begin{aligned} \text { Number of states }&=4 \pi g \int \frac{k^{2} d k}{(2 \pi)^{3}} \\&=\frac{g}{2 \pi^{2}} \int k^{2} d k \\&=\frac{g}{2 \pi^{2}} \int 2 m \in d \sqrt{2 m \epsilon} \\&=\frac{g}{\sqrt{2} \pi^{2}} m^{3 / 2} \int \sqrt{\epsilon} d \epsilon \end{aligned} \] So \[ N(0)=\frac{g}{\sqrt{2} \pi^{2}} m^{3 / 2} \sqrt{\epsilon_{F}} \] And thus the dielectric function \[ \epsilon^{ RPA }\left(q, i \omega_{n}\right)=1-\frac{4 \pi e^{2}}{q^{2}} \Pi^{ RPA }\left(q, i \omega_{n}\right) \] In the \(\omega=0\) and \(q \rightarrow 0\) limit \[ \lim _{q \rightarrow 0} \epsilon^{ RPA }(q, \omega=0)=1+\frac{4 \pi e^{2}}{q^{2}} N(0) \] One can define the Thomas-Fermi screening wave vector \(q_{ TF }\) \[ q_{ TF }^{2}=\frac{6 \pi e^{2} n}{\epsilon_{F}} \] here \(n\) is the density of electron and \(n=\frac{2}{3} \epsilon_{F} N(0)\) \[ \begin{aligned} n&=\int_{0}^{\epsilon_{F}} d \epsilon N(\epsilon)=\frac{g}{\sqrt{2} \pi^{2}} m^{3 / 2} \int d \epsilon \sqrt{\epsilon} \\&=\frac{g}{\sqrt{2} \pi^{2}} \frac{2}{3}\left(m \epsilon_{F}\right)^{3 / 2} \\&=\frac{2}{3} \epsilon_{F} \frac{g}{\sqrt{2} \pi^{2}} m^{3 / 2} \sqrt{\epsilon_{F}} \\&=\frac{2}{3} \epsilon_{F} N(0) \end{aligned} \] Therefore, \[ q_{ TF }^{2}=\frac{6 \pi e^{2} n}{\epsilon_{F}}=\frac{6 \pi e^{2}}{\epsilon_{F}} \frac{2}{3} \epsilon_{F} N(0)=4 \pi e^{2} N(0) \] So \[ \lim _{q \rightarrow 0} \epsilon^{ RPA }(q, \omega=0)=1+\frac{q_{ TF }^{2}}{q^{2}} \] In other words, the dielectric constant for a metal is \(\infty,\) which diverges as \(1 / q^{2}\) in the \(q \rightarrow 0\) limit. As for the effective interaction, \[ V(q, \omega)=\frac{V^{(0)}(q, \omega)}{\epsilon(q, \omega)}=\frac{\frac{4 \pi e^{2}}{q^{2}}}{\epsilon(q, \omega)} \] At \(\omega=0,\) because both denominator and numerator diverge as \(1 / q^{2}\) in the small \(q\) limit, \(V(q=0, \omega=0)\) is finite. This is very different from the bare interaction \(V^{(0)}(q),\) which is singular at \(q=0\) \[ \lim _{q \rightarrow 0} V(q, \omega=0)=\frac{\frac{4 \pi e^{2}}{q^{2}}}{1+\frac{q_{ TF }^{2}}{q^{2}}}=\frac{4 \pi e^{2}}{q^{2}+q_{ TF }^{2}} \] If we convert this formula into the real space, the small \(q\) limit of \(V(q)\) give us the large \(r\) limit of \(V(r)\). \[ V(r, \omega=0)=\int \frac{d^{d} q}{(2 \pi)^{d}} \exp (i \vec{q} \cdot \vec{r}) V(q, \omega=0) \] When \(r\) is large enough, the small \(q\) part of the integral dominates the whole integral (large \(q\) and large \(r\) means very rapid oscillation in \(\exp (i \vec{q} \cdot \vec{r}),\) which is zero after integral). So at long distance, \[ \begin{aligned} V(r, \omega=0) &\approx \int \frac{d^{3} q}{(2 \pi)^{3}} \exp (i \vec{q} \cdot \vec{r}) \frac{4 \pi e^{2}}{q^{2}+q_{ TF }^{2}} \\&=\frac{1}{4 \pi^{2}} \int_{0}^{\infty} q^{2} d q \int_{0}^{\pi} d \theta \sin \theta \exp (i q r \cos \theta) \frac{4 \pi e^{2}}{q^{2}+q_{ TF }^{2}} \\&=\frac{1}{4 \pi^{2}} \int_{0}^{\infty} q^{2} d q \frac{2 \sin (q r)}{q r} \frac{4 \pi e^{2}}{q^{2}+q_{ TF }^{2}} \\&=\frac{e^{2}}{i \pi r}\left[\int_{0}^{\infty} \frac{q \exp (i q r)}{q^{2}+q_{ TF }^{2}} d q-\int_{0}^{\infty} \frac{q \exp (-i q r)}{q^{2}+q_{ TF }^{2}} d q\right] \\&=\frac{e^{2}}{i \pi r}\left[\int_{-\infty} \frac{\infty}{q^{2}+q_{ TF }^{2}} d q\right] \\&=\frac{e^{2}}{i \pi r} 2 \pi i \operatorname{Res}\left[\frac{q \exp (i q r)}{q^{2}+q_{ TF }^{2}}, q=i q_{ TF }\right] \\&=\frac{e^{2}}{r} \exp \left(-q_{ TF } r\right) \end{aligned} \] The interaction decays exponential at long distance with decay rate \(q_{ TF }\).

Fermion self-energy within the random phase approximation

For a Fermi liquid, the RPA shows pretty good agreement with experiments and is the minimum approximation to describe an interacting Fermi liquid. \[ G\left(k, i \omega_{n}\right)=\frac{1}{i \omega_{n}-\epsilon(k)-\Sigma^{ RPA }\left(k, i \omega_{n}\right)} \] where

and the double dashed line is the effective interaction computed using the RPA approximation. We will not go into the detailed calculation. Instead, we just present the result here. After analytic continuation, we get \[ G(k, \omega)=\frac{1}{\omega-\epsilon(k)-\Sigma^{ RPA }(k, \omega)} \] For a Fermi liquid at \(T =0 K ,\) we focus on \(k \sim k_{F}\) and \(z \sim 0\).

Near \(k_{F}, \epsilon(k)=\mu+v_{F}\left(k-k_{F}\right)+O\left(k-k_{F}\right)^{2}\) \[ G^{(0)}(k, \omega)=\frac{1}{\omega-v_{F}\left(k-k_{F}\right)-\mu+O\left(k-k_{F}\right)^{2}} \] Using this \(G^{(0)},\) we can compute \(\Sigma^{ RPA }\). For \(\Sigma^{ RPA }(k, z),\) we expand it around \(k \sim k_{F}\) and \(z \sim 0\). \[ G\left(k_{F}, \omega\right)=\frac{1}{\omega-v\left(k-k_{F}\right)-\mu-\Sigma^{ RPA }\left(k_{F}, 0\right)-a\left(k-k_{F}\right)+b \omega+O\left(k-k_{F}\right)^{2}+O(z)^{2}} \] It is important to notice that \(b\) is real and positive (the \(\omega^{2}\) term has a complex coefficient). The imaginary part of \(\Sigma^{ RPA }\) will only appear at order \(\omega^{2}\) and above. Therefore, in the limit \(k \sim k_{F}\) and \(\omega \sim 0,\) the imaginary part of the self-energy is much smaller than the real part, and thus we can ignore the imaginary part in the low-energy limit \(\omega \rightarrow 0\). Therefore, \[ G^{ RPA }\left(k_{F}, \omega\right)=\frac{Z}{\omega-\tilde{v}\left(k-k_{F}\right)-\tilde{\mu}}+O\left(k-k_{F}\right)^{2}+O(z)^{2} \] here

  • $Z=1 /(1+b)<1 $
  • \(\tilde{v}=\frac{v+a}{1+b}\)
  • \(\tilde{\mu}=\frac{\mu+\Sigma^{ RPA }\left(k_{F}, 0\right)}{1+b} .\)

Notice that in the limit of \(k \rightarrow k_{F}\) and \(\omega \rightarrow 0, G ^{ RPA }\left( k _{ F }, \omega\right)\) looks just like the Green's function for a free fermion, with a renormalized Fermi velocity and a renormalized chemical potential. This is why we can treat electrons as "free" particles in a Fermi liquid, although the interaction is pretty strong. In addition, we have an extra factor \(Z,\) which is known as the Fermi residue \[ G^{ RPA }\left(k_{F}, z\right)=Z G^{ Free }\left(k_{F}, z\right) \] This extra factor is \(0<Z<1\). The physical meaning is that now at \(k_{F}\) the jump in occupation number \(n\left(k_{F}-\delta\right)-n\left(k_{F}+\delta\right)=Z\) not 1

The imaginary part of the self-energy (inverse life-time of our particle) is proportional to \(\omega^{2},\) which means that at zero frequency and at \(k=k_{F}\) our particles has infinite life time.

Summary

In Landau's Fermi liquid theory, we can consider the low-energy excitations (near the Fermi surface) in an interacting Fermi liquid as quasiparticles. In the limit \(\omega \rightarrow 0,\) the life time of our quasi-particles diverge and they behave just like free particles.

These quasi-particles carry the same charge and spin as an electron. However, the dispersion (Fermi velocity) and the chemical potential of these quasi-particles are renormalized by interactions. In addition, interaction reduces the Fermi residue from 1 to some finite value \(0<Z<1\).

This is why we can treat metals as if the electrons are free particles, although the interaction is in fact pretty strong.
