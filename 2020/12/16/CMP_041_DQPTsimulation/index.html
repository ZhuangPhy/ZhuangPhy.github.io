1 Theoretical Model

We begin with a two-band model with the Hamiltonian written in momentum space \[ H=\sum_{k} \Psi_{k}^{\dagger} h(k) \Psi_{k} \] where

  • \(\Psi_{k}\) denotes a spinor
  • \(h(k)\) can be expanded in \(\hat{\sigma }=\left(\sigma_{x}, \sigma_{y}, \sigma_{z}\right)\) Pauli matrices base

\[ h(k)=d_{0}(k)+\hat{d }(k) \cdot \hat{\sigma } \]

This model can describe a variety of physically different many-body systems

  1. SSH model： $ (k)=[(t+t)+(t-t) k,(t-t) k, 0)]$
  2. Kitaev chain: \(\hat{d } (k)=[0, \Delta \sin k,(-\mu / 2-t \cos k)]\)
  3. Transverse field Ising model : \(\hat{d } (k)=[0, \sin k,(h- \cos k)]\)

The eigenvalues of \(h(k)\) are given by \[ \epsilon_{\pm}(k)=d_{0}(k) \pm| \hat{d } (k)| \] The corresponding eigenvectors are denoted by \(\left|\phi_{\pm}(k)\right\rangle,\) or written as density matrices \[ \begin{aligned} \rho_{\pm}(k)&=\left|\phi_{\pm}(k)\right\rangle\left\langle\phi_{\pm}(k)\right|\\&=\frac{1}{2}[1 \pm \hat{ d }(k) \cdot \sigma] \end{aligned} \]

quench dynamics

  1. we first prepare the system in the ground state of the initial Hamiltonian \(h_{i}(k)\)

\[ \begin{aligned} \rho_{i}(k)&=\left|\phi_{i}(k)\right\rangle\left\langle\phi_{i}(k)\right|\\&=\frac{1}{2}\left[1-\hat{ d }_{i}(k) \cdot \sigma\right] \end{aligned} \]

  1. a sudden quench to the final Hamiltonian \(h_{f}(k)\)

  2. Time evolution of density matrices \[ i \frac{\partial \rho(k, t)}{\partial t}=\left[h^{f}(k), \rho(k, t)\right] \] the solution \[ \begin{aligned} \rho(k, t)&=e^{-i h^{f}(k) t} \rho(k, 0) e^{i h^{f}(k) t}\\&=\frac{1}{2}[1-\hat{d}(k, t) \cdot \sigma] \end{aligned} \]

  3. geometric picture

    Bloch vector \[ \begin{aligned} \hat{ d }(k, t) \cdot \sigma&=e^{-i t d _{f}(k) \cdot \sigma}\left[\hat{ d }_{i}(k) \cdot \sigma\right] e^{i t d _{f}(k) \cdot \sigma}\\ \hat{ d }(k, t)&=\hat{ d }^{i} \cos \left(2\left| d ^{f}\right| t\right)+2 \hat{ d }^{f}\left(\hat{ d }^{i} \cdot \hat{ d }^{f}\right) \sin ^{2}\left(\left| d ^{f}\right| t\right)+\hat{ d }^{i} \times \hat{ d }^{f} \sin \left(2\left| d ^{f}\right| t\right) \end{aligned} \]

    the spin precession on the Bloch sphere:

    \(\hat{ d }_{i}(k)\) rotating around \(\hat{ d }_{f}(k)\) with period \(\pi /\left| d _{f}(k)\right|\)

dynamical topological invariant

  • In general, the 1D Brillouin zone (BZ) has the topology \(S^{1}\).

  • the topology of time can be viewed as \(S^{1}\)

hence the total momentum-time manifold has the topology \(T^{2}\)

  • If there are two fixed points \(k=k_{1}\) and \(k_{2},\) the corresponding circle contract to a point, then the momentum-time manifold can be reduced to a series of spheres \(S^{2}\).

  • In general, if there are \(N\) fixed points in a BZ, the momentum-time manifold \(T^{2}\) can be reduced to \(N\) sub-manifolds, each of them has the topology \(S^{2}\).

Now we naturally have a map from each momentum-time sub-manifold to the Bloch vector \(\hat{ d }(k, t),\) which is\(S^{2} \rightarrow S^{2},\) and then we can define a Chern number \[ \begin{aligned} C_{d y n}^{m}&=\frac{1}{4 \pi} \int_{k_{m}}^{k_{m+1}} d k \int_{0}^{\pi} d t^{\prime}\left(\hat{ d } \times \partial_{t^{\prime}} \hat{ d }\right) \cdot \partial_{k}, \hat{ d }\\ &=\frac{1}{2}\left(\cos \theta_{k=k_{m}}-\cos \theta_{k=k_{m+1}}\right) \end{aligned} \] where

  • \(m=1,2, \ldots, N\) denotes the \(m\) th sub-manifold
  • \(k_{m}\) denotes the \(m\) th fixed point
  • \(k_{N+1}=k_{1}+\) \(2 \pi\) is the same point modulo \(2 \pi .\)
  • \(\theta_{k}\) is the included angle between \(\hat{ d }^{i}(k)\) and \(\hat{ d }^{f}(k)\)

Hence the integral is an integer and it measures how many times the Bloch vectors cover the unit sphere.

example：SSH Model

SSH Model Hamiltonian \[ H=\sum_{k} \Psi_{k}^{\dagger} h(k) \Psi_{k} \] where

  • \(h(k)=d_{x} \sigma_{x}+d_{y} \sigma_{y}\)
  • \(d_{x}=(1+\delta)+(1-\delta) \cos k\)
  • \(d_{y}=(1-\delta) \sin k\)

topological phase

  • For \(\delta>0\) the half-filled system is topologically trivial
  • For \(\delta<0\) the system is topological

dynamical topological invariant

  • both \(\delta^{i}\) and \(\delta^{f}\) lie in the same phase, have \(C_{d y n}^{1}=C_{d y n}^{2}=0\)
  • if \(\delta^{i}\) and \(\delta^{f}\) are in different phases, dynamical topological invariants are \(C_{d y n}^{1}=-1\) and \(C_{d y n}^{2}=1\)

The evolution of Bloch vector for different momenta

    1. \(\delta^{i}=-0.7\) and \(\delta^{f}=-0.3 ;\) corresponding to topological cases
    1. \(\delta^{i}=-0.7\) and \(\delta^{f}=0.3 ;\) corresponding to topological cases
  • \(( c ) \delta^{i}=0.4\) and \(\delta^{f}=0.2 ;\) corresponding to trivial cases.
  • $( d ) ^{i}=0.4 \(and\)^{f}=-0.2 .$ corresponding to trivial cases.

The black dotted loop represents the distribution of initial Bloch vectors \(\hat{ d }^{i}(k)\)

  • For \(( a )\) and \(( c ),\) both \(\delta^{i}\) and \(\delta^{f}\) lie in the same phase, and the trajectories of different Bloch vectors cancel out each other, giving rise to the dynamical Chern number \(C_{d y n}^{1}=0 ;\)
  • For. \(2( b )\) and \(( d ), \delta^{i}\) and \(\delta^{f}\) lie in different phases, and the trajectories for Bloch vectors of \(k \in(0, \pi)\) cover the whole Bloch sphere, corresponding to \(C_{d y n}^{1}=-1\)

2 Superconducting Qubits

LC-oscillator(Quantum Harmonic Oscillator )

In LC-oscillator system, energy oscillates between

  • electrical energy in the capacitor \(C\) \(\rightarrow\) associate the electrical energy with the "kinetic energy"
  • magnetic energy in the inductor \(L\). \(\rightarrow\) the magnetic energy with the "potential energy" of the oscillator

The Hamiltonian of LC-oscillator \[ H=\frac{Q^{2}}{2 C}+\frac{\Phi^{2}}{2 L} \equiv \frac{1}{2} C V^{2}+\frac{1}{2} L I^{2} \] where

  • \(Q(t)\) the charge on the capacitor
  • \(\Phi\) the flux on the inductor

this Hamiltonian is analogous to that of a mechanical harmonic oscillator \[ H=p^{2} / 2 m+m \omega^{2} x^{2} / 2 \] with

  • mass \(m=C\)
  • resonant frequency \(\omega=1 / \sqrt{L C}\)

The Hamiltonian described above is classical. In order to proceed to a quantum-mechanical description of the system, we need to promote the charge and flux coordinates to quantum operators. the quantum operators similarly satisfy a commutation relation: \[ [\hat{\Phi}, \hat{Q}]=\hat{\Phi} \hat{Q}-\hat{Q} \hat{\Phi}=i \hbar \] second quantization \[ \begin{aligned} \hat{a} &=\left(\hat{\Phi}+i Z_{r} \hat{Q}\right) / \sqrt{2 \hbar \bar{Z}_{r}} \\ \hat{a}^{\dagger} &=\left(\hat{\Phi}-i Z_{r} \hat{Q}\right) / \sqrt{2 \hbar Z_{r}} \end{aligned} \] the quantum harmonic oscillator (QHO) Hamiltonian \[ H=\hbar \omega_{r}\left(a^{\dagger} a+\frac{1}{2}\right) \]

since many gate operations depend on frequency selectivity, the equidistant level-spacing of the QHO, we need to add an-harmonicity (or nonlinearity) into our system. In short, we require the transition frequencies \(\omega_{ q }^{0 \rightarrow 1}\) and \(\omega_{ q }^{1 \rightarrow 2}\) be sufficiently different to be individually adressable.

Josephson junction

To introduce the nonlinearity required to modify the harmonic potential, we use the Josephson junction, which is consists of two or more superconductors coupled by a thin insulating barrier

modified Hamiltonian \[ \hat{H}_{0}=4 E_{C} \hat{n}^{2}-E_{J} \cos \hat{\phi} \] where

  • \(\hat{n}\) is the operator corresponding to the number of Cooper pairs tunneled through the Josephson junctions
  • \(\hat{\phi}\) denotes the gauge-invariant phase-difference operator across the Josephson junctions.

There are three main categories of the quantum superconducting qubit working in different regimes according the ratio of the Josephson energy \(E_{J}\) to the charging energy \(E_{C}:\)

\((1)\) charge qubit with \(E_{J} / E_{C} \sim 0.1 ;\)

  1. flux qubit with \(E_{J} / E_{C} \sim 50 ;\)

  2. phase qubit \(E_{J} / E_{C} \sim 10^{6}\).

In the regime: \(20 \lesssim E_{J} / E_{C} \lesssim 100,\) expanding the potential \[ E_{J} \cos (\phi)=\frac{1}{2} E_{J} \phi^{2}-\frac{1}{24} E_{J} \phi^{4}+ O \left(\phi^{6}\right) \] an-harmonic oscillator (AHO). \[ H=\omega_{ q } a^{\dagger} a+\frac{\alpha}{2} a^{\dagger} a^{\dagger} a a \] The anharmonicity is big enough for the addressability of the two lowest energy levels and thus constitutes a qubit(a quantum two-level system) \[ \hat{H}_{0}=\frac{1}{2} \hbar \omega \hat{\sigma}_{z} \] with

  • \(\omega=2\left(\sqrt{8 E_{J} E_{C}}-E_{C}\right) / \hbar\)
  • \(\hat{\sigma}_{z}=|e\rangle\langle e|-| g\rangle\langle g|\)
  • Here \(|g\rangle\) is the ground state of the transmon
  • while \(|e\rangle\) is its first excited eigenstate.

effectively treat the AHO as a quantum two-level system, thus constitutes a qubit

3 Experimental results

Results 1: dynamical topological invariant

example for demonstration in paper: The transverse-field Ising model \[ H_{\text {Ising }}=-\sum_{l} \sigma_{l}^{x} \sigma_{l+1}^{x}+g \sigma_{l}^{z} \] where \(g\) is the transverse-field strength.

The spin model can be mapped to the free-fermion model by using Jordan-Wigner transformation \[ \begin{aligned} \sigma_{l}^{z}&=1-2 c_{l}^{\dagger}c_{l}\\\sigma_{l}^{x}&=\prod_{j<l}\left(1-2 c_{j}^{\dagger} c_{j}\right)\left(c_{l}+c_{l}^{\dagger}\right) \end{aligned} \] The Hamiltonian changes to \[ H_{\text {Ising }}=-\sum_{l}\left(c_{l}^{\dagger} c_{l+1}+c_{l}^{\dagger} c_{l+1}^{\dagger}+\text { h.c. }\right)+g\left(1-2 c_{l}^{\dagger} c_{l}\right) \] where \(d (k)=[0, \sin k,(g-\cos k)],\) It is well known that the model is

  • in ferromagnetic phase when \(g<1\)
  • in paramagnetic phase when \(g>1\)

In the experiment, we let \(g_{i}=0.2,\) which is in the ferromagnetic phase regime. The system is suddenly quenched to the final Hamiltonian \(h_{f}(k) .\) Here two different strengths of the field are chosen

  1. \(g_{f}=1.5\) corresponding to the paramagnetic phases
  2. \(g_{f}=0.5\) corresponding to the ferromagnetic phases

For each value of \(k,\) the state is rotated for two cycles on the Bloch sphere, representing time evolution for two periods. The rotation axis is determined by the quench Hamiltonian \(h_{f}(k)\). The path of the state time evolution is presented in Fig

Results 2: Rate function

the rate function of the dynamical free energy \[ f(t)=-\frac{1}{N} \sum_{k} \log \left|\left\langle\phi_{i}(k)\left|e^{-i t h_{f}(k)}\right| \phi_{i}(k)\right\rangle\right|^{2} \] The nonanalytic behavior of \(f(t)\) corresponds to \(DQPT\)

presents the time-dependent rate functions for different \(g_{f},\) all with initial parameter \(g_{i}=0.2 .\)

  • rate functions \(f(t)\) have sharp peaks at the critical time \(t_{c}\) for \(g_{f}=3.5,2.5,1.5>1 ,\) which leads to discontinuity for derivative of \(f(t)\) at \(t_{c} .\) This phenomenon corresponds to the DQPT.
  • rate functions for \(g_{f}=0.9,0.8,0.7\) are much more smooth and no sharp peak appears, so no discontinuity is expected for derivative of the rate functions. Thus no DQPT happens.

Results of a different number \(N\) of modes for \(k\), corresponding to size \(N\) of the Ising model.

Demonstrating the finite-size effect. As \(N\) increases, it approaches the theoretical value for \(N \rightarrow \infty\) and demonstrates nonanalytic behavior.

Results 3: Skyrmion lattice

the Loschmidt amplitude of a two-band system can be written as \[ G(t)=\prod_{k}\left[\cos \left(\left| d _{f}(k)\right| t\right)+i \hat{ d }_{i}(k) \cdot \hat{ d }_{f}(k) \sin \left(\left| d _{f}(k)\right| t\right)\right] \] The DQPT occurs when the Loschmidt amplitude reaches zero at a critical time \(t_{c} .\) the existence of zero of \(G(t)\) requires that there are at least one critical momentum \(k^{*}\) satisfying \[ \hat{ d }_{i}\left(k^{*}\right) \cdot \hat{ d }_{f}\left(k^{*}\right)=0 \] For clarity, we employ the Ising model to elucidate the condition of DQPT. One has, \[ \begin{aligned} d _{i}(k) \cdot d _{f}(k) &=\sin ^{2} k+\cos ^{2} k-\left(g_{i}+g_{f}\right) \cos k+g_{i} g_{f} \\ &=0 \end{aligned} \] The solution exists when \[ |\cos k|=\left|\frac{1+g_{i} g_{f}}{g_{i}+g_{f}}\right|<1 \] DQPT​ occurs if and only if the initial Hamiltonian and the final Hamiltonian belong to different phases for the Ising model \[ k^{*}=\pm \arccos \left[\left(1+g_{i} g_{f}\right) /\left(g_{i}+g_{f}\right)\right] \] a dynamical Chern number can be defined in momentum-time space in a quench process.

The nontrivial dynamical Chern number indicates the emergence of the Skyrmion lattice in the momentum time space.

the emergence of a Skyrmion lattice in momentum-time space for DQPT

initial parameter \(g_{i}=0.2 .\)

  • \(g_{f}=1.5\) in Fig. \(( c )\) the emergence of the Skyrmion lattice in momentum-time space can be seen obviously, which indicates the nontrivial dynamical Chern number implying the occurrence of DQPT. The time coordinates of the center of Skyrmion is just the critical time \(t_{c} .\)
  • \(g_{f}=0.5\) in Fig. \(( d )\) While if \(g_{i}=0\) and \(g_{f}=0.5\) lie in the same phase, the configuration of the Skyrmion lattice does not appear , the corresponding dynamical Chern number is trivial. There is no DQPT as shown Fig. \(( b )\).
