《Nonequilibrium Many-Body Theory of Quantum Systems: A Modern Introduction》Gianluca Stefanucci

Noninteracting models

1 Bloch theorem and band structure

A crystal consists of a unit cell repeated periodically in three, two, or one dimensions. Each unit cell contains the same (finite) number of atoms arranged in the same geometry. An example is graphene, i.e., a planar structure of \(s p^{2}\) -bonded carbon atoms arranged in a honeycomb lattice as illustrated in Fig. 2.4. In this case the unit cell consists of two carbon atoms, \(a\) and \(b\) in the figure, repeated periodically along the directions \(v _{+}\) and \(v _{-}\). The unit cells can be labeled with a vector of integers \(n =\left(n_{1}, \ldots, n_{d}\right)\) where \(d\) is the dimension of the crystal, see again Fig. \(2.4 .\) The expansion of the vector \(n\) over the orthonormal basis \(\left\{ e _{i}\right\}\) with \(\left( e _{i}\right)_{j}=\delta_{i j}\) reads \[ n =\sum_{i=1}^{d} n_{i} e _{i} \] Two unit cells with labels \(n\) and \(n ^{\prime}\) are nearest neighbor if \(\left| n - n ^{\prime}\right|=1 .\) As for the PPP model we assign to each unit cell a set of localized orbitals \(\left\{\varphi_{ n s \sigma}\right\}\) which we assume already orthonormal and denote by \(\hat{d}_{ n s \sigma}^{\dagger}\) and \(\hat{d}_{ n s \sigma}\), the creation and annihilation operators for electrons in the orbital \(\varphi_{ n s \sigma}\). The index \(s\) runs over all orbitals of a given unit cell. In the case of graphene we may assign a single \(p_{z}\) orbital to each carbon, so \(s=1,2\).

In the absence of magnetic fields or other couplings that break the spin symmetry, the matrix elements of \(\hat{h}\) are \[ h_{ n s \sigma n ^{\prime} s^{\prime} \sigma^{\prime}}=\delta_{\sigma \sigma^{\prime}} h_{ n s n ^{\prime} s^{\prime}} \] If we choose orbitals localized around the atomic positions then the matrix elements \(h_{ n s n ^{\prime} s^{\prime}}\) are very small for \(\left| n - n ^{\prime}\right| \gg 1 .\) We then discard \(h_{ n s n ^{\prime} s^{\prime}}\) unless \(n\) and \(n ^{\prime}\) are nearest neighbors. The periodicity of a crystal is reflected in the fact that the unit cell Hamiltonian \(h_{ n s n s^{\prime}},\) as well as the Hamiltonian connecting two nearest neighbor cells \(h_{ n s} n \pm e _{i} s^{\prime},\) does not depend on \(n\). We therefore define the matrices \[ h_{s s^{\prime}} \equiv h_{ ns ns ^{\prime}} \] and \[ \begin{aligned} T_{i, s s^{\prime}} \equiv h_{ n + e _{i} s n s^{\prime}}, \quad \Rightarrow \quad T_{i, s s^{\prime}}^{\dagger} &=h_{ n + e _{i} s^{\prime} n s}^{*}=h_{ n s n + e _{i} s^{\prime}} \\ &=h_{ n - e _{i} s n s^{\prime}} \end{aligned} \] With these definitions our model for the noninteracting Hamiltonian of a crystal takes the form \[ \hat{H}_{0}=\sum_{ n \sigma} \sum_{s s^{\prime}}\left(h_{s s^{\prime}} \hat{d}_{ n s \sigma}^{\dagger} \hat{d}_{ n s^{\prime} \sigma}+\sum_{i=1}^{d} T_{i, s s^{\prime}} \hat{d}_{ n + e _{i} s \sigma}^{\dagger} \hat{d}_{ n s^{\prime} \sigma}+T_{i, s s^{\prime}}^{\dagger} \hat{d}_{ n - e _{i} s \sigma}^{\dagger} \hat{d}_{ n s^{\prime} \sigma}\right) \] To better visualize the matrix structure of \(\hat{H}_{0}\) we introduce the vector of \(\hat{d}\) -operators \[ \hat{ d }_{ n \sigma}^{\dagger} \equiv\left(\hat{d}_{ n 1 \sigma}^{\dagger}, \hat{d}_{ n 2 \sigma}^{\dagger}, \ldots\right), \quad \hat{ d }_{ n \sigma}=\left(\hat{d}_{ n 1 \sigma}^{\dagger}, \hat{d}_{ n 2 \sigma}^{\dagger}, \ldots\right)^{\dagger} \] and rewrite \(\hat{H}_{0}\) in terms of the product between these vectors and the matrices \(h, T_{i}\) and \(T_{i}^{\dagger}\) \[ \hat{H}_{0}=\sum_{ n \sigma}\left(\hat{ d }_{ n \sigma}^{\dagger} h \hat{ d }_{ n \sigma}+\sum_{i=1}^{d} \hat{ d }_{ n + e _{i} \sigma}^{\dagger} T_{i} \hat{ d }_{ n \sigma}+\hat{ d }_{ n - e _{i} \sigma}^{\dagger} T_{i}^{\dagger} \hat{ d }_{ n \sigma}\right) \] The strategy to find the one-particle eigenvalues of \(\hat{H}_{0}\) consists of considering a finite block of the crystal and then letting the volume of the block go to infinity. This block is, for convenience, chosen as a parallelepiped with edges given by \(d\) linearly independent vectors of integers \(N _{1}, \ldots, N _{d}\) each radiating from a given unit cell. Without loss of generality we can choose the radiating unit cell at the origin. The set \(V _{b}\) of all unit cells contained in the block is then \[ V _{b}=\left\{ n : \frac{ n \cdot N _{i}}{ N _{i} \cdot N _{i}}<1 \text { for all } i\right\} \] In the case of a one-dimensional crystal we can choose only one vector \(N _{1}=N\) and the unit cells of the block \(n =n \in V _{b}\) are those with \(n=0,1, \ldots, N-1\). For the two-dimensional graphene, instead, we may choose, e.g., \(N _{1}=(N, 0)\) and \(N _{2}=(0, M)\) or \(N _{1}=(N, N)\) and \(N _{2}=(M,-M)\) or any other couple of linearly independent vectors. When the volume of the block tends to infinity the eigenvalues of the crystal are independent of the choice of the block. Interestingly, however, the procedure below allows us to know the eigenvalues of \(\hat{H}_{0}\) for any finite \(N _{1}, \ldots, N _{d},\) and hence to have access to the eigenvalues of blocks of different shapes. We come back to this aspect at the end of the section. To simplify the mathematical treatment we impose periodic boundary conditions along \(N _{1}, \ldots, N _{d}\), i.e., we "wrap" the block onto itself forming a ring in \(d=1\), a torus in \(d=2\), etc. This choice of boundary conditions is known as the Born-von Karman (BvK) boundary condition and turns out to be very convenient. Other kinds of boundary condition would lead to the same results in the limit of large \(V _{b}\). The Bvk condition implies that the cells \(n\) and \(n + N _{i}\) are actually the same cell and we therefore make the identifications \[ \hat{ d }_{ n + N _{i} \sigma}^{\dagger} \equiv \hat{ d }_{ n \sigma}^{\dagger}, \quad \hat{ d }_{ n + N _{i} \sigma} \equiv \hat{ d }_{ n \sigma} \] for all \(N _{i}\). We are now in the position to show how the diagonalization procedure works. Consider the Hamiltonian (2.8) in which the sum over \(n\) is restricted to unit cells in \(V _{b}\) and the identification (2.10) holds for all boundary terms with one \(\hat{d}\) -operator outside \(V _{b}\). To bring \(\hat{H}_{0}\) into a diagonal form we must find a suitable linear combination of the \(\hat{d}\) -operators that preserves the anti-commutation relations, as discussed in Section \(1.6 .\) For this purpose we construct the matrix \(U\) with elements \[ U_{ nk }=\frac{1}{\sqrt{\left| V _{b}\right|}} e^{ ik \cdot n } \] where \(\left| V _{b}\right|\) is the number of unit cells in \(V _{b} .\) In (2.11) the row index runs over all \(n \in V _{b}\) whereas the column index runs over all vectors \(k =\left(k_{1}, \ldots, k_{d}\right)\) with components \(-\pi<\) \(k_{i} \leq \pi\) and, more importantly, fulfilling \[ k \cdot N _{i}=2 \pi m_{i}, \quad \text { with } m_{i} \text { integers. } \] We leave it as an exercise for the reader to prove that the number of \(k\) vectors with these properties is exactly \(\left| V _{b}\right|\) and hence that \(U\) is a square matrix. Due to property (2.12) the quantity \(U_{ nk }\) is periodic in \(n\) with periods \(N _{1}, \ldots, N _{d}\). It is this periodicity that we now exploit to prove that \(U\) is unitary. Consider the following set of equalities and the likes with \(k_{2}^{\prime}, \ldots, k_{d}^{\prime} .\) In the second line of the above identities we use the fact that the sum over all \(n \in V _{b}\) of a periodic function \(f( n )\) is the same as the sum of \(f\left( n - e _{1}\right)\). For the left and the right hand side of these equations to be the same we must either have \(k = k ^{\prime}\) or \[ \sum_{ n } U_{ n k }^{*} U_{ n k ^{\prime}}=0 \quad \text { for } k \neq k ^{\prime} \] Since for \(k = k ^{\prime}\) the sum \(\sum_{ n } U_{ n k }^{*} U_{ n k }=1\), the matrix \(U\) is unitary and hence the operators \[ \hat{ c }_{ k \sigma}=\frac{1}{\sqrt{\left| V _{b}\right|}} \sum_{ n \in V _{b}} e^{- i k \cdot n } \hat{ d }_{ n \sigma}, \quad \hat{ c }_{ k \sigma}^{\dagger}=\frac{1}{\sqrt{\left| v _{b}\right|}} \sum_{ n \in V _{b}} e^{ i k \cdot n } \hat{ d }_{ n \sigma}^{\dagger} \] preserve the anti-commutation relations. The inverse relations read \[ \hat{ d }_{ n \sigma}=\frac{1}{\sqrt{\left| V _{b}\right|}} \sum_{ k } e^{i k \cdot n } \hat{ c }_{ k \sigma}, \quad \hat{ d }_{ n \sigma}^{\dagger}=\frac{1}{\sqrt{\left| V _{b}\right|}} \sum_{ k } e^{- i k \cdot n } \hat{ c }_{ k \sigma}^{\dagger} \] and the reader can easily check that due to property (2.12) the \(\hat{d}\) -operators satisfy the BvK boundary conditions (2.10). Inserting these inverse relations into (2.8) (in which the sum is restricted to \(n \in V _{b}\) ) we get the Hamiltonian \[ \hat{H}_{0}=\sum_{ k \sigma} \hat{ c }_{ k \sigma}^{\dagger}\left(h+\sum_{i=1}^{d}\left(T_{i} e^{- i k_{i}}+T_{i}^{\dagger} e^{ i k_{i}}\right)\right) \hat{ c }_{ k \sigma} \] In this expression the matrix \(h_{ k }\) is Hermitian and can be diagonalized. Let \(\epsilon_{ k \nu}\) be the eigenvalues of \(h_{ k }\) and \(u_{ k }\) be the unitary matrix that brings \(h_{ k }\) into the diagonal form, i.e., \(h_{ k }=u_{ k } \operatorname{diag}\left(\epsilon_{ k 1}, \epsilon_{ k 2}, \ldots\right) u_{ k }^{\dagger} .\) The unitary matrix \(u_{ k }\) has the dimension of the number of orbitals in the unit cell and should not be confused with the matrix \(U\) whose dimension is \(\left| V _{b}\right| .\) We now perform a further change of basis and construct the following linear combinations of the \(\hat{c}\) -operators with fixed \(k\) vector \[ \hat{ b }_{ k \sigma}=u_{ k }^{\dagger} \hat{ c }_{ k \sigma}, \quad \hat{ b }_{ k \sigma}^{\dagger}=\hat{ c }_{ k \sigma}^{\dagger} u_{ k } \] Denoting by \(u_{ k \nu}(s)=\left(u_{ k }\right)_{s \nu}\) the \((s, \nu)\) matrix element of \(u_{ k }\) the explicit form of the \(\hat{b}\) -operators is \[ \hat{b}_{ k \nu \sigma}=\sum_{s} u_{ k \nu}^{*}(s) \hat{c}_{ k s \sigma}, \quad \hat{b}_{ k \nu \sigma}^{\dagger}=\sum_{s} u_{ k \nu}(s) \hat{c}_{ k s \sigma}^{\dagger} \] With these definitions the Hamiltonian \(\hat{H}_{0}\) takes the desired form since \[ \begin{aligned} \hat{H}_{0} &=\sum_{ k \sigma} \hat{ c }_{ k \sigma}^{\dagger} u_{ k } \operatorname{diag}\left(\epsilon_{ k 1}, \epsilon_{ k 2}, \ldots\right) u_{ k }^{\dagger} \hat{ c }_{ k \sigma} \\ &=\sum_{ k \nu \sigma} \epsilon_{ k \nu} \hat{b}_{ k \nu \sigma}^{\dagger} \hat{b}_{ k \nu \sigma} \end{aligned} \] We have derived the Bloch theorem: the one-particle eigenvalues of \(\hat{H}_{0}\) are obtained by diagonalizing \(h_{ k }\) for all \(k\) and the corresponding one-particle eigenkets \(| k \nu \sigma\rangle=\hat{b}_{ k \nu \sigma}^{\dagger}|0\rangle\) have overlap with the original basis functions \(\left| n s \sigma^{\prime}\right\rangle=d_{n s \sigma^{\prime}}^{\dagger}|0\rangle\) given by \[ \psi_{ k \nu \sigma}\left( n s \sigma^{\prime}\right)=\left\langle n s \sigma^{\prime} \mid k \nu \sigma\right\rangle=\frac{\delta_{\sigma \sigma^{\prime}}}{\sqrt{\left| V _{b}\right|}} e^{i k \cdot n } u_{ k \nu}(s) \] which is a plane-wave with different amplitudes on different atoms of the same unit cell. When the volume of the block \(\left| V _{b}\right| \rightarrow \infty\) the eigenvalues \(\epsilon_{ k \nu}\) become a continuum called a band. \(^{3}\) We then have a band for each \(\nu\) and the total number of bands coincides with the number of localized orbitals per unit cell. Each crystal is characterized by its band structure and, as we see in Section 6.3 .4 , the band structure can be experimentally measured. If we choose the localized basis functions to be of the form (2.1) and subsequently we want to increase the accuracy of the calculations by reducing the spacing \(\Delta\), then in the limit \(\Delta \rightarrow 0\) the quantities \(u_{ k \nu}(s) \rightarrow u_{ k \nu}( r )\) become continuous functions of the position \(r\) in the cell. These functions can be periodically extended to all space by imposing a requirement that they assume the same value in the same point of all unit cells. In the literature the periodic functions \(u_{ k \nu}( r )\) are called Bloch functions. Below we illustrate some elementary applications of this general framework.

One-band model

Figure 2.5 (a) A ring with \(N\) unit cells. (b) Eigenvalues of the ring Hamiltonian with \(t=-1\) and \(\epsilon=2\) for different numbers \(N\) of unit cells. The eigenvalues with \(N=61\) and 121 are shifted upward by 1 and 2 respectively.

The simplest example is a one-dimensional crystal with one orbital per unit cell, see Fig. \(2.5( a ) .\) Then the matrices \(h \equiv \epsilon\) and \(T \equiv t\) are \(1 \times 1\) matrices and the one-particle eigenvalues are \[ \epsilon_{k}=\epsilon+t e^{- i k}+t e^{ i k}=\epsilon+2 t \cos k \] If the number \(N\) of unit cells is, e.g., odd, then the one-dimensional \(k\) vector takes the values \(k=2 \pi m / N\) with \(m=-\frac{(N-1)}{2}, \ldots, \frac{(N-1)}{2},\) as follows from (2.12). The eigenvalues (2.14) are displayed in Fig. \(2.5( b )\) for different \(N\). It is clear that when \(N \rightarrow \infty\) they become a continuum and form one band.

Two-band model

Figure 2.6 (a) A ring with \(N\) unit cells and two atoms per cell. (b) Eigenvalues of the ring Hamiltonian with \(t=-1, \epsilon=2\) and \(\Delta=1 / 2\) for different numbers \(N\) of unit cells.

Another example of a one-dimensional crystal is shown in Fig. \(2.6( a ) .\) Each unit cell consists of two different atoms, \(a\) and \(b\) in the figure. We assign a single orbital per atom and neglect all off-diagonal matrix elements except those connecting atoms of type \(a\) to atoms of type \(b\). For simplicity we also consider the case that the distance between two nearest neighbor atoms is everywhere the same along the crystal. Then the matrices \(h\) and \(T\) are \(2 \times 2\) matrices with the following structure \[ h=\left(\begin{array}{cc} \epsilon+\Delta & t \\ t & \epsilon-\Delta \end{array}\right), \quad T=\left(\begin{array}{ll} 0 & t \\ 0 & 0 \end{array}\right) \] where \(\Delta\) is an energy parameter that takes into account the different nature of the atoms. The structure of \(T_{s s^{\prime}}=h_{n+1 s n s^{\prime}},\) see \((2.7),\) can be derived by checking the overlap between atom \(s\) in cell \(n+1\) and atom \(s^{\prime}\) in cell \(n\). For instance, if we take cell 2 we see from Fig. 2.6 (a) that atom \(a\) has overlap with atom \(b\) in cell 1 , hence \(T_{a b}=t\), but it does not have overlap with atom \(a\) in cell 1 , hence \(T_{a a}=0 .\) On the other hand atom \(b\) in cell 2 does not have overlap with any atom in cell \(1,\) and hence \(T_{b a}=T_{b b}=0 .\) The eigenvalues of \[ h_{k}=h+T e^{- i k}+T^{\dagger} e^{ i k}=\left(\begin{array}{cc} \epsilon+\Delta & t\left(1+e^{- i k}\right) \\ t\left(1+e^{ i k}\right) & \epsilon-\Delta \end{array}\right) \] are \[ \epsilon_{k \pm}=\epsilon \pm \sqrt{\Delta^{2}+2 t^{2}(1+\cos k)} \] and are displayed in Fig. \(2.6( b )\) for different numbers \(N\) of unit cells. As in the previous example, the values of \(k\) have the form \(2 \pi m / N\) with \(m=-\frac{(N-1)}{2}, \ldots, \frac{(N-1)}{2}\) for odd \(N\). When \(N \rightarrow \infty\) the \(\epsilon_{k \pm}\) become a continuum and form two bands separated by an energy gap of width \(2 \Delta\). If the crystal contains two electrons per unit cell then the lower band is fully occupied whereas the upper band is empty. In this situation we must provide a minimum energy \(2 \Delta\) to excite one electron in an empty state; the crystal behaves like an insulator if the gap is large and like a semiconductor if the gap is small. It is also worth noting that for \(\Delta=0\) we recover the previous example but with \(2 N\) atoms rather than \(N\).

Graphene

Another important example that we wish to discuss is the two-dimensional crystal with which we opened this section. Graphene is a single layer of graphite and, as such, not easy to isolate. It was not until 2004 that graphene was experimentally realized by transferring a single layer of graphite onto a silicon dioxide substrate [9] , an achievement which was rewarded with the Nobel prize in physics in \(2010 .\) The coupling between the graphene and the substrate is very weak and does not alter the electrical properties of the graphene. Before 2004 experimentalists were already able to produce tubes of graphene with a diameter of few nanometers. These carbon nanotubes can be considered as an infinitely long graphene strip wrapped onto a cylinder. Due to their mechanical (strong and stiff) and electrical properties, carbon nanotubes are among the most studied systems at the time of writing this book. There exist several kinds of nanotube depending on how the strip is wrapped. The wrapping is specified by a pair of integers \((P, Q)\) so that atoms separated by \(P_{ V _{+}}+Q_{ v -}\) are identified. Below we consider the armchair nanotubes for which \((P, Q)=(N, N)\) and hence the axis of the tube is parallel to \(v _{+}- v _{-}\)

Let us define the vector \(N _{1}=(N, N)\) and the vector parallel to the tube axis \(N _{2}=\) \((M,-M)\). The armchair nanotube is recovered when \(M \rightarrow \infty\). For simplicity we assign a single \(p_{z}\) orbital to each carbon atom and consider only those off-diagonal matrix elements that connect atoms \(a\) to atoms \(b\). From Fig. 2.4 we then see that the matrices \(h, T_{1}\) and \(T_{2}\) must have the form \(^{4}\) \[ h=\left(\begin{array}{cc} 0 & t \\ t & 0 \end{array}\right), \quad T_{1}=\left(\begin{array}{cc} 0 & t \\ 0 & 0 \end{array}\right), \quad T_{2}=\left(\begin{array}{cc} 0 & t \\ 0 & 0 \end{array}\right) \] The eigenvalues of \[ h_{ k }=h+\sum_{i=1}^{2}\left(T_{i} e^{-i k_{i}}+T_{i}^{\dagger} e^{i k_{i}}\right)=\left(\begin{array}{cc} 0 & t\left(1+e^{-i k_{1}}+e^{-i k_{2}}\right) \\ t\left(1+e^{i k_{1}}+e^{i k_{2}}\right) & 0 \end{array}\right) \] can easily be calculated and read \[ \epsilon_{ k \pm}=\pm t \sqrt{1+4 \cos \frac{k_{1}-k_{2}}{2}\left(\cos \frac{k_{1}-k_{2}}{2}+\cos \frac{k_{1}+k_{2}}{2}\right)} \] The possible values of \(k\) belong to a square with vertices in \((\pm \pi, \pm \pi)\) and fulfill (2.12), i.e., \[ \begin{array}{l} k \cdot N _{1}=N\left(k_{1}+k_{2}\right)=2 \pi m_{1} \\ k \cdot N _{2}=M\left(k_{1}-k_{2}\right)=2 \pi m_{2} \end{array} \]

Figure 2.7 (a) Equivalent domain of \(k\) vectors. The grey areas with the same roman numbers are separated either by the vector \((2 \pi, 0)\) or by the vector \((0,2 \pi)\). (b) Eigenvalues of a (5,5) armchair nanotube. The thick lines correspond to the valence band (below zero) and the conduction band (above zero). All bands in between the top and the conduction band and in between the bottom and the valence band are doubly degenerate.

From these relations it is evident that an equivalent domain of \(k\) vectors, like the one illustrated in Fig. \(2.7( a ),\) is more convenient for our analysis. This equivalent domain is a rectangle tilted by \(\pi / 4,\) with the short edge equal to \(\sqrt{2} \pi\) and the long edge equal to \(2 \sqrt{2} \pi\). If we define \(k_{x}=k_{1}+k_{2}\) and \(k_{y}=k_{1}-k_{2}\) then \(-2 \pi<k_{x} \leq 2 \pi\) and \(-\pi<k_{y} \leq \pi\) and hence \[ \begin{array}{l} k_{x}=2 \pi \frac{m_{1}}{N}, \quad \text { with } m_{1}=-N+1, \ldots, N \\ k_{y}=2 \pi \frac{m_{2}}{M}, \quad \text { with } m_{2}=-\frac{(M-1)}{2}, \ldots, \frac{M-1}{2} \end{array} \] for odd \(M\). The reader can easily check that the number of \(k\) is the same as the number of atoms in the graphene block. In the limit \(M \rightarrow \infty\) the quantum number \(k_{y}\) becomes a continuous index and we obtain \(2 \times 2 N\) one-dimensional bands corresponding to the number of carbon atoms in the transverse direction, \(^{5}\) \[ \epsilon_{k_{y} k_{x} \pm}=\pm t \sqrt{1+4 \cos \frac{k_{y}}{2}\left(\cos \frac{k_{y}}{2}+\cos \frac{k_{x}}{2}\right)} \] The set of all carbon atoms in the transverse direction can be considered as the unit cell of the one-dimensional crystal, which is the nanotube.

In Fig. \(2.7( b )\) we plot the eigenvalues \(\epsilon_{k_{y} k_{x} \pm}\) for the \((N, N)=(5,5)\) armchair nanotube. Since the neutral nanotube has one electron per atom, all bands with negative energy are fully occupied. It is noteworthy that the highest occupied band (valence band) and the lowest unoccupied band (conduction band) touch only in two points. Around these points the energy dispersion is linear and the electrons behave as if they were relativistic [10] . Increasing the diameter of the nanotube also, \(k_{x}\) becomes a continuous index and the \(\epsilon_{k \pm}\) become the eigenvalues of the two-dimensional graphene. In this limit the \(\epsilon_{ k \pm}\) form two two-dimensional bands which still touch in only two points. Due to its so peculiar band structure graphene is classified as a semi-metal, that is a crystal with properties between a metal and a semiconductor.

Fano model

The Fano model is ubiquitous in condensed matter physics as it represents the simplest schematization of a discrete state "interacting" with a continuum of states. It was originally introduced by Fano to explain a universal asymmetric line-shape observed in the absorption spectrum of several physical systems [14]. We introduce it here to model an atom or a molecule adsorbed on the surface of a metal, see the schematic illustration below.

Let \(|\epsilon Q\rangle\) be the single-particle energy eigenket of the metal, where \(Q\) is a quantum number (continuous or discrete) that accounts for the degeneracy of the eigenvalue \(\epsilon\). In this section the spin index carried by states and operators is understood since everything is diagonal in spin space. Equivalently, we can think of fermions with spin zero. For the atom we consider only a single localized orbital and denote by \(\left|\epsilon_{0}\right\rangle\) the corresponding ket. For large distances between the atom and the surface the Hamiltonian of the system is the sum of the Hamiltonian \(\hat{H}_{\text {met }}\) of the metal and the Hamiltonian \(\hat{H}_{\text {at }}\) of the atom \[ \hat{H}_{ met }+\hat{H}_{ at }=\int d \epsilon d Q \epsilon \hat{d}_{\epsilon Q}^{\dagger} \hat{d}_{\epsilon Q}+\epsilon_{0} \hat{d}_{0}^{\dagger} \hat{d}_{0} \] where the \(\hat{d}\) -operators create or annihilate electrons and hence satisfy the anti-commutation relations \[ \left[\hat{d}_{\epsilon Q}, \hat{d}_{\epsilon^{\prime} Q^{\prime}}^{\dagger}\right]_{+}=\delta\left(\epsilon-\epsilon^{\prime}\right) \delta\left(Q-Q^{\prime}\right), \quad\left[\hat{d}_{0}, \hat{d}_{0}^{\dagger}\right]_{+}=1 \] When the atom approaches the surface the overlap \(T_{\epsilon Q} \equiv\left\langle\epsilon Q|\hat{h}| \epsilon_{0}\right\rangle\) increases and cannot be neglected any longer. In this regime the Hamiltonian of the system becomes \[ \hat{H}_{0}=\hat{H}_{ met }+\hat{H}_{ at }+\int d \epsilon d Q\left(T_{\epsilon Q} \hat{d}_{\epsilon Q}^{\dagger} \hat{d}_{0}+T_{\epsilon Q}^{*} \hat{d}_{0}^{\dagger} \hat{d}_{\epsilon Q}\right) \] This is the general structure of the Fano model. It is common (and for later purposes also instructive) to discretize the continuum of states by retaining only one state \(\left|\epsilon_{k} Q_{k}\right\rangle\) in the volume element \(\Delta \epsilon \Delta Q\). Then, for small volume elements the Fano Hamiltonian can be approximated as \[ \hat{H}_{0}=\sum_{k} \epsilon_{k} \hat{d}_{k}^{\dagger} \hat{d}_{k}+\epsilon_{0} \hat{d}_{0}^{\dagger} \hat{d}_{0}+\sum_{k}\left(T_{k} \hat{d}_{k}^{\dagger} \hat{d}_{0}+T_{k}^{*} \hat{d}_{0}^{\dagger} \hat{d}_{k}\right) \] where the discrete \(\hat{d}\) -operators satisfy the anti-commutation relations \(\left[\hat{d}_{k}, \hat{d}_{k^{\prime}}\right]_{+}=\delta_{k k^{\prime}} .\) To recover the continuum limit we establish the correspondence \[ T_{k}=\sqrt{\Delta \epsilon \Delta Q} T_{\epsilon_{k} Q_{k}} \] for the off-diagonal matrix elements of \(\hat{h}\), and \[ \hat{d}_{k}=\sqrt{\Delta \epsilon \Delta Q} \hat{d}_{\epsilon_{k} Q_{k}}, \quad \hat{d}_{k}^{\dagger}=\sqrt{\Delta \epsilon \Delta Q} \hat{d}_{\epsilon_{k} Q_{k}}^{\dagger} \] for the fermionic operators. Letting \(\Delta \epsilon \Delta Q \rightarrow 0\) the discrete Hamiltonian (2.18) reduces to the continuum Hamiltonian (2.17) and \[ \left[\hat{d}_{\epsilon_{k} Q_{k}}, \hat{d}_{\epsilon_{k^{\prime}} Q_{k^{\prime}}}^{\dagger}\right]_{+}=\frac{\delta_{k k^{\prime}}}{\Delta \epsilon \Delta Q} \rightarrow \delta\left(\epsilon-\epsilon^{\prime}\right) \delta\left(Q-Q^{\prime}\right) \] We now calculate the atomic occupation \(n_{0}\) for a given Fermi energy \(\epsilon_{ F }\), which is the energy of the highest occupied level of the metal. Let \(|\lambda\rangle\) be the single-particle eigenkets of \(\hat{H}_{0}\) with eigenenergies \(\epsilon_{\lambda}\). The corresponding annihilation and creation operators \(\hat{c}_{\lambda}\) and \(\hat{c}_{\lambda}^{\dagger}\) are a linear combination of the original \(\hat{d}\) -operators as discussed in Section 1.5 , \[ \hat{c}_{\lambda}^{\dagger}=\left\langle\epsilon_{0} \mid \lambda\right\rangle \hat{d}_{0}^{\dagger}+\sum_{k}\langle k \mid \lambda\rangle \hat{d}_{k}^{\dagger} \] and with a similar equation for the adjoint. The ground state \(\left|\Phi_{0}\right\rangle\) of the system is obtained by occupying all the \(|\lambda\rangle\) s with energies \(\epsilon_{\lambda} \leq \epsilon_{ F }\) and reads \[ \left|\Phi_{0}\right\rangle=\prod_{\lambda: \epsilon_{\lambda} \leq \epsilon_{F}} \hat{c}_{\lambda}^{\dagger}|0\rangle \] To calculate the atomic occupation \(n_{0}=\left\langle\Phi_{0}\left|\hat{d}_{0}^{\dagger} \hat{d}_{0}\right| \Phi_{0}\right\rangle\) we evaluate the ket \(\hat{d}_{0}\left|\Phi_{0}\right\rangle\) by moving the operator \(\hat{d}_{0}\) through the string of \(\hat{c}^{\dagger}\) -operators, as we did in (1.57). The difference here is that the anti-commutator is \(\left[\hat{d}_{0}, \hat{c}_{\lambda}^{\dagger}\right]_{+}=\left\langle\epsilon_{0} \mid \lambda\right\rangle\) rather than a Kronecker delta. We then find \[ \hat{d}_{0}\left|\Phi_{0}\right\rangle=\sum_{\lambda}(-)^{p_{\lambda}}\left\langle\epsilon_{0} \mid \lambda\right\rangle \prod_{\lambda^{\prime} \neq \lambda} \hat{c}_{\lambda^{\prime}}^{\dagger}|0\rangle \] where the sum and the product are restricted to states below the Fermi energy. The integer \(p_{\lambda}\) in the above equation refers to the position of \(\hat{c}_{\lambda}^{\dagger}\) in the string of operators (2.20) in agreement with (1.57). The atomic occupation \(n_{0}\) is the inner product of the manyparticle state \(\hat{d}_{0}\left|\Phi_{0}\right\rangle\) with itself. In this inner product all cross terms vanish since they are proportional to the inner product of states with different strings of \(\hat{c}_{\lambda}^{\dagger}\) operators, see (1.58). Taking into account that \(\prod_{\lambda^{\prime} \neq \lambda} \hat{c}_{\lambda^{\prime}}^{\dagger}|0\rangle\) is normalized to 1 for every \(\lambda\) we obtain the intuitive result \[ n_{0}=\sum_{\lambda: \epsilon_{\lambda} \leq \epsilon_{F}}\left|\left\langle\epsilon_{0} \mid \lambda\right\rangle\right|^{2} \] the atomic occupation is the sum over all occupied states of the probability of finding an electron in \(\left|\epsilon_{0}\right\rangle .\)

From (2.21) it seems that we need to know the eigenkets \(|\lambda\rangle\) and eigenenergies \(\epsilon_{\lambda}\) in order to determine \(n_{0}\). We now show that this is not strictly the case. Let us rewrite \(n_{0}\) as \[ n_{0}=\int_{-\infty}^{\epsilon_{ F }} \frac{d \omega}{2 \pi} \sum_{\lambda} 2 \pi \delta\left(\omega-\epsilon_{\lambda}\right)\left|\left\langle\epsilon_{0} \mid \lambda\right\rangle\right|^{2} \] The eigenkets \(|\lambda\rangle\) satisfy \(\hat{h}|\lambda\rangle=\epsilon_{\lambda}|\lambda\rangle\) where \(\hat{h}\) is the one-particle Hamiltonian in first quantization. For the Fano model \(\hat{h}\) has the following ket-bra form: \[ \hat{h}=\sum_{k} \epsilon_{k}|k\rangle\left\langle k\left|+\epsilon_{0}\right| \epsilon_{0}\right\rangle\left\langle\epsilon_{0}\right|+\sum_{k}\left(T_{k}|k\rangle\left\langle\epsilon_{0}\left|+T_{k}^{*}\right| \epsilon_{0}\right\rangle\langle k|\right) \] where we use (1.86) . Then \[ \sum_{\lambda} \delta\left(\omega-\epsilon_{\lambda}\right)\left|\left\langle\epsilon_{0} \mid \lambda\right\rangle\right|^{2}=\sum_{\lambda}\left\langle\epsilon_{0}|\delta(\omega-\hat{h})| \lambda\right\rangle\left\langle\lambda \mid \epsilon_{0}\right\rangle=\left\langle\epsilon_{0}|\delta(\omega-\hat{h})| \epsilon_{0}\right\rangle \] where we use the completeness relation \(\sum_{\lambda}|\lambda\rangle\langle\lambda|=\hat{ 1 }\). Inserting this result into (2.22) we get \[ n_{0}=\int_{-\infty}^{\epsilon_{ F }} \frac{d \omega}{2 \pi}\left\langle\epsilon_{0}|2 \pi \delta(\omega-\hat{h})| \epsilon_{0}\right\rangle \] This is our first encounter with the spectral function (first quantization) operator \[ \hat{ A }(\omega) \equiv 2 \pi \delta(\omega-\hat{h})= i \left[\frac{1}{\omega-\hat{h}+ i \eta}-\frac{1}{\omega-\hat{h}- i \eta}\right] \] In the second equality \(\eta\) is an infinitesimally small positive constant and we used the Cauchy relation \[ \frac{1}{\omega-\epsilon \pm i \eta}=P \frac{1}{\omega-\epsilon} \mp i \pi \delta(\omega-\epsilon) \] where \(P\) denotes the principal part. Thus we can calculate \(n_{0}\) if we find a way to determine the matrix element \(A_{00}(\omega)=\left\langle\epsilon_{0}|\hat{ A }(\omega)| \epsilon_{0}\right\rangle\) of the spectral function. As we see in Chapter 6 , this matrix element can be interpreted as the probability that an electron in \(\left|\epsilon_{0}\right\rangle\) has energy \(\omega^{6}\) To determine \(A_{00}(\omega)\) we separate \(\hat{h}=\hat{ E }+\hat{ T }\) into a sum of the metal+atom Hamiltonian \(\hat{ E }\) and the off-diagonal part \(\hat{ T },\) and use the identity \[ \frac{1}{\zeta-\hat{h}}=\frac{1}{\zeta-\hat{ E }}+\frac{1}{\zeta-\hat{h}} \hat{ T } \frac{1}{\zeta-\hat{ E }} \] where \(\zeta\) is an arbitrary complex number. This identity can easily be verified by multiplying both sides from the left by \((\zeta-\hat{h})\). Sandwiching of (2.24) between \(\left\langle\epsilon_{0}\right|\) and \(\left|\epsilon_{0}\right\rangle\) and between \(\left\langle\epsilon_{0}\right|\) and \(|k\rangle\) we find \[ \begin{aligned} \left\langle\epsilon_{0}\left|\frac{1}{\zeta-\hat{h}}\right| \epsilon_{0}\right\rangle &=\frac{1}{\zeta-\epsilon_{0}}+\sum_{k} T_{k}\left\langle\epsilon_{0}\left|\frac{1}{\zeta-\hat{h}}\right| k\right\rangle \frac{1}{\zeta-\epsilon_{0}} \\ \left\langle\epsilon_{0}\left|\frac{1}{\zeta-\hat{h}}\right| k\right\rangle &=T_{k}^{*}\left\langle\epsilon_{0}\left|\frac{1}{\zeta-\hat{h}}\right| \epsilon_{0}\right\rangle \frac{1}{\zeta-\epsilon_{k}} \end{aligned} \] Substituting the second of these equations into the first we arrive at the following important result \[ \left\langle\epsilon_{0}\left|\frac{1}{\zeta-\hat{h}}\right| \epsilon_{0}\right\rangle=\frac{1}{\zeta-\epsilon_{0}-\Sigma_{ em }(\zeta)}, \quad \text { with } \quad \Sigma_{ em }(\zeta)=\sum_{k} \frac{\left|T_{k}\right|^{2}}{\zeta-\epsilon_{k}} \] The embedding self-energy \(\Sigma_{ em }(\zeta)\) appears because the atom is not isolated; we can think of it as a correction to the atomic level \(\epsilon_{0}\) induced by the presence of the metal. Taking \(\zeta=\omega+ i \eta\) we can separate \(\Sigma_{ em }(\zeta)\) into a real and an imaginary part, \[ \Sigma_{ em }(\omega+ i \eta)=\sum_{k} \frac{\left|T_{k}\right|^{2}}{\omega-\epsilon_{k}+ i \eta}=P \sum_{k} \frac{\left|T_{k}\right|^{2}}{\omega-\epsilon_{k}}-\frac{ i }{2} 2 \pi \sum_{k}\left|T_{k}\right|^{2} \delta\left(\omega-\epsilon_{k}\right) \] where

  • \(\Lambda(\omega)=P \sum_{k} \frac{\left|T_{k}\right|^{2}}{\omega-\epsilon_{k}}\)
  • \(\Gamma(\omega)=2 \pi \sum_{k}\left|T_{k}\right|^{2} \delta\left(\omega-\epsilon_{k}\right)\)

The real and imaginary parts are not independent but instead related by a Hilbert transformation \[ \Lambda(\omega)=P \int \frac{d \omega^{\prime}}{2 \pi} \frac{\Gamma\left(\omega^{\prime}\right)}{\omega-\omega^{\prime}} \] as can be verified by inserting the explicit expression for \(\Gamma(\omega)\) into the r.h.s.. In conclusion, we have obtained an expression for \(n_{0}\) in terms of the quantity \(\Gamma(\omega)\) only \[ \begin{aligned} n_{0} &=\int_{-\infty}^{\epsilon_{ F }} \frac{d \omega}{2 \pi} A_{00}(\omega) \\ &=\int_{-\infty}^{\epsilon_{ F }} \frac{d \omega}{2 \pi} i \left[\frac{1}{\omega+ i \eta-\epsilon_{0}-\Sigma_{ em }(\omega+ i \eta)}-\frac{1}{\omega- i \eta-\epsilon_{0}-\Sigma_{ em }(\omega- i \eta)}\right] \\ &=-2 \int_{-\infty}^{\epsilon_{ F }} \frac{d \omega}{2 \pi} \operatorname{Im} \frac{1}{\omega-\epsilon_{0}-\Lambda(\omega)+\frac{i}{2} \Gamma(\omega)+ i \eta} \end{aligned} \] If the coupling \(T_{k}\) between the atom and the metal is weak then \(\Lambda\) and \(\Gamma\) are small and the dominant contribution to the above integral comes from a region around \(\epsilon_{0}\). In the continuum limit the function \(\Gamma(\omega)\) is a smooth function since \[ \Gamma(\omega) \rightarrow 2 \pi \int d \epsilon d Q\left|T_{\epsilon Q}\right|^{2} \delta(\omega-\epsilon)=2 \pi \int d Q\left|T_{\omega Q}\right|^{2} \] and in the integral (2.27) we can approximate \(\Gamma(\omega)\) by a constant \(\Gamma=\Gamma\left(\epsilon_{0}\right)\). This approximation is known as the Wide Band Limit Approximation (WBLA) since for the r.h.s. of (2.28) to be \(\omega\) independent the spectrum of the metal must extend from \(-\infty\) and \(+\infty\). For a \(\Gamma(\omega)=\Gamma\) independent of \(\omega\) the Hilbert transform \(\Lambda(\omega)\) vanishes and the formula for the atomic occupation simplifies to \({ }^{7}\) \[ n_{0}=\int_{-\infty}^{\epsilon_{ F }} \frac{d \omega}{2 \pi} \frac{\Gamma}{\left(\omega-\epsilon_{0}\right)^{2}+\Gamma^{2} / 4} \] We thus see that in the WBLA \(n_{0}\) is the integral up to the Fermi energy of a Lorentzian of width \(\Gamma\) centered at \(\epsilon_{0}\). The atomic level is occupied \(\left(n_{0} \sim 1\right)\) if \(\epsilon_{0}<\epsilon_{ F }+\Gamma\), whereas it is empty \(\left(n_{0} \sim 0\right)\) if \(\epsilon_{0}>\epsilon_{ F }+\Gamma\). This result should be compared with the atomic limit, \(\Gamma=0\), corresponding to the isolated atom. In this limit the Lorentzian becomes a \(\delta\) -function since \[ \lim _{\Gamma \rightarrow 0} \frac{1}{\pi} \frac{\Gamma / 2}{\left(\omega-\epsilon_{0}\right)^{2}+\Gamma^{2} / 4}=\delta\left(\omega-\epsilon_{0}\right) \] as follows immediately from the Cauchy relation (2.23). In the atomic limit the atomic occupation is exactly 1 for \(\epsilon_{0}<\epsilon_{ F }\) and zero otherwise. We say that the presence of the metal broadens the sharp atomic level and transforms it into a resonance of finite width as illustrated in Fig. \(2.8 .\) This broadening is a general feature of discrete levels "interacting" or "in contact" with a continuum and it is observed in interacting systems as well.

Hubbard model

The Hubbard model was originally introduced to describe transition metals and rare-earth metals, i.e., solids composed of atoms with very localized outer electrons \((d\) or \(f\) shells). In these materials the degree of localization of the outer-electron orbitals is so high that the Coulomb integrals \(v_{s_{1} s_{2} s_{3} s_{4}}\) can be approximated as in (2.3) with the diagonal elements \(U_{s} \equiv v_{s s}\) about an order of magnitude larger than the off-diagonal ones. For this reason Hubbard, in a milestone paper from 1963 [15], included only the diagonal interaction \(U_{s}\) in his treatment and wrote the model Hamiltonian \[ \hat{H}=\hat{H}_{0}+\hat{H}_{ int }=\sum_{\sigma} \sum_{s s^{\prime}} h_{s s^{\prime}} \hat{d}_{s \sigma}^{\dagger} \hat{d}_{s^{\prime} \sigma}+\sum_{s} U_{s} \hat{n}_{s \uparrow} \hat{n}_{s \downarrow} \] that today carries his name. In this Hamiltonian the sums run over the lattice position \(s\) of the nuclei, the \(\hat{d}\) -operators are fermionic annihilation and creation operators for electrons with the usual anti-commutation relations \[ \left[\hat{d}_{s \sigma}, \hat{d}_{s^{\prime} \sigma^{\prime}}^{\dagger}\right]_{+}=\delta_{\sigma \sigma^{\prime}} \delta_{s s^{\prime}}, \quad\left[\hat{d}_{s \sigma}, \hat{d}_{s^{\prime} \sigma^{\prime}}\right]_{+}=0 \] and \(\hat{n}_{s \sigma}=\hat{d}_{s \sigma}^{\dagger} \hat{d}_{s \sigma}\) is the occupation operator for electrons in \(s\) with spin \(\sigma\). The matrix elements \(h_{s s^{\prime}}\) are typically set to zero if the distance between \(s\) and \(s^{\prime}\) exceeds a few lattice spacings. In the context of model Hamiltonians the off-diagonal matrix elements \(h_{s s^{\prime}}\) are also called hopping integrals or simply hoppings since they multiply the operator \(\hat{d}_{s \sigma}^{\dagger} \hat{d}_{s^{\prime} \sigma}\), which destroys an electron in \(s^{\prime}\) and creates an electron in \(s,\) an operation that can be pictured as the hopping of an electron from \(s^{\prime}\) to \(s\). It is interesting to note that in the Hubbard model two electrons interact only if they occupy the same atomic site (and of course have opposite spins).

To gain some insight into the physics of the Hubbard model we compare two limiting situations, i.e., \(\hat{H}_{\text {int }}=0\) and \(\hat{H}_{0}=0 .\) We see that electrons behave as "waves" if \(\hat{H}_{\text {int }}=0\) and as "particles" if \(\hat{H}_{0}=0\); how they behave when both \(\hat{H}_{0}\) and \(\hat{H}_{\text {int }}\) are nonzero is a fascinating problem intimately related to wave-particle dualism. To readers interested in the physics of the Hubbard model we suggest the review article in Ref. [16] and the books in Refs. [17,18].

For \(\hat{H}_{\text {int }}=0\) the Hubbard Hamiltonian describes noninteracting electrons on a lattice. Let \(|k \sigma\rangle=c_{k \sigma}^{\dagger}|0\rangle\) be the one-particle eigenkets with energies \(\epsilon_{k} .\) If we order the energies \(\epsilon_{k} \leq \epsilon_{k+1}\), the state of lowest energy with \(N_{\uparrow / \downarrow}\) electrons of spin up/down is \[ |\Phi\rangle=\prod_{k=1}^{N_{\uparrow}} \prod_{k^{\prime}=1}^{N_{\downarrow}} \hat{c}_{k \uparrow}^{\dagger} \hat{c}_{k^{\prime} \downarrow}^{\dagger}|0\rangle \quad \text { with } \quad \hat{H}_{0}|\Phi\rangle=\left(\sum_{k=1}^{N_{\uparrow}} \epsilon_{k}+\sum_{k^{\prime}=1}^{N_{\downarrow}} \epsilon_{k^{\prime}}\right)|\Phi\rangle \] We can say that in \(|\Phi\rangle\) the electrons behave as "waves" since they have probability 0 or 1 of being in a delocalized state \(|k \sigma\rangle .\) The \(N\) -particle ground state of \(\hat{H}_{0}\) is obtained by minimizing the eigenvalue in (2.30) with respect to \(N_{\uparrow}\) and \(N_{\downarrow}\) under the constraint \(N_{\uparrow}+N_{\downarrow}=N\). For nondegenerate energies \(\epsilon_{k}<\epsilon_{k+1}\), the ground state of \(\hat{H}_{0}\) with an even number \(N\) of electrons is unique and reads \[ \left|\Phi_{0}\right\rangle=\prod_{k=1}^{N / 2} \hat{c}_{k \uparrow}^{\dagger} \hat{c}_{k \downarrow}^{\dagger}|0\rangle \] We leave it as an exercise for the reader to show that \(\left|\Phi_{0}\right\rangle\) is also an eigenstate of the total spin operators [see (1.92)], \[ \hat{ S }=\left(\hat{S}^{x}, \hat{S}^{y}, \hat{S}^{z}\right) \equiv \sum_{s} \hat{ S }_{s}=\sum_{s}\left(\hat{S}_{s}^{x}, \hat{S}_{s}^{y}, \hat{S}_{s}^{z}\right) \] with vanishing eigenvalue, i.e., it is a singlet. If we perturb the system with a weak external magnetic field \(B\) along, say, the \(z\) axis and discard the coupling to the orbital motion, the noninteracting part of the Hamiltonian changes to \[ \hat{H}_{0} \rightarrow \hat{H}_{0}-g \mu_{ B } \hat{ S } \cdot B =\hat{H}_{0}-\frac{1}{2} g \mu_{ B } B\left(\hat{N}_{\uparrow}-\hat{N}_{\downarrow}\right) \] with \(g\) the electron gyromagnetic ratio, \(\mu_{ B }\) the Bohr magneton, and \(\hat{N}_{\sigma}=\sum_{s} \hat{n}_{s \sigma}\) the operator for the total number of particles of spin \(\sigma\). The eigenkets (2.30) are also eigenkets of this new Hamiltonian but with a different eigenvalue \[ \left(\hat{H}_{0}-g \mu_{ B } \hat{ S } \cdot B \right)|\Phi\rangle=\left(\sum_{k=1}^{N_{\uparrow}}\left(\epsilon_{k}-\frac{1}{2} g \mu_{ B } B\right)+\sum_{k^{\prime}=1}^{N_{\downarrow}}\left(\epsilon_{k^{\prime}}+\frac{1}{2} g \mu_{ B } B\right)\right)|\Phi\rangle \] Thus, in the presence of an external magnetic field the state (2.31) is no longer the lowest in energy since for, e.g., \(B>0,\) it becomes energetically convenient to have more electrons of spin up than of spin down. This is the typical behavior of a Pauli paramagnet, that is a system whose total spin is zero for \(B=0\) and grows parallel to \(B\) for \(B \neq 0 .\)

Next we discuss the case \(\hat{H}_{0}=0\). The Hamiltonian \(\hat{H}_{\text {int }}\) is already in a diagonal form and the generic eigenket can be written as \[ \left|\Phi_{X Y}\right\rangle=\prod_{s \in X} \prod_{s^{\prime} \in Y} \hat{d}_{s \uparrow}^{\dagger} \hat{d}_{s^{\prime} \downarrow}^{\dagger}|0\rangle \] where \(X\) and \(Y\) are two arbitrary collections of atomic sites. We can say that in \(\left|\Phi_{X Y}\right\rangle\) the electrons behave like "particles" since they have probability 0 or 1 of being on a given atomic site. The energy eigenvalue of \(\left|\Phi_{X Y}\right\rangle\) is \[ E_{X Y}=\sum_{s \in X \cap Y} U_{s} \] The ground state(s) for a given number \(N=N_{\uparrow}+N_{\downarrow}\) of electrons can be constructed by choosing \(X\) and \(Y\) that minimize the energy \(E_{X Y}\). Denoting by \(N_{ V }\) the total number of atomic sites, we have that for \(N \leq N_{ V }\) all states with \(X \cap Y=\emptyset\) are ground states (with zero energy) and, again, the system behaves like a Pauli paramagnet.

In conclusion, neither \(\hat{H}_{0}\) nor \(\hat{H}_{\text {int }}\) favors any kind of magnetic order. However, their sum \(\hat{H}_{0}+\hat{H}_{\text {int }}\) sometimes does. In Section 2.5 we illustrate an example of this phenomenon.

Particle-hole symmetry

In this section we solve explicitly the Hubbard model with only two sites, the so called Hubbard dimer, with Hamiltonian \[ \hat{H}=T \sum_{\sigma}\left(\hat{d}_{1 \sigma}^{\dagger} \hat{d}_{2 \sigma}+\hat{d}_{2 \sigma}^{\dagger} \hat{d}_{1 \sigma}\right)+U\left(\hat{n}_{1 \uparrow} \hat{n}_{1 \downarrow}+\hat{n}_{2 \uparrow} \hat{n}_{2 \downarrow}\right) \] This Hamiltonian belongs to a general class of Hubbard Hamiltonians defined on bipartite lattices, i.e., lattices that can be divided into two sets of sites \(A\) and \(B\) with hoppings only from sites of \(A\) to sites of \(B\) and vice versa, i.e. \[ \hat{H}_{ bip }=\sum_{\sigma} \sum_{s \in A \atop s^{\prime} \in B}\left(h_{s s^{\prime}} \hat{d}_{s \sigma}^{\dagger} \hat{d}_{s^{\prime} \sigma}+h_{s^{\prime} s} \hat{d}_{s^{\prime} \sigma}^{\dagger} \hat{d}_{s \sigma}\right)+\sum_{s} U_{s} \hat{n}_{s \uparrow} \hat{n}_{s \downarrow} \] In the Hubbard dimer \(A\) is site 1 and \(B\) is site 2 and \(h_{12}=T .\) If the lattice is bipartite and if \(h_{s s^{\prime}}=h_{s^{\prime} s}\) and \(U_{s}=U\) is independent of \(s\), the Hubbard Hamiltonian enjoys an interesting particle-hole symmetry which can be used to simplify the calculation of eigenvalues and eigenkets. Let us first explain what this symmetry is. The fermionic operators \[ \hat{b}_{ s \sigma} \equiv\left\{\begin{aligned} \hat{d}_{s \sigma}^{\dagger} & s \in A \\ -\hat{d}_{s \sigma}^{\dagger} & s \in B \end{aligned}\right. \] satisfy the same anti-commutation relations as the original \(\hat{d}\) operators, and in terms of them \(\hat{H}_{\text {bip }}\) becomes \[ \begin{aligned} \hat{H}_{\text {bip }} &=-\sum_{\sigma} \sum_{s \in A \atop s^{\prime} \in B}\left(h_{s s^{\prime}}, \hat{b}_{s \sigma} \hat{b}_{s^{\prime} \sigma}^{\dagger}+h_{s^{\prime} s} \hat{b}_{s^{\prime} \sigma} \hat{b}_{s \sigma}^{\dagger}\right)+U \sum_{s} \hat{b}_{s \uparrow} \hat{b}_{s \uparrow}^{\dagger} \hat{b}_{s \downarrow} \hat{b}_{s \downarrow}^{\dagger} \\ &=\sum_{\sigma} \sum_{s \in A \atop s^{\prime} \in B}\left(h_{s s^{\prime}} \hat{b}_{s \sigma}^{\dagger} \hat{b}_{s^{\prime} \sigma}+h_{s^{\prime} s} \hat{b}_{s^{\prime} \sigma}^{\dagger} \hat{b}_{s \sigma}\right)+U \sum_{s} \hat{n}_{s \uparrow}^{(b)} \hat{n}_{s \downarrow}^{(b)}-U \hat{N}^{(b)}+U N_{V} \end{aligned} \] where \(\hat{n}_{s \sigma}^{(b)}=\hat{b}_{s \sigma}^{\dagger} \hat{b}_{s \sigma}, \hat{N}^{(b)}=\sum_{s \sigma} \hat{n}_{s \sigma}^{(b)}\) and \(N_{ V }\) is the total number of sites. Except for the last two terms, which both commute with the first two terms, the Hamiltonian written with the \(\hat{b}\) -operators is identical to the Hamiltonian written with the \(\hat{d}\) -operators. This means that if \[ |\Psi\rangle=\sum_{s_{1} \ldots s_{N_{\uparrow}}} \sum_{s_{1}^{\prime} \ldots s_{N_{\downarrow}}^{\prime}} \Psi\left(s_{1} \uparrow, \ldots, s_{N_{\uparrow}} \uparrow, s_{1}^{\prime} \downarrow, \ldots, s_{N_{\downarrow}}^{\prime} \downarrow\right) \hat{d}_{s_{1} \uparrow}^{\dagger} \ldots \hat{d}_{s_{N_{\uparrow}} \uparrow}^{\dagger} \hat{d}_{s_{1}^{\prime} \downarrow}^{\dagger} \ldots \hat{d}_{s_{N_{\downarrow}}^{\prime}}^{\dagger}|0\rangle \] is an eigenstate of \(\hat{H}_{\text {bip }}\) with \(N_{\sigma}\) electrons of spin \(\sigma\) and energy \(E\), then the state \(\left|\Psi^{(b)}\right\rangle\) obtained from \(|\Psi\rangle\) by replacing the \(\hat{d}\) -operators with the \(\hat{b}\) -operators and the empty ket |0with the empty ket \(\left|0^{(b)}\right\rangle\) of the \(\hat{b}\) -operators is also an eigenstate of \(\hat{H}_{ bip }\) but with energy \(E-U\left(N_{\uparrow}+N_{\downarrow}\right)+U N_{ V }\). The empty ket \(\left|0^{(b)}\right\rangle\) is by definition the ket for which \(\hat{b}_{s \sigma}\left|0^{(b)}\right\rangle=0 \quad\) for all \(s\) and \(\sigma\)

Recalling the definition of the \(\hat{b}\) -operators, this is equivalent to saying that \(\hat{d}_{s \sigma}^{\dagger}\left|0^{(b)}\right\rangle=0\) for all \(s\) and \(\sigma\), i.e., the ket \(\left|0^{(b)}\right\rangle\) is the full ket with one electron of spin up and down on every site. Thus the state \(\left|\Psi^{(b)}\right\rangle\) can alternatively be written as a linear combination of products of annihilation \(\hat{d}\) -operators acting on the full ket. The number of electrons of spin \(\sigma\) in \(\left|\Psi^{(b)}\right\rangle\) is therefore \(N_{ V }-N_{\sigma} .\) In conclusion we can say that if \(E\) is an eigenvalue of \(\hat{H}_{ bip }\) with \(N_{\sigma}\) electrons of spin \(\sigma\) then \(E-U\left(N_{\uparrow}+N_{\downarrow}\right)+U N_{ V }\) is also an eigenvalue of \(\hat{H}_{\text {bip }}\) but with \(N_{ V }-N_{\sigma}\) electrons of spin \(\sigma\). The relation between the corresponding eigenkets is that the empty ket is replaced by the full ket and the creation operators are replaced by the annihilation operators. Let us now return to the Hubbard dimer. The Fock space \[ F = H _{0} \oplus H _{1} \oplus H _{2} \oplus H _{3} \oplus H _{4} \] is in this case finite because we cannot have more than four electrons. The zero-particle Hilbert space \(H _{0}\) contains only the empty ket which is an eigenket of \(\hat{H}\) with eigenvalue 0 . In \(H _{1}\) we have four possible kets \(|i \sigma\rangle=\hat{d}_{i \sigma}^{\dagger}|0\rangle\) with \(i=1,2\). They are all eigenkets of the interaction operator with eigenvalue \(0 .\) Furthermore the matrix \(\left\langle i \sigma|\hat{H}| j \sigma^{\prime}\right\rangle=\delta_{\sigma \sigma^{\prime}} h_{i j}\) is spin diagonal. The eigenvalues of \(h=\left(\begin{array}{cc}0 & T \\ T & 0\end{array}\right)\) are \(\pm T\) and the corresponding eigenvectors are \(\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(1,\pm 1)\). Thus, the one-particle eigenkets of the Hubbard dimer are \(\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\left(\hat{d}_{1 \sigma}^{\dagger} \pm \hat{d}_{2 \sigma}^{\dagger}\right)|0\rangle\) see also Table \(2.1 .\) Let us now consider the two-particle Hilbert space \(H _{2} .\) If both electrons have spin \(\sigma\) then the only possible ket is \(\hat{d}_{1 \sigma}^{\dagger} \hat{d}_{2 \sigma}^{\dagger}|0\rangle\) due to the Pauli exclusion principle. The reader can easily verify that this ket is an eigenket of \(\hat{H}\) with eigenvalue \(0 .\) In particular it is also an eigenket of the interaction Hamiltonian with eigenvalue 0 since the electrons have parallel spin. On the other hand, if the electrons have opposite spin then we have four possible kets: \[ \left|\Psi_{1}\right\rangle=\hat{d}_{1 \uparrow}^{\dagger} \hat{d}_{1 \downarrow}^{\dagger}|0\rangle, \quad\left|\Psi_{2}\right\rangle=\hat{d}_{2 \uparrow}^{\dagger} \hat{d}_{2 \downarrow}^{\dagger}|0\rangle, \quad\left|\Psi_{3}\right\rangle=\hat{d}_{1 \uparrow}^{\dagger} \hat{d}_{2 \downarrow}^{\dagger}|0\rangle, \quad\left|\Psi_{4}\right\rangle=\hat{d}_{1 \downarrow}^{\dagger} \hat{d}_{2 \uparrow}^{\dagger}|0\rangle \]

The Hamiltonian does not couple these states to those with parallel spin. Therefore, we can calculate the remaining eigenvalues and eigenkets in \(H _{2}\) by diagonalizing the matrix \(h_{2}\) with elements \(\left(h_{2}\right)_{i j}=\left\langle\Psi_{i}|\hat{H}| \Psi_{j}\right\rangle .\) After some simple algebra one finds \[ h_{2}=\left(\begin{array}{cccc} U & 0 & T & -T \\ 0 & U & T & -T \\ T & T & 0 & 0 \\ -T & -T & 0 & 0 \end{array}\right) \] This matrix has eigenvalues \(E_{1}=0, E_{2}=U, E_{3}=\frac{1}{2}(U-\Delta),\) and \(E_{4}=\frac{1}{2}(U+\Delta)\) where \(\Delta=\sqrt{16 T^{2}+U^{2}}\). Let us take, e.g., \(U>0\) so that \(E_{3}<0\) and \(E_{4}>0 .\) The normalized eigenkets corresponding to these eigenvalues are reported in Table \(2.1 .\) The occurrence of the zero eigenvalue \(E_{1}\) should not come as a surprise. The Hamiltonian commutes with the total spin operator \(\hat{S}^{2}=\left(\hat{ S }_{1}+\hat{ S }_{2}\right) \cdot\left(\hat{ S }_{1}+\hat{ S }_{2}\right),\) as well as with \(\hat{S}^{z}=\hat{S}_{1}^{z}+\hat{S}_{2}^{z}\) (the spin operators were defined in (1.92)\()\). Therefore the degenerate eigenkets of \(\hat{H}\) must belong to spin multiplets. It is easy to see that the three eigenkets with vanishing eigenvalue belong to a triplet whereas the eigenkets with eigenvalues \(E_{2}, E_{3}\) and \(E_{4}\) are singlets. To conclude our analysis we must calculate the eigenkets and eigenvalues with three and four particles. This can be done using the particle-hole symmetry and the results are reported in Table \(2.1 .\) It is interesting to observe that when \(T \rightarrow 0\) the eigenvalues in \(H _{2}\) become 0 (4 times degenerate) and \(U\) (two times degenerate). This is the dissociation limit which corresponds to pulling the two atomic sites infinitely far apart. The results agree with our physical intuition; if two isolated (Hubbard) atoms have one electron each (either with spin up or down) then the energy is zero whereas if both electrons are on the same atom then the energy is \(U\).
