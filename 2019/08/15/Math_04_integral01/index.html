1 fundamental

the Gaussian integral \[ \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-x^{2}} d x=\sqrt{\pi} \] Proof: \[ \begin{aligned} \left(\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-x^{2}} d x\right)^{2}=&\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-x^{2}} d x \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-y^{2}} d y \\=&\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-\left(x^{2}+y^{2}\right)} d x d y \end{aligned} \] By polar coordinates \(e^{-\left(x^{2}+y^{2}\right)}=e^{-r^{2}}\) \[ \begin{aligned} \iint_{\mathbf{R}^{2}} e^{-\left(x^{2}+y^{2}\right)} d(x, y) &=\int_{0}^{2 \pi} \int_{0}^{\infty} e^{-r^{2}} r d r d \theta \\ &=2 \pi \int_{0}^{\infty} r e^{-r^{2}} d r \\ &=2 \pi \int_{-\infty}^{0} \frac{1}{2} e^{s} d s \\ &=\pi \int_{-\infty}^{0} e^{s} d s \\ &=\pi\left(e^{0}-e^{-\infty}\right) \\ &=\pi \end{aligned} \] thus \[ \left(\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-x^{2}} d x\right)^{2}=\pi \quad \rightarrow \quad \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-x^{2}} d x=\sqrt{\pi} \]

scaled

\[ \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{ a x^{2}} d x=\sqrt{\frac{ \pi}{a}} \]

proof: \[ \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{a x^{2}} d x=\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{(\sqrt{a} x)^{2}} d x \] scale \(\sqrt{a} x \rightarrow y\), then \(d x=\frac{1}{\sqrt{a}}d y\) \[ \begin{aligned} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{(\sqrt{a x})^{2}} d x&=\frac{1}{\sqrt{a}} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{a y^{2}} d y\\&=\frac{1}{\sqrt{a}}\sqrt{\pi}\\&=\sqrt{\frac{\pi}{a}} \end{aligned} \]

shift

\[ \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-a(x+b)^{2}} d x=\sqrt{\frac{\pi}{a}} \]

proof:

replace \(x+b=y\), then \(d x= d y\) \[ \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-a(x+b)^{2}} d x=\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{a y^{2}} d y= \sqrt{\frac{\pi}{a}} \]

Generalizations

\[ \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-a x^{2}+b x+c} d x=\sqrt{\frac{\pi}{a}} e^{\frac{b^{2}}{4 a}+c} \]

proof: \[ \begin{aligned} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-a x^{2}+b x+c} d x&=\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-a(x+\frac{b^{}}{ a})^{2}+\frac{b^{2}}{4 a}+c} d x\\ &=e^{\frac{b^{2}}{4 a}+c} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-a(x+\frac{b^{}}{ a})^{2}} d x\\ &=\sqrt{\frac{\pi}{a}} e^{\frac{b^{2}}{4 a}+c} \end{aligned} \] special case：

2 multiple dimensions

Gaussian integrals in higher dimensions \[ \int \exp \left(-\frac{1}{2} x \cdot A \cdot x+J \cdot x\right) d^{n} x=\sqrt{\frac{(2 \pi)^{n}}{\operatorname{det} A}} \exp \left(\frac{1}{2} J \cdot A^{-1} \cdot J\right) \] This integral is performed by [diagonalization] of symmetric matrix A with an [orthogonal transformation] \(\boldsymbol{x}=\boldsymbol{S} \boldsymbol{y}\)

proof； \[ \begin{aligned} \boldsymbol{x} &=\boldsymbol{S} \boldsymbol{y} \\ d \boldsymbol{x} &=\boldsymbol{S} d \boldsymbol{y} \\ d x_{1} d x_{2} \ldots &=|\boldsymbol{S}| d y_{1} d y_{2} \ldots \end{aligned} \] where a determinant of unity \(|\boldsymbol{S}|=1\) . The integral \[ \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \ldots \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \exp \left(-\frac{1}{2} \boldsymbol{x}^{T} \boldsymbol{A} \boldsymbol{x}\right) d x_{1} d x_{2} \ldots d x_{n} = \int \exp \left(-\frac{1}{2} \boldsymbol{y}^{T} \boldsymbol{S}^{-1} \boldsymbol{A} \boldsymbol{S} \boldsymbol{y}\right) d y_{1} d y_{2} \ldots d y_{n} \] we demand that the matrix \(\boldsymbol{S}\) be a diagonalizing matrix, \[ \boldsymbol{S}^{-1} \boldsymbol{A} \boldsymbol{S}=\left[\begin{array}{cccc}{d_{1}} & {0} & {0} & {\cdots} \\ {0} & {d_{2}} & {0} & {\cdots} \\ {0} & {0} & {d_{3}} & {\cdots} \\ {\vdots} & {\vdots} & {\vdots} & {\ddots}\end{array}\right] \] changes the integral to \[ \begin{aligned} \int \exp \left(-\frac{1}{2} \boldsymbol{y}^{T} \boldsymbol{S}^{-1} \boldsymbol{A} \boldsymbol{S} \boldsymbol{y}\right) d y_{1} d y_{2} \ldots d y_{n}&=\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \exp \left[-\frac{1}{2}\left(d_{1} y_{1}^{2}+d_{2} y_{2}^{2}+\ldots d_{n} y_{n}^{2}\right)\right] d y_{1} d y_{2} \ldots d y_{n}\\ &=\sqrt{\frac{2 \pi}{d_{1}}} \cdot \sqrt{\frac{2 \pi}{d_{2}}} \cdots \sqrt{\frac{2 \pi}{d_{n}}}=\frac{(2 \pi)^{n / 2}}{|\boldsymbol{A}|^{1 / 2}} \end{aligned} \] where \(\prod_{n=1}^{2} \lambda_{n}=|\boldsymbol{A}|=\operatorname{det}(A)\)

with linear term

\[ \begin{aligned} -\frac{1}{2} \boldsymbol{x}^{T} \boldsymbol{A} \boldsymbol{x}+\boldsymbol{J}^{T} \boldsymbol{x} &\rightarrow-\frac{1}{2} \boldsymbol{y}^{T} \boldsymbol{D} \boldsymbol{y}+\boldsymbol{J}^{T} \boldsymbol{S} \boldsymbol{y}\\ &=-\frac{1}{2}\left(d_{1} y_{1}^{2}+d_{2} y_{2}^{2}+\ldots d_{n} y_{n}^{2}\right)+J_{a} S_{a 1} y_{1}+J_{a} S_{a 2} y_{2}+\ldots J_{a} S_{a n} y_{n}\\ &=\sum_i -\frac{1}{2} d_{i}\left(y_{i}-\frac{J_{a} S_{a i}}{d_{i}}\right)^{2}+\frac{\left(J_{a} S_{a i}\right)^{2}}{2 d_{i}} \end{aligned} \]

where \(-\frac{1}{2} d_{1} y_{1}^{2}+J_{a} S_{a 1} y_{1}=-\frac{1}{2} d_{1}\left(y_{1}-\frac{J_{a} S_{a 1}}{d_{1}}\right)^{2}+\frac{\left(J_{a} S_{a 1}\right)^{2}}{2 d_{1}}\)

making the substitution \(y_{i} \rightarrow y_{i}-J_{a} S_{a i} / d_{i}\) \[ \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \exp \left(-\frac{1}{2} \boldsymbol{x}^{T} \boldsymbol{A} \boldsymbol{x}+\boldsymbol{J}^{T} \boldsymbol{x}\right) d^{n} x=\frac{(2 \pi)^{n / 2}}{|\boldsymbol{A}|^{1 / 2}} \exp \left[\frac{\left(J_{a} S_{a i}\right)^{2}}{2 d_{i}}\right] \] the exponential term can be put into a more recognizable form \[ \frac{\left(J_{a} S_{a i}\right)^{2}}{2 d_{i}}=\frac{J_{a} S_{a i}\left(J_{b} S_{b i}\right)^{T}}{2 d_{i}}=\frac{J_{a} S_{a i} S_{i b}^{-1} J_{b}}{2 d_{i}} \] where \(S^{T}=S^{-1}\) give \(S_{a i} S_{i b}^{-1}=\delta_{a b}\) \[ \frac{J_{a} S_{a i} S_{i b}^{-1} J_{b}}{2 d_{i}}=\frac{1}{2} J_{a} S_{a i} \cdot \frac{1}{d_{i}} \cdot S_{i b}^{-1} J_{b} \] t the diagonal matrix defined \(\boldsymbol{S}^{-1} \boldsymbol{A} \boldsymbol{S}=\boldsymbol{D}\) allows a expression \(S^{-1} A^{-1} S=D^{-1}\) in n matrix summation notation \[ S_{i k}^{-1} A_{k b}^{-1} S_{b i}=D_{i i}^{-1}=\frac{1}{d_{i}} \] Plugging this \(\frac{1}{d_{i}}=S_{i k}^{-1} A_{k b}^{-1} S_{b i}\) into equation \[ \begin{aligned} \frac{1}{2} J_{a} S_{a i} \cdot \frac{1}{d_{i}} \cdot S_{i b}^{-1} J_{b} &=\frac{1}{2} J_{a} S_{a i} S_{i k}^{-1} A_{k b}^{-1} S_{b i} S_{i c}^{-1} J_{c} \\ &=\frac{1}{2} J_{k} A_{k c}^{-1} J_{c} \\ &=\frac{1}{2} J^{T} A^{-1} J \end{aligned} \] The Gaussian integral \[ \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \exp \left(-\frac{1}{2} \boldsymbol{x}^{T} \boldsymbol{A} \boldsymbol{x}+\boldsymbol{J}^{T} \boldsymbol{x}\right) d x_{1} d x_{2} \ldots d x_{n}=\frac{(2 \pi)^{n / 2}}{|\boldsymbol{A}|^{1 / 2}} \exp \left[\frac{1}{2} \boldsymbol{J}^{T} \boldsymbol{A}^{-1} \boldsymbol{J}\right] \]

Application

  1. In quantum field theory, the matrix \(\boldsymbol{A}^{-1}\) is related to what is known as a propagator, and it’s akin to the term in the probability amplitude

\[ \partial^{2}+m^{2} \]

It is essentially a Green’s function that describes the physical propagation of a particle created at the source \(\boldsymbol{J}\)and annihilated at the “antisource” or sink \(\boldsymbol{J}^{T}\). The matrix identity \(\boldsymbol{A}^{-1} \boldsymbol{A}=\boldsymbol{I}\) gets transcribed into \[ \left(\partial^{2}+m^{2}\right) D(\boldsymbol{x}-\boldsymbol{y})=\delta^{4}(\boldsymbol{x}-\boldsymbol{y}) \] or the path integral, where the quantity \(D(\boldsymbol{x}-\boldsymbol{y})\) is the propagator.

  1. In quantum field theory n-dimensional integrals of the form \[ \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \exp \left(-\frac{1}{\hbar} f(q)\right) d^{n} q \]

For small values of Planck's constant, f can be expanded about its minimum \[ \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \exp \left[-\frac{1}{\hbar}\left(f\left(q_{0}\right)+\frac{1}{2}\left(q-q_{0}\right)^{2} f^{\prime \prime}\left(q-q_{0}\right)+\cdots\right)\right] d^{n} q \] neglect higher order terms this integral can be integrated explicitly. \[ \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \exp \left[-\frac{1}{\hbar}(f(q))\right] d^{n} q \approx \exp \left[-\frac{1}{\hbar}\left(f\left(q_{0}\right)\right)\right] \sqrt{\frac{(2 \pi \hbar)^{n}}{\operatorname{det} f^{\prime \prime}}} \]

Example in 2d

The Gaussian integral in two dimensions \[ \int \exp \left(-\frac{1}{2} A_{i j} x^{i} x^{j}\right) d^{2} x=\sqrt{\frac{(2 \pi)^{2}}{\operatorname{det} A}} \] where A is a two-dimensional symmetric matrix with components specified as \[ A=\left[\begin{array}{ll}{a} & {c} \\ {c} & {b}\end{array}\right] \] Diagonalize the matrix \[ A_{i j} x^{i} x^{j} \equiv x^{T} A x=x^{T}\left(O O^{T}\right) A\left(O O^{T}\right) x=\left(x^{T} O\right)\left(O^{T} A O\right)\left(O^{T} x\right) \] where, since A is a real symmetric matrix, we can choose O to be orthogonal, and hence also a unitary matrix. O can be obtained from the eigenvectors of A. We choose O such that: \[ D=O^{T} A O=\left[\begin{array}{cc}{\lambda_{-}} & {0} \\ {0} & {\lambda_{+}}\end{array}\right] \] is diagonal.

Eigenvalues \[ \left[\begin{array}{ll}{a} & {c} \\ {c} & {b}\end{array}\right]\left[\begin{array}{l}{u} \\ {v}\end{array}\right]=\lambda\left[\begin{array}{l}{u} \\ {v}\end{array}\right] \] The eigenvalues are solutions of the characteristic polynomial \[ \begin{array}{l}{(a-\lambda)(b-\lambda)-c^{2}=0} \\ {\lambda^{2}-\lambda(a+b)+a b-c^{2}=0}\end{array} \] The eigenvalues are \[ \begin{aligned} \lambda_{ \pm} &=\frac{1}{2}(a+b) \pm \frac{1}{2} \sqrt{(a+b)^{2}-4\left(a b-c^{2}\right)} \\ \lambda_{ \pm} &=\frac{1}{2}(a+b) \pm \frac{1}{2} \sqrt{a^{2}+2 a b+b^{2}-4 a b+4 c^{2}} \\ \lambda_{ \pm} &=\frac{1}{2}(a+b) \pm \frac{1}{2} \sqrt{(a-b)^{2}+4 c^{2}} \end{aligned} \] Eigenvectors

Substitution of the eigenvalues back into the eigenvector equation \[ v=-\frac{\left(a-\lambda_{ \pm}\right) u}{c}, \quad v=-\frac{c u}{\left(b-\lambda_{ \pm}\right)} \]

\[ \frac{a-\lambda_{ \pm}}{c}=\frac{c}{b-\lambda_{ \pm}} \]

The eigenvectors can be written as: \[ \left[\begin{array}{c}{\frac{1}{\eta}} \\ {-\frac{a-\lambda_{-}}{c \eta}}\end{array}\right], \quad\left[\begin{array}{c}{-\frac{b-\lambda_{+}}{c \eta}} \\ {\frac{1}{\eta}}\end{array}\right] \] where normalizing factor \[ \eta=\sqrt{1+\left(\frac{a-\lambda_{-}}{c}\right)^{2}}=\sqrt{1+\left(\frac{b-\lambda_{+}}{c}\right)^{2}} \] two eigenvectors are orthogonal to each other \[ \left(\frac{1}{\eta}\quad -\frac{a-\lambda_{-}}{c \eta} \right) \left(\begin{array}{c}{-\frac{b-\lambda_{+}}{c \eta}} \\ {\frac{1}{\eta}}\end{array}\right)=\frac{a-\lambda_{-}}{c \eta^2}+\frac{b-\lambda_{+}}{c \eta^2}=1 \] orthogonal matrix

The orthogonal matrix is constructed by assigning the normalized eigenvectors as columns in the orthogonal matrix \[ O=\left[\begin{array}{cc}{\frac{1}{\eta}} & {-\frac{b-\lambda_{+}}{c \eta}} \\ {-\frac{a-\lambda_{-}}{c \eta}} & {\frac{1}{\eta}}\end{array}\right] \] note \(\operatorname{det}(O)=1\) and \(O^{-1}=O^{T}\)

3 moment average

\[ I_{n}(a) \equiv \int_{0}^{\infty} e^{-a x^{2}} x^{n} d x \]

setting \[ \begin{aligned} x & \equiv a^{-1 / 2} y \\ d x &=a^{-1 / 2} d y \\ y^{2} &=a x^{2} \end{aligned} \] then \[ \begin{aligned} I_{n}(a) &=a^{-1 / 2} \int_{0}^{\infty} e^{-y^{2}}\left(a^{-1 / 2} y\right)^{n} d y \\ &=a^{-(n+1) / 2} \int_{0}^{\infty} e^{-y^{2}} y^{n} d y \end{aligned} \] for \(n=0\) this is the usual Gaussian integral, \[ I_{0}(a)=\frac{\sqrt{\pi}}{2} a^{-1 / 2}=\frac{1}{2} \sqrt{\frac{\pi}{a}} \] For \(n=1\), the integrand is integrable by quadrature, \[ I_{1}(a)=a^{-1} \int_{0}^{\infty} e^{-y^{2}} y d y=a^{-1}\left[-\frac{1}{2} e^{-y^{2}}\right]_{0}^{\infty}=\frac{1}{2} a^{-1} \] for \(n>1\) use the identity \[ \begin{aligned}-\frac{\partial}{\partial a} I_{n-2}(a) &=-\frac{\partial}{\partial a} \int_{0}^{\infty} e^{-a x^{2}} x^{n-2} d x \\ &=-\int_{0}^{\infty}-x^{2} e^{-a x^{2}} x^{n-2} d x \\ &=\int_{0}^{\infty} e^{-a x^{2}} x^{n} d x \\ &=I_{n}(a) \end{aligned} \]

even

\(n=2 s\) \[ \begin{aligned} I_{n}(a) &=\left(-\frac{\partial}{\partial a}\right) I_{n-2}(a) \\ &=\left(-\frac{\partial}{\partial a}\right)^{2} I_{n-4} \\ &=\ldots=\left(-\frac{\partial}{\partial a}\right)^{n / 2} I_{0}(a) \\ &=\frac{\partial^{n / 2}}{\partial a^{n / 2}} I_{0}(a) \\ &=\frac{\sqrt{\pi}}{2} \frac{\partial^{n / 2}}{\partial a^{n / 2}} a^{-1 / 2} \end{aligned} \] then \[ \begin{aligned} \int_{0}^{\infty} x^{2 s} e^{-a x^{2}} d x &=\frac{\left(s-\frac{1}{2}\right) !}{2 a^{s+1 / 2}} \\ &=\frac{(2 s-1) ! !}{2^{s+1} a^{s}} \sqrt{\frac{\pi}{a}} \end{aligned} \]

odd

\(n=2 s+1\) \[ \begin{aligned} I_{n}(a) &=\left(-\frac{\partial}{\partial a}\right) I_{n-2}(a) \\ &=\left(-\frac{\partial}{\partial a}\right)^{2} I_{n-4}(a) \\ &=\ldots=\left(-\frac{\partial}{\partial a}\right)^{(n-1) / 2} I_{1}(a) \\ &=\frac{\partial^{(n-1) / 2}}{\partial a^{(n-1) / 2}} I_{1}(a) \\ &=\frac{1}{2} \frac{\partial^{(n-1) / 2}}{\partial a^{(n-1) / 2}} a^{-1} \end{aligned} \] then \[ \int_{0}^{\infty} x^{2 s+1} e^{-a x^{2}} d x=\frac{s !}{2 a^{s+1}} \] the general solution \[ \int_{0}^{\infty} e^{-a x^{2}} x^{n} d x=\left\{\begin{array}{ll}{\frac{(n-1) ! !}{2^{n / 2+1} a^{n / 2}} \sqrt{\frac{\pi}{a}}} & {\text { for } n \text { even }} \\ {\frac{\left[\frac{1}{2}(n-1)\right] !}{2 a^{(n+1) / 2}}} & {\text { for } n \text { odd }}\end{array}\right. \] The first few values are \[ \begin{array}{l}{I_{0}(a)=\frac{1}{2} \sqrt{\frac{\pi}{a}}} \\ {I_{1}(a)=\frac{1}{2 a}} \\ {I_{2}(a)=\frac{1}{4 a} \sqrt{\frac{\pi}{a}}} \\ {I_{3}(a)=\frac{1}{2 a^{2}}} \\ {L_{4}(a)=\frac{3}{8 a^{2}} \sqrt{\frac{\pi}{a}}} \\ {I_{5}(a)=\frac{1}{a^{3}}} \\ {I_{6}(a)=\frac{15}{16 a^{3}} \sqrt{\frac{\pi}{a}}}\end{array} \]

Related Integral

\[ H_{n}(a) \equiv \frac{1}{\sqrt{\pi}} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-a x^{2}} x^{n} d x=\left\{\begin{array}{ll}{\frac{2 I_{n}(a)}{\sqrt{\pi}}} & {\text { for } n \text { even }} \\ {0} & {\text { for } n \text { odd }}\end{array}\right. \]

more general \[ \begin{aligned} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} x^{n} e^{-a x^{2}+b x} d x &=i^{-n} a^{-(n+1) / 2} \sqrt{\pi} e^{b^{2} /(4 a)} U\left(-\frac{1}{2} n ; \frac{1}{2} ;-b^{2} / 4 a\right) \\ &=\sqrt{\frac{\pi}{a}} e^{b^{2} /(4 a)} \sum_{k=0}^{ | n / 2\rfloor} \frac{n !}{k !(n-2 k) !} \frac{(2 b)^{n-2 k}}{(4 a)^{n-k}} \\ &=\sqrt{\frac{\pi}{a}} e^{b^{2} /(4 a)} \sum_{k=0}^{ | n / 2\rfloor}\left(\begin{array}{c}{n} \\ {2 k}\end{array}\right)(2 k-1) ! !(2 a)^{k-n} b^{n-2 k} \end{aligned} \] where \(U(a ; b ; x)\) is a confluent hypergeometric function of the second kind
